## What These Foundations Covered

### Visual Arts Foundation

The ART 150 through ART 280 sequence covers the standard studio-foundations curriculum: drawing from observation, two-dimensional design and color theory, painting fundamentals across media, three-dimensional design through sculpture, and a survey of ancient and medieval art history. The ART-LD elective block (nine credits in additional lower-division studio work) rounds out the visual-arts foundation with further studio practice across disciplines.

This is the cluster of credits with the most direct continuity into Joshua's later work. The portfolio's [Gallery](/art/) is in many ways the evidentiary record of this coursework and the years of practice it anchored. Drawing fundamentals show up in the [illustration](/art/illustration/) archive, design fundamentals carry forward into the [branding](/art/branding/) work and the visual systems threaded through every product he has shipped since, and the painting and sculpture training surfaces in the [fine-art](/art/fine-art/) pieces. Even where the original course assignments are lost, the skills they built are visible across more than two decades of finished work.

### Natural Sciences

BISC 104 (Fundamentals of Biology) provides a general-education survey of cellular biology, genetics, ecology, and evolution. PHYS 101 and its companion lab PHYS 1011 cover introductory astronomy: the solar system, stellar life cycles, galactic structure, cosmology, with the lab adding hands-on observation and measurement work.

These courses give the systems-thinking baseline that resurfaces in Joshua's later sustainability-related work, particularly the framing in [ADMG 285](../admg/) and in the research-argument frame of [ENG 104](../eng/eng-104-academic-writing-2-reference). The astronomy work also pairs naturally with the long-running speculative-worldbuilding interest that runs through his game-design and tabletop projects.

### Humanities and Communication

COM 207 (Intro to Communication) covers interpersonal, small-group, and public communication theory along with practical speaking and listening skills. ENG 101 (English Composition) is the foundational essay course, building the thesis-and-evidence patterns that ENG 104 later turns into research-based academic argument. HIST 143 surveys US history from colonization through the Civil War, including political, social, and economic threads of the early republic. PHIL 201 (Intro to Logic) covers formal and informal reasoning: arguments, validity, fallacies, propositional and predicate logic.

These courses are the writing-and-reasoning bedrock under every later academic and product-management deliverable. The composition foundation from ENG 101 is what ENG 104 builds on. The formal-logic training from PHIL 201 shows up in the structured way Joshua writes specifications, project briefs, and the multi-tier plans threaded through the scholar archive and the studio's pillar documents.

### Social Sciences

POSC 101 (Intro to Politics) covers the basics of political science: institutions, ideologies, comparative systems, and the mechanics of democratic governance. PSY 101 (General Psychology) is the standard survey of cognition, personality, development, social psychology, and behavioral research methods.

This pair pairs naturally with the later customer-relationship and influence coursework (RMT 340, RMT 366) and with the character and personality work in Facsimile, the studio's character-modeling tool. The Big Five personality model that Facsimile uses, and the social-influence frames that show up in product copy and brand work, trace back to introductory exposure here.

### Practical Electives

FCSA-LD (Family and Consumer Sciences: Fashion Merchandising / Textile) and TRELEC-LD (a general lower-division transfer elective) round out the block. These are the kind of general-education and exploratory credits that lower-division undergraduates accumulate while figuring out a major. They do not map cleanly into a single later thread of work, but together they reflect the breadth of a pre-CWU education.

## How Transfer Credits Fit the Degree

CWU's degree-completion model expects most students in the BS in Information Technology and Administrative Management to arrive with a substantial lower-division transfer block already in place. The upper-division ADMG and IT coursework in the 300 and 400 numbers presumes that the writing, communication, math, science, and humanities foundations are already built. Transfer credits like these are not filler. They are the floor that the major sits on.

Joshua's specific arc into the degree is non-traditional. Military service first, then community college, then CWU. The variety of foundational coursework here reflects an exploratory pre-CWU education before he committed to the ITAM track. The visual-arts emphasis is the clearest signal of where his interests already lived; the breadth across sciences, humanities, social sciences, and electives is the signal of a student still mapping the territory. By the time the CWU upper-division work begins, the path tightens into product, systems, and management. The transfer block is the wider base that made that narrowing possible.
