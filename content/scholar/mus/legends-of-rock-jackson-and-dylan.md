## Introduction

Some stars are born of their time and accentuate the moment. They burn brightly and pass into memory, leaving their mark on culture. Some artists rise to such stardom that they become the marks of culture, maintaining relevance over decades and generations across genres. Michael Jackson and Bob Dylan are such artists, so keenly talented. To explore the staying power of these legends of rock, each artist's early successes are compared with later achievements that span their careers over decades. We may discover how these two preserved and shared their authentic artistic evolution over time while maintaining a sensibility toward cultural shifts, adapting new technologies, and responding to genre evolutions while meeting industry demands.

## Bob Dylan

Bob Dylan has always stood just askew of the mainstream push of the time. He began his career as a strong folk revivalist with his storytelling style. Over time, Dylan adapted to include and pioneer experimentation with electric guitars, causing some controversy for the folk scene of the time. Dylan's lyrics became more introspective, shifting to more rock, laying down the banner of social reform as he evolved. Further adapting, Dylan mixed his past experiences with new technologies to find himself a genre-bending (Rockwell) experimental rocker of the 1980s.

### "The Times They Are A-Changin'" (1964), *The Times They Are A-Changin'*

"The Times They Are A-Changin'" is a successful folk-rooted single of the sixties that is raw with stripped-down instrumentation, relying on acoustic guitar, harmonica, and vocalized lyrics that are socially conscious. Dylan speaks of the up-and-coming generation's different approach to the older generation. The various perspectives, it is warned, will be respected, as time will inevitably place the youth in control of the social constructs. Thus, the previous generation should get with the times and embrace change, lest they "sink like a stone." This early Bob Dylan track typifies his early acoustic folk style, which focused on storytelling and social change.

### "Jokerman" (1983), *Infidels*

A far departure from the acoustic folk of the 1960s, "Jokerman" utilizes much of the technical advancements of the time. The use of synthesizers, sophisticated production, and rock-oriented instrumentation with a gospel influence land this just-over-six-minute track within the bounds of no single genre. Dylan brought back his earlier social commentary from his early years to accompany the retrospection he garnered in the 1970s. "Jokerman" seems to be a personification of the twists and turns of the fate of mankind, relating a picture of the classic comedic-drama trope encapsulated in fate.

The later Dylan track exemplifies the artist's ability to retain authenticity while responding to technological changes in instrumentation options, cultural changes in lyrical topics, and industry demands to remain financially successful. Bob Dylan was able to stay relevant and thriving throughout the 60s, 70s, and into the 80s. From folk purist to electric rock, and finally to experimental rock blending folk and reggae (Rockwell), Dylan is a true rock legend who still stands the test of time.

## Michael Jackson

Michael Jackson started early as part of the Jackson Five, which originated with a Motown, Rhythm and Blues, soul sound. Even then, the band adapted to be more pop with funk and disco influences. As Jackson transitioned to a solo career, his work with producer Quincy Jones (Qwest TV) helped establish a funk, disco, rhythm and blues style, a marketable pop of the late 70s. With the advent of MTV and the visual aesthetic associated with bands, Jackson was able to transcend the competition with his unique vocals, adoption of electric sounds, personal branding, and remarkable knack for dance performance.

### "Rock with You" (1979), *Off the Wall*

A catchy, cleanly produced disco track that played well to the mainstream, hitting number one on multiple rhythm-and-blues and pop Billboard charts. The track, featuring Jackson's iconic vocals, is accompanied by a danceable drumbeat, bass, and electric guitar funk, featuring synth and horns, congas, shakers, and even strings.

The song's lyrics are a pop-centric love groove with unsurprising double entendres that accentuate the story of two lovers dancing to the disco boogie beat all night and beyond. The song builds on clean, highly produced elements that are strikingly and expertly melded together to paint a dreamy vision that captures the intimate, exclusionary atmosphere that focuses all the world on just you and your chosen partner.

Amazingly, this 1979 track demonstrates Jackson reinventing himself with the help of producer Quincy Jones. As a member of the Jackson 5, Michael had been a hit personality since 1972. However, this single exemplifies the artist's ability to harness technology, culture, and industry appetite even before his explosion as a superstar in the early 1980s with the advent of MTV and Jackson's determination to conquer rhythm and blues, rock, and pop.

### "Black or White" (1991), *Dangerous*

A catchy tune with several mix-ups, an intro skit, and changeups, this single from Jackson's 1991 album *Dangerous* brings together a myriad of production studio techniques and genre-bending and -blending styles. Jackson's unique vocals, including samples, synthesizers, electric drums, and cleanly produced distorted rock guitars, are featured on the roughly four-minute track. A rap bridge, guitar solo, and an outro coda break up the verse-chorus structure.

The song's start features a skit of a child listening to rock music while his father insists he turn it off because it's "too late." This beds the song in the disparate attitudes of generations. The older generation does not approve of music for social or political reasons, while the youth respond to music purely on its own sonic merits. The youth plays the forthcoming Jackson ode to racial equality in an act of rebellion.

Though Jackson maintained hits until his last album, sardonically named *Invincible*, in 2001 (Healy), by 1991 it is clear that Jackson had quickly adapted to technologies in the music studio and out, as evidenced in his video works that included collaboration with John Landis on the innovative music videos *Thriller* and *Black or White* (Cheema). That sensibility to cultural issues and understanding of mainstream industry demands set him firmly in history as the King of Pop.

## Conclusion

The explored works are but small samples in the incredible bodies of work by the legends of rock that are Michael Jackson and Bob Dylan. These artists' work has moved generations, undoubtedly influenced social and political agendas, and become cultural touchstones. Their ability to adapt an authentic artistic evolution, maintain a sensibility to the shifts of culture, leverage new technologies, evolve with musical genres, and meet the demands of industry make these two legends that will retain their rightful place in the history of rock.
