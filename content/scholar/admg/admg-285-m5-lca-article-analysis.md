## LCA Article Analysis

This document is intended to be reviewed by relevant stakeholders pertaining to sustainability impacts in conjunction with the circular end-of-use scenarios for smartphones. The information presented in this document is oriented to a life cycle assessment (LCA) practices approach that complies with the International Organization for Standards (ISO) 14040-44. The document will summarize, with special regard to ISO LCA Study Design Parameters, the peer-reviewed journal article *Modelling Of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios For Smartphones* (Pamminger et al., 2021). Specifically, the study's methods, LCA relevant summary and analysis, and recommendations are noted.

## Background and Purpose of the Study

Currently, the world is stricken with an issue of sustainable resources. Humans utilize more of Earth's global resources than can be regenerated, at about 1.75 Earths per person (Global Footprint Network, 2023). That means that, on average, each individual consumes 75% more resources than the Earth can regenerate in the same amount of time. This consumption rate indicates our Ecological footprint, or more specifically, according to Eco Online (What Is an Ecological Footprint?, n.d.), how many environmental resources are required to support a specific way of life or business.

The solution is to strive for what is called Sustainability. Sustainability is a broad term originating in a 1987 UN article that indicates that "humanity has the ability to make development sustainable to ensure that it meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs" (United Nations, 1987). Since mobile phones are so widely used worldwide, the draw on natural resources and resulting harmful e-waste has also become a growing issue (Paska, 2010) that cannot continue if we wish not to compromise future generations' ability to meet their needs.

Finally, the study (Pamminger et al., 2021) itself discusses what is called circular economy (CE) or circular end-of-use scenarios. This concept is an element of consideration in regard to the product life cycle concept of cradle-to-cradle and describes end-of-pipe treatment (Hendrickson et al., 2014). Also detailed by *Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter,* end-of-pipe treatment accounts for the resulting outputs of a product at the end of its product life cycle. The text also details that cradle-to-cradle refers to the end of a product life cycle as returning and transforming into another product. Cradle-to-grave is the scope of a life cycle that starts at production and ends when the use-case of the product ends (Hendrickson et al., 2014). The article (Pamminger et al., 2021) to be summarized discusses three options for a possible return to use for cellular devices or cradle-to-cradle product life cycle design plans. Specifically, it will account for which of the three options has a greater positive impact on sustainability.

## Methods

The study (Pamminger et al., 2021) performs LCA on smartphones to generate a baseline for what is considered the smartphone's first-life. This is an essential term because the study does not want to expand the scope of study to include the LCA of second-use scenarios but desires to comply with ISO recommendations of an attributional approach and to reduce system expansion. The second life assessment will depend on one of the three possible circular end-of-use or cradle-to-cradle scenarios. These three are repair, refurbish, or remanufacturing. This means that the study will issue first-life and second-life valuation attributes that will aggregate into a total economic valuation parameter in order to more effectively compare the three scenarios it plans to be testing.

## Life Cycle Assessment Summary and Analysis

The LCA of this article will utilize a unified metric that considers first-life and second-life parameters of a standardized cellular device with an average 2.5-year use time. The first-life is taken from other LCA studies and will be impacted by this study's focus on the second-life of the cellular device. By focusing on the second-life aspect the study will determine which of the three options, repair, refurbish, or remanufacture, is the best strategy in consideration of sustainability impacts.

### Goal

The goal of the article (Pamminger et al., 2021) is to assist in guiding decisions concerning CE strategies of smartphones after their first-life when multiple life cycles might be considered and which strategy might be the best option in consideration of sustainability. Essentially, the goal is to determine what might be the most sustainable options at the end of the life of cellular devices between repair, refurbishment, or the remanufacturing of cell phones.

### System Boundaries (Scope)

The system's boundaries include the three above-mentioned end-of-use scenarios only as second-life options. The devices being tested are not those that have been designed specifically for second-life product life cycles. Furthermore, the study (Pamminger et al., 2021) suggests the information is normalized into an aggregated valuation for comparison.

### Function and Functional Unit

The functional unit for this study is "one working smartphone for a use time of 2.5 years" (Pamminger et al., 2021). This unit is derived from annotated previous works. The second-life use time is an additional parameter that is scaled into the average of the functional unit. The study further defines the constraints of what a working smartphone is.

### Data Sources and Inventory

The study (Pamminger et al., 2021) indicates the life cycle inventory by detailing the reference smartphone model used. The article details comparative life cycle maps of the three different overlaid end-of-life strategy processes. Each process step is consistently considered but not necessarily applied (where appropriately unnecessary) throughout the three possible second-life scenarios. Each of the steps is detailed, with accountability metrics utilized to determine the input and output resource implications. These metrics are aggregated for scoring each of the end-of-life strategies.

Furthermore, to indicate the environmental impact, the study utilizes Global Warming Potential (GWP) as a metric assigned to each of the three strategies (Pamminger et al., 2021). Over the course of 2.5 years of use time considered, the results for each are 10kg CO2eq for repair impacts, 15.5kg CO2eq for refurbishing, and 25.8kg for remanufacturing.

### Life Cycle Impact Assessment

According to the study (Pamminger et al., 2021), the repairing and refurbishing options are far more effective than the remanufacturing options. Repair is the first and best choice for sustainability, with some variance in consideration of the part that is being repaired and the success of said repair. The metrics here, despite not having a guaranteed success rate of repair, are still better than other options. Also noted is that if the repair is unsuccessful, the device may still fold into one of the other competing options. Refurbishing is a closer second choice than the last. The contention with refurbishing is the extended reallocation phase, which may include various transportation costs. Incursions and resource consumption at refurbishing locations are also cited. In comparison, repairing has a near-null location and transportation impact, as repairs can often be done locally or at preexisting multipurpose locations (at home) (Pamminger et al., 2021). Remanufacturing is the least advised option as it has the most likely resource consumption in transportation and disassembles with the most negligible value reclamation.

## Recommendation

I would recommend consideration that aligns with the results of the *Modelling Of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios For Smartphones* journal article (Pamminger et al., 2021). The findings support a common sense approach to cellular device CE considerations. I would also consider other studies and options related to the reuse case of cellular devices. Notwithstanding the consideration of specific scenarios, generally, the best option is to delay replacement and extend the time the device is used whenever possible (Pamminger et al., 2021). Repairs should first be attempted, then refurbishing attempts, and finally, remanufacturing attempts.

I would consider the following strategies for cellular devices and any technology product my organization uses. The delay or removal of acquisition needs for other devices by finding a new use case for in-stock older corporate asset devices. For instance, Apple phones now have the ability to utilize cameras as webcams. At the very least, the organization should consider repurposing old cellular devices as web cameras, as the quality and accessibility of the devices are much greater than those of the average webcam. This saves money for the organization, reduces e-waste, and increases the quality and flexibility of equipment used by employees.

For a remote work environment, I would recommend sending repair kits to employees from iFixit to attempt self-repairs before any replacement action is ordered. This has the added benefit of educating employees, supporting learning and growth in different areas, and aligning company culture with sustainability actions, self-improvement, and self-reliance. This would be a cost-saving measure and reduce e-waste as well.

Purchasing refurbished devices would be investing in sustainability while saving a business money. Refurbished devices should not adversely affect employees' experience with technologies and tools utilized in daily work actions. I would argue employees might well celebrate an organization's dedication to sustainable actions.

Ensuring an organization recycles responsibly when devices cannot be fixed and replacing them with refurbished assets will ensure the best-case scenario of end-of-life remanufacturing options. This can be a part of an organization's commitment claim to sustainability, increasing brand image that will translate into client trust and greater corporate valuation.
