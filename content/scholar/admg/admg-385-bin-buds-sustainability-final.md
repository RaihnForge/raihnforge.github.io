*Team project delivered with two CWU classmates under the team name Sustainability! Original deliverable was a presentation deck; this is the speaker-track adaptation for the web view, preserving the argument and the visual reasoning. Mascot illustrations and visual identity work were my contribution.*

## CWU's Vision and Mission

Central Washington University publishes a sustainability commitment in its mission language, naming culturally sustaining practices and a commitment to fostering sustainability across campus operations. That commitment is the backdrop for the recommendation that follows. The university already wants to do this. The question this Final tries to answer is how to make the want actually translate into different behavior at the bin.

## The Problem: Decision Paralysis at the Bin

Students on campus would like to be sustainable. The friction is not motivation. The friction is the moment of the decision itself.

A student rushing between classes, with academic load on their mind, approaches a waste bin with an item in hand. Reading the labels becomes a small puzzle. Current signage tends to fail in one of two ways. Either it is too detailed (long lists of accepted and rejected items that require sustained reading) or it is too vague (a single icon that does not actually resolve the question). In the absence of clarity, the fastest behavior wins, and the fastest behavior is to put the item in whatever bin is closest, or default to "trash" for anything uncertain.

This is analysis paralysis at micro-scale. The Wikipedia framing is useful precisely because it is not jargon: "an individual or group process where overanalyzing or overthinking a situation can cause forward motion or decision-making to become paralyzed, meaning that no solution or course of action is decided upon within a natural time frame" (Wikipedia contributors, n.d.). At the bin, the natural time frame is seconds. If the decision does not resolve in that window, the student moves on and recycling loses.

## The Solution: Brand-Identity Mascots Backed by Gestalt

The recommendation is to treat the waste bin as a brand-identity surface. Two bodies of design literature support this.

**Gestalt principles** (Interaction Design Foundation, n.d.) describe how human perception groups visual information. Three of them carry the weight here:

- *Like shapes group with like shapes.* If trash bins share a silhouette and recycling bins share a different silhouette, the eye sorts them before the reading brain has to engage.
- *Like colors group with like colors.* Color contrast between bin categories does the same work in parallel, with the additional benefit of being legible at a distance.
- *Repetition builds expectation.* When the same shapes and colors recur across every building, stairwell, and dining area on campus, the sorting decision becomes pre-loaded rather than re-derived each time.

**Mascots for brand identity** (Kongdee et al., 2020) extend the visual work into a character. The research on educational-institution mascots names three benefits: mascots build familiarity faster than logos alone, they communicate traits and personality the institution wants to project, and they increase engagement with whatever campaign the mascot is attached to. Disney, the NFL, and almost every successful marketing campaign of the last forty years lean on this. There is no reason the CWU recycling initiative cannot.

The combined move is to assign each bin category a Bin Bud. The Bin Bud has a silhouette, a color, a face, and an appetite. Trash Bin "eats" styrofoam, food waste, shredded paper, and greasy items. Recycling Bin "eats" clean paper, glass, metal, plastic, and cardboard. Paper Recycling, where it is offered, can be a child character of the Recycling Bin family. The decision at the bin shifts from "where does this go" to "which Bud is hungry for this," which is a faster and more memorable cognitive operation.

## Spreading Awareness: Materials and Infographic

The mascots themselves only work if students are introduced to them before they hit the bin. The deliverable includes a campaign of supporting materials:

- Posters and fliers that reveal each character and what they like to eat
- Side-by-side reference of trash vs. recycling materials, anchored on the characters rather than the rules
- Signage declaring where the bins can be found (front of every building, near every stairwell, within reasonable distance of every classroom)

The CWU directives on what counts as recycling and trash on campus (Central Washington University, n.d.) become the source material the infographic translates into character language.

| Recycling (clean) | Trash |
| :--- | :--- |
| Paper (newspaper, cereal boxes, printer paper, mail, notebook paper) | Styrofoam |
| Glass (bottles and jars) | Plates and utensils |
| Metal (tin and aluminum cans) | Food waste |
| Plastic bottles (no caps) | Shredded paper |
| Heavy corrugated cardboard (designated bins) | Anything with greasy food residue (no pizza boxes) |

## Persona: Who This Is For

The primary persona is the new or current CWU student living on campus, especially in residence halls. This is the population that interacts with bins most frequently and most repeatedly, which means the per-decision savings compound. It is also the population most likely to absorb a brand-mascot campaign as part of their first-year campus experience, which is when sustainable defaults get set for the rest of the degree.

## Call to Action

Remove the confusion. Create a sustainable solution students actually use. The three-step roadmap:

1. **Design.** Finalize the Bin Bud character set (Trash, Recycling, optional Paper Recycling). Lock the silhouettes, colors, and personality.
2. **Develop.** Produce the informational materials, the signage system, and the rollout plan for bin wraps and posters.
3. **Implement.** Run the awareness campaign on a campus-wide schedule, anchored to the start of the academic year when habit formation is at its highest leverage.

## Conclusion

By mitigating analysis paralysis at the moment of the bin decision, through Gestalt-grounded design and brand-recognition mascots, CWU can raise sustainability awareness and improve sustainable practices without asking students to work harder. The asks are smaller and the campus does the cognitive carrying.
