This paper responds to Schwalbe Chapters 10 and 11. Chapter 10 covers communication. Chapter 11 covers risk. They are paired for a reason: most of what we call risk in projects is, underneath, a communication failure that nobody named in time.

## Chapter 10: Communication

### Tone of Voice

Oral communication carries information that words alone do not (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 394). Someone can say "I am fine with the timeline" and be telling you something different with their voice. Stress and tension show up in tone before they show up in vocabulary, often before the speaker has even registered the feeling themselves. A project manager who only reads the words is reading half the message.

### Body Language

Mehrabian's *Silent Messages* research, cited by Schwalbe, is that body language carries an enormous share of face-to-face communication (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 394). If you task someone and ask whether they have it, they may say yes while shifting in their chair and avoiding the camera. The yes is polite. The shoveling is the truth. Paying attention to this is not optional in project management. It is how you catch a problem while it is still small.

### Punctuation in Virtual Communication

Coolman (2014) asks readers to feel the difference between "Okay" and "Okay!" The first reads as hesitant or even passive. The second reads as energy. Depending on the reader's mood, the first can read as cold or annoyed. Punctuation and capitalization are tools in writing that have to match the intended tone, especially over Slack, Teams, and email, where every other context cue has been stripped out. I treat punctuation as part of the message.

### Same Words, Different Meaning

"That's fine" and "that's great" can read as affirmation or irritation depending on delivery. "What do you want?" can be a sincere question or a frustrated exclamation from someone who feels they have not been given a clear ask. In project work I avoid phrases that can flip meaning over text, and when I catch myself using one, I follow it immediately with the more specific version.

### Distribution Methods

| Method | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|---|---|---|
| Project team meeting | Immediate feedback, clarification | Risk of unclear ownership: did the team accept this, or is one person supposed to act on it? |
| One-on-one meeting | Direct clarification, course correction | If others are excluded, can read as a power imbalance or back-channel |
| Stakeholder meeting | High-bandwidth update tuned to stakeholder interest | Can drift into too much detail and frustrate the stakeholder if every micro-decision lands on them |
| Video conference | Decent context, reactive response | Easy time sink if not all attendees need to be in the room |
| Conference call | Quick to set up, allows context and feedback | Audience attention drops fast when information is not specifically relevant |
| Email | Async, lets recipients read on their own time | Hard to verify context or timely receipt, and threads decay into ambiguity |
| Collaborative work tool (Google Docs, etc.) | Real-time co-authoring, inline comments | Disorientation when text moves under you, and a tools-knowledge tax on the team |

### Collaborative Work Tools, In Detail

A shared doc removes the red-pen-and-email loop. A reviewer can highlight a passage, leave a comment, and the author can accept or push back inline. That is a real productivity gain. The downside is that everyone has to know the tool the same way, including the team's local conventions for how to use it. Software updates also change behavior. Maintaining shared knowledge of the tool is itself a recurring cost, and training is part of that cost (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 394).

### Video Conferences, In Detail

Video conferences recover some of the body language that audio-only calls lose. They are not as rich as the room, though. In a physical group meeting, attendees inherit each other's energy, and one yawn can pull the rest into fatigue. On video, you can miss the early cues because everyone is in their own square. Video is also too easy to schedule, which means it can become the default channel and interrupt the actual work, especially for developers in deep focus. Standups exist partly to keep contributions short, and that is harder to enforce when nobody has to stand (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 395).

### Applying This to Our Team

I already operate on these instincts, especially in online-only environments where webcams are not always on. When I catch myself using a phrase that could be read multiple ways, I follow it with a clarifying sentence in the same message. I would rather over-explain my intent than leave a teammate guessing.

## Chapter 11: Risk

### Golden Rule 8: Plan and Implement Risk Responses

Schwalbe and Jutte both push the same point: identifying risk is not enough. You have to update process and documentation when a risk actually shows up (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 433). Jutte (2021) goes a step further with three concrete response patterns:

- **Risk avoidance.** Reorganize the project so the risk no longer applies.
- **Risk minimization.** Use the analysis you already have to soften the impact.
- **Risk acceptance.** Consciously decide to take the hit and be ready to adapt.

Every project has risks. Having a planned response reduces friction, emotional churn, and cost, and it is often the difference between a project that survives and a project that quietly bleeds out.

### Golden Rule 2: Identify Risks Early

Both sources agree that risk identification is foundational and that documentation is part of the process (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 433). Jutte adds a useful split for where risks come from (Jutte, 2021):

- **People.** Interview your team and outside experts. They know risks you have not thought of.
- **Paper.** Review documentation from past projects, especially ones with similar shape. Old problems show you new exposure.

The golden rule matters because it pulls risk identification out of the project manager's head and into a group activity. The best risk register is not the one I wrote alone. It is the one the team and the prior project's docs wrote with me.

### Why Risk Management Plans Matter

The benefit of working through the topics in Schwalbe's Table 11-2 is that the table externalizes what would otherwise live as a feeling (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 433). Before I had this framing, I would notice that a project did not feel right and try to reason backward from the discomfort. The table lets me skip the emotional analysis and go straight to the variables: probability, impact, owner, response, trigger. Once the plan is in place, encountering a new risk becomes a lookup against well-documented data instead of a stress event. It also makes the response defensible to anyone who challenges the call, because the data is right there.
