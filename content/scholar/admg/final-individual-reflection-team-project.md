This is the end-of-quarter reflection for ADMG 374. The team delivered a nonprofit website project across three labs and a final, with four members working entirely over Microsoft Teams and Zoom during the COVID quarter. The team average came out at 99.7. I scored the team at 96, reserving room for improvement that any team can use.

## Team Performance

Lexi Cozart was excellent at the work itself, and she was open about a schedule that pulled her in many directions, including a drive to Wenatchee on the day of the final. Her risk management section in Lab 3 carried the depth of someone who had taken a dedicated risk class the prior quarter, and it raised the ceiling of the deliverable.

Ziyan Ma started quiet and camera-off. Over the quarter she turned the camera on, joined more actively, and produced consistently strong written work, especially as project manager during Lab 3. English is her second language, and the culture she comes from is less prone to interjecting. Her output was always thorough and on time, even when her presence in conversation was light.

Amberley Snyder was the team's organizing engine. She set up Zooms, tracked deadlines, and reached out to teammates when she sensed something was stuck. When Ziyan could not locate a template document, Amberley followed up by text the next day until it was found. Her strength is also her edge case: she would step in fast enough that, when I was project manager, I sometimes had to ask for a beat to think, because she read my pauses as confusion rather than consideration.

I gave myself a 99. I produced most of the graphics and web development, was lead on creative problem solving, owned stakeholder prediction, and was the team's default voice for ambiguous roles. I should have been more vocal earlier in the quarter about how much of the web work was uncounted in the Lab task split. Letting that go is a leftover Marine Corps instinct: do, and survive. In a school team, that instinct caused avoidable friction late in the project.

For the 100-point allocation across the team, I split it 28 Amberley, 28 myself, 22 Lexi, 22 Ziyan. The two even-weight slots reflect the two of us carrying the most cross-functional and organizational load. The 22s reflect strong, consistent contribution inside well-defined sections.

## My Performance Against the 7 Habits

Schwalbe's adaptation of the seven habits (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 356) maps cleanly onto what I drew from the Marine Corps. *Seek first to understand* mattered most on a team this diverse in age, culture, and sex. I volunteered concepts early so the team could react to a draft rather than a blank page. I picked up the unattractive tasks. I volunteered as speaker for the final presentation. *Think Win/Win* is native to me because client design work runs on tight budgets that demand high impact, like a one-color T-shirt design that leans into the constraint instead of apologizing for it. *Begin with the End in Mind* is a habit from a decade of project work outside school. The habit I did not exhibit well is *Keep the Saw Sharp*. I do not prioritize my own health and recovery during sustained pushes, and the project surfaced that gap.

## Suggestions for Improvement

We came together fast and delivered, but earlier and more frequent contact would have lowered the friction we hit in the back half. Per Schwalbe, "It also motivates workers to have some progress to report" (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 403). A short after-action debrief after each lab turn-in would have let us name what went right and wrong while it was fresh, and adjust before the next lab. Pairing those debriefs with a small reward ritual would have built human connection. Sciacovelli (2018) makes the case directly: "you may consider team-based reward structures within your reward strategy as one means of increasing team performance."

## Lessons Learned

**Be proactive about communicating when you are taking on more work.** I undercounted the web development effort in our task split, then carried the extra weight without raising it. That is a habit from environments where there is no concept of fair, only do or do not. In a school team, silence about extra load creates resentment that the team cannot fix because it cannot see it.

**Do not rely on a single delivery service for a final deliverable.** Microsoft Teams went down at CWU for several hours leading up to our turn-in. Canvas attachments later started serving the wrong PowerPoint to viewers. I pivoted the team to Dropbox, which I already pay for through freelance design, and reposted the final from there. Backups and a known fallback path are a project deliverable, not a personal preference.

## What Went Right and What Went Wrong

Lab 3 and the final are the example of the team at its best. More meetings, more shifts, more moving parts, all delivered ahead of our internal deadline. Trust held, communication held, and the deliverable was professional.

The CWU outage is the example of what went wrong, and the fix is in my hands going forward: keep redundant backups, and have a secondary delivery channel ready to go before it is needed.

## Project Management Concepts and Processes

The most useful new tools for me were the Work Breakdown Structure paired with Gantt charts, and the critical path. I have managed projects for close to a decade without formal names for these. Knowing them changes the conversation with stakeholders, because the cost of a feature creep request becomes visible on a chart rather than abstract in a meeting. Several past projects of mine could have been saved by being able to point at a critical path and say, calmly, this is what the change actually costs.

How this helps in the real world: it lets me decide which projects to invest my time in. I used to over-weight skill and under-weight communication and project literacy when choosing collaborators. Now I know I can hire or outsource skill. What I cannot outsource is alignment on the triple constraints. As Schwalbe puts it, "Many IT projects have talented people working on them, but it takes teamwork to complete most projects successfully" (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 369). Gantt charts and WBS breakdowns are also a stakeholder filter. Stakeholders who refuse to engage with what the chart is showing them are stakeholders I do not want to invest in.

Nishadha (2018) puts the same point well: "Where there is a visual framework for the work to be done, there are fewer chances for misunderstanding, especially when it comes to highly complex tasks. Using Gantt charts allows all types of stakeholders to have the same information, set mutually understood expectations, and conduct their efforts according to the desired protocol."

## Project Manager Self-Test

I scored a 72 on the Mind Tools quiz, which it labeled as "OK." The questions are blunt instruments. "I give people a deadline to complete their project work, and then I expect them to coordinate with others if and when they need to" assumes a trust state I do not extend to strangers. I respect people on site until they earn my disrespect, and I distrust strangers until they earn my trust. A twenty-question quiz cannot model that. I take the planning feedback seriously. I am skeptical of the framing.

What I do well, against the text: most of the seven habits, especially balancing the triple constraints. "To create a successful project, a project manager must consider scope, time, and cost and balance" (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 7). What I would do differently with this same team: more meetings, an after-action ritual, and a deliberate push to reach the *Performing* stage of the Tuckman model (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 370) faster. Where I want to keep growing: WBS, Gantt charts, and critical paths (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 239); formal risk management plans (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 432); a maintained stakeholder register (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 500); and well-drafted project charters (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 95). People bring me into new ideas constantly. I want to be the person who can either guide them through the process or know early that the project is not one I should join.

## Five Process Groups

The five process groups are Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring and Controlling, and Closing (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 81). Our team spent the most time in Closing. Last-minute changes to delivery method and scope details required retuning to stay aligned with stakeholders. This was the right use of our time given the digital-only environment, and it mirrors a real-world pattern where stakeholders lose some of the original luster and worry, near the end, that the deliverable is no longer innovative enough. That is why constraints need to be locked down early and monitored continuously. "Project scope management includes the processes involved in defining and controlling what work is or is not included in a project" (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 184).

## Scope Creep

Scope creep is "the tendency for project scope to keep getting bigger and bigger" (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 205). Our team faced it. The website had many plausible directions. We used scope management principles around controlling scope, especially managing changes (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 207). We deliberated each proposed change, removed ideas that did not earn their place, and reframed requests that mattered so they did not crowd out higher-priority scope. "Develop a process for soliciting and monitoring changes to project scope. Stakeholders should be encouraged to suggest changes that will benefit the overall project and discouraged from suggesting unnecessary changes" (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 208).

## Ten Knowledge Areas

Schwalbe's eighth edition uses ten knowledge areas, with stakeholder management added in (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 12). Project Planning was the spine throughout (Schwalbe, 2015, p. 98).

1. **Project integration management.** Developed the project and management plan. The Project Charter was a frequent reference for keeping inside the triple constraints, and it served as the baseline for communications and team behaviors.
2. **Scope management.** Collected requirements, defined scope, created the WBS, validated the scope statement, and used change logs to control creep. Scope changed, but the change process was respected.
3. **Time management.** WBS plus Gantt charts produced our activity and milestone lists. We drafted network diagrams, respected our critical path, and built in lead and lag time to absorb late changes.
4. **Cost management.** Cost management plan, activity cost estimates, updates, Excel for budgeting, ROI and NPV templates, and sponsor management feeding the funding plan. We preserved a small margin even as a nonprofit, to cushion against sponsor withdrawal and to allow growth.
5. **Quality management.** Quality plans, process improvement plans, quality metrics, and checklists. User feedback and focus groups were our main signal. Periodic feedback cycles tied to metrics let us push UI and information quality past satisfactory.
6. **Human resources management.** Distribution of work matched skills to people. We checked in on workload and well-being, inside and outside the project, to minimize burnout.
7. **Communications management.** Communications plan posted in MS Teams, with Teams as the primary hub and backup channels documented. Scheduled meetings, with the ability to spin up an ad-hoc Zoom when text and email could not carry the situation.
8. **Risk management.** Risk plan and risk register, with qualitative and quantitative analysis and responses. Lexi's prior risk class gave that section more depth than we would have had on our own.
9. **Procurement management.** Procurement plan informed budget and sponsor outreach. Change requests pushed the budget up considerably between first estimate and final.
10. **Stakeholder management.** Stakeholder management plan and register kept us oriented to who we were serving with each change. Every scope decision passed back through stakeholder motivations.
