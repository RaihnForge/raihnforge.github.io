> *Prepared for ADMG 479, Project Management Maturity, for Professor Dr. Laura Williamson. Drafted by Joshua Keyes, June 4, 2025.*

## Introduction

My journey through the Project Management specialization at Central Washington University has been one of steady growth, practical application, and transformative learning. With a diverse professional background spanning design, compliance, and team leadership, I entered this program with a desire to formalize my management experience and refine my skills in organizational planning, execution, and strategic communication. This reflection paper serves as a capstone for that passage, offering a comprehensive reflection on how this specialization shaped my growth as a project manager.

Throughout the specialization, I gained hands-on experience with tools like Microsoft Project, Excel, and Jira (IT 490A/B, ADMG 474/475). I developed fluency in frameworks such as Agile (ADMG 479), Earned Value Management (ADMG 477), and the Project Management Maturity Model (ADMG 479); and I enhanced my leadership, reporting, and collaboration skills through both academic simulations and real-world projects. Early foundational courses in business (ADMG 201), sustainability (ADMG 285), and communication (ADMG 385) built the base upon which advanced project execution, risk mitigation, and reporting skills were layered (ADMG 476, 477). These experiences collectively helped refine my understanding of how successful projects are conceived, guided, and delivered.

Upon beginning this journey, I endeavored to gain greater validation for my previous achievements while acquiring the academic language to better analyze and convert my previous failed project history into valuable experiences that support personal mastery and stronger future execution. Reaching for my undergraduate degree since middle school, and coming from a challenging childhood, I have worked hard to complete this program in a way that honors Central Washington University, reflects the high standards of the United States Marine Corps, and expresses the heart of a critical and creative thinker committed to community and service. I also take pride in the fact that my daily work directly impacts the wellness of K-12 students through our educational tools, which support emotional intelligence and double-loop learning concepts as explored in ADMG 372 and ADMG 285.

Exploring lessons across the specialization highlights how they shape my growth, clarify my strengths, and reveal where I still intend to improve. It outlines my current development state, early career goals, and professional outlook in a diverse and global environment. I strive to enrich my character through the core values of Honor, Courage, and Commitment that I have adopted as my foundation during my service as a US Marine.

## Application to Career

This undergraduate degree equips me with the tools and resources necessary to chart the trajectory of my continued growth. As I transition from academic work into the next phase of my professional life, I carry with me both the frameworks I have studied and the leadership responsibilities I have embraced in the field. The first few years of my project management career will continue to bridge academic refinement and applied experience, grounded in theory, real-world environments, and made flexible through the application of IT202 (Change Management Skills).

My work at an EdTech employer over the past three years has served as both my job and my internship experience. In this role, I have worked with engineers, content directors, marketing leads, and customer service managers to scope, plan, and execute a variety of technical and creative initiatives. I led the development of our organizational brand kit, managed cross-team collaboration, and contributed to the successful pursuit of a SOC 2 Type I certification by drawing directly on my training in cybersecurity (IT 238), IT management, and comprehension of ISO (International Standards Organization) compliance learned in Sustainable Decision-Making (ADMG 285).

This lived experience made courses like ADMG 474/475 (Executing Project Management) and IT 301 (Cybersecurity and Ethics) especially resonant. Through them, I gained language to understand better what I was already doing intuitively, and I learned to course-correct where instinct alone had previously failed. I also discovered new value in analyzing project risk, understanding organizational maturity, and utilizing performance reporting to align team actions with strategic objectives.

Courses like ADMG 374 (Project Management) and ADMG 302 (Financial Analysis) provided tools such as the triple constraint model, risk registers, and financial ratio interpretation. ADMG 479 deepened my systems thinking through the maturity model assessment. From ADMG 372 (Leadership and Supervision), I learned to recognize and apply concepts such as ETTO (Efficiency and Thoroughness Trade-Off), and from ADMG 285 (Sustainable Decision Making), I gained an understanding of the distinction between quality and grade. Both proved essential in stakeholder communications.

These concepts have helped me grow beyond seeing project management as a checklist. I now view it as a dynamic interplay of time, cost, scope, people, and culture. I have learned to visualize systems, synthesize technical and interpersonal details, and communicate constraints and opportunities more clearly and effectively across teams.

That said, I continue to identify areas for development. Delegation and trusting others with execution are skills that I am still building. My strong work ethic can sometimes lead to taking on too much, which risks creating bottlenecks in my workflow. Additionally, I aim to deepen my fluency in project budgeting, forecasting, and portfolio-level analysis.

Perhaps the most significant shift has been my perspective on success. It is not an individual deliverable, but rather a collective outcome shaped by a shared understanding and an aligned vision. It is not the failure or success of the deliverable, but rather how I can learn, improve, and iterate to achieve a place of positive, ethical change that is impactful. Through leadership skills, administrative understanding, and creative problem-solving, I am confident that I am capable of improving myself, others, and my community.

## Professional Development

Looking ahead, I view professional development not as a linear checklist, but as an evolving map, one that adapts as I grow in awareness, responsibility, and skill. My immediate goal is to refine the core competencies of a project manager while intentionally shaping the broader identity of a strategic leader, storyteller, and systems thinker.

In the short term, I plan to pursue professional certifications that align with the work I have already begun. The Project Management Professional (PMP) certification is a significant milestone, both for its rigorous standards and its widespread recognition across industries. In tandem, I am exploring PMI-ACP or Scrum Master certifications to deepen my understanding of Agile principles (reinforced in ADMG 479) and better support iterative, user-centered workflows.

Beyond certification, I intend to sharpen my technical fluency. While I am already comfortable with tools like Jira, Microsoft Project, and Canva, I want to grow more proficient in data analysis (IT 165), budget forecasting (ADMG 302), and portfolio-level reporting (ADMG 477). These are the areas where strategic decisions are often made, and I want to be capable of influencing them with confidence and clarity.

To continue making positive contributions, I must develop my skills as a communicator and facilitator. I plan to seek out opportunities to lead cross-functional meetings, mentor junior team members, and contribute to organizational learning materials (ADMG 385, ADMG 372). Whether through formal presentations or informal storytelling, I want to be someone who brings people together through a clear vision, emotional intelligence, and shared purpose.

In the longer term, I envision myself moving toward a Product Manager or Senior Project Strategist role within a research and development (R&D) team. These are spaces where innovation, systems design, and human impact converge, and they are areas I feel deeply called to. I intend to supplement my growth with books on leadership and systems thinking, actively engage in professional communities, and possibly pursue a master's degree in organizational leadership or design strategy once I have gained more field experience.

Throughout this journey, I will continue to use personal reflection, feedback loops, and trusted peer networks to refine not only what I do, but also how and why I do it. My goal is to remain adaptable, grounded, and purpose-driven while also staying open to learning. This is the example of a holistic, responsible, and empathic leader that I wish to embody.

## Diverse Global Perspective

Equally crucial to professional development is cultivating a global mindset and the cultural intelligence necessary for inclusive leadership. One of the most unexpected and rewarding dimensions of my project management education has been the deepening of my global and cultural awareness. While many of the tools and frameworks we study are universally applicable, the contexts in which they are used vary dramatically across teams, industries, and cultures. Understanding that has reshaped how I approach leadership, communication, and collaboration.

Working in a remote-first company like an EdTech employer taught me that diversity is not just about background. Perspective, timing, and context are valuable assets that add to success. In leading projects with contributors across departments and regions, I learned to pause, ask better questions, and design communication methods that honored different work styles and cultural rhythms. This flexibility clarifies goals and ensures the best possible outcomes. Successful projects are very often about creating shared understanding through considerate communication.

Academically, I was exposed to international case studies and diverse ethical frameworks that challenged my assumptions. For instance, discussions in ADMG 285 around the triple bottom line and in ADMG 201 on business ethics forced me to consider how decisions made in one market ripple out across global supply chains and ecosystems. Similarly, exploring Agile in the context of international teams (ADMG 479) highlighted both its adaptability and its dependence on strong communication and trust.

I have also come to see my own background experiences in military service, creative freelancing, and working with veteran and educational communities as a lens that helps me connect across divides. These experiences taught me to listen first, translate complexity, and adapt without losing authenticity. As I grow, I want to expand that lens by working on international teams, attending global conferences, and continuing to study cross-cultural leadership models.

Moving forward, I view inclusivity not as a checkbox, but as a foundational skill for ethical and effective project management. It is not just about knowing how to navigate differences. It is about seeing diversity as an asset to decision-making, innovation, and long-term success. Whether leading a local initiative or contributing to a globally distributed team, I want to be someone who bridges gaps, invites participation, and helps shape outcomes that reflect a broader human perspective.

## Conclusion

Looking back on my journey through the Project Management specialization, I am struck not just by how much I have learned, but also by how much I have clarified who I am as a professional. Each course added a new tool, perspective, or practice, but more importantly, they helped me frame the kind of leader I want to become. Someone who does not just manage tasks but fosters clarity, alignment, and shared meaning across people and systems.

The academic foundation developed through intellectual exploration of concepts such as sustainability, stakeholder communication, and Agile implementation with risk consideration has equipped me with more than just technical skills. It has given me a way to think holistically about projects, to ask better questions, and to lead with both precision and empathy. These lessons are continuous in my full-time work at an EdTech employer, where theory meets action and growth takes root in our everyday collaborative work.

I am entering the next chapter with clear intentions. I aim further to develop my technical, strategic, and interpersonal skills. I plan to continue seeking mentorship, offering mentorship, and serving teams in a way that balances results with well-being. My contribution will be to build systems that work effectively and, most importantly, work for those in our global community.

Ultimately, this capstone experience has affirmed what I have long believed: that meaningful leadership is about integrity, adaptability, and the willingness to grow through challenge. I am ready to take what I have learned and turn it into action, not perfectly, but purposefully. That, to me, reflects the kind of leader this journey has prepared me to become. And in all I do, I strive to live by the core values instilled in me by the Marine Corps: Honor, Courage, and Commitment.
