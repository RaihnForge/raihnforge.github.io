## Contingency Theories

Contingency theories are anchored by the concept of a leader's use of different leadership styles as situations change. Situational leadership implies that success can be achieved when the leadership style matches the situation. There is contention between developers of the theory. Ken Blanchard and Paul Hersey differ from Fred Fiedler. Fiedler believes that personalities have innate leadership styles (VanHuss, 2014, p. 35). Fiedler would conclude that if the situation changes, a new person would need to be found to fit the leadership style required by the situation. In opposition, Blanchard and Hersey's concept essentially credits individuals with a greater ability to be flexible. Their view is that the same person could adapt to a new required leadership style.

The benefits of changing leadership styles to match the situation are innumerable. Flexibility in an environment is generally coveted. Being able to adapt to changes in project scope, cost, and time constraints is important, especially in the information technology sector. "The approach is adaptive. This means that it allows organizations to stay flexible and transform their policies and strategies according to the changing environment" (Go Assignment Help, 2018).

## Concept and Values of Each Theory

### Scientific Management

Frederick Taylor is the father of scientific management. His goal was to simplify and optimize the way tasks were performed (VanHuss, 2014, p. 29).

Four principles:

1. Conduct a scientific study of workers' tasks. Replace old rule-of-thumb methods with improved ones.
2. Scientifically select, train, and develop each employee rather than leaving workers to teach themselves.
3. Cooperate with employees to ensure they do their work according to the scientifically developed principles.
4. Divide the work nearly equally between managers and workers, with managers doing the planning and workers the execution.

The theory is tailored for factory and industrial settings and is less relevant for service industries. Through the movement, the concept of motion studies was popularized, which led to a great increase in efficiency on many fronts (VanHuss, 2014, p. 29).

Advantages of scientific management systems are efficient production, reduced inaccuracy, and the ability to control. Some disadvantages are that it may be overly bureaucratic, capital intensive, and prone to creating work division (Nyanchama, 2019).

I believe this concept is important as a way to increase efficiency. I believe it is weak as a structure to manage by, but strong as a method to implement effective and positive changes to procedures and practices. It lacks a great deal of accounting for the human element, except as a purely mechanical aspect of the overall machine, which it excels at when observing motion studies. The human condition is accounted for more completely when looking at Administrative Management's *esprit de corps* and remuneration principles. Behavioral Management focuses almost solely on the mentality of the workforce, which is in direct contention with the scientific management theory.

### Administrative Management

This theory focuses on managers leading and taking initiative, finding major functions that allow for carrying out the intent of the organization through the use of management principles and practices. Henri Fayol developed the fourteen principles that expand on function. Division of work, authority and responsibility, discipline, unity of command, unity of direction, subordination of individual interests to the general interest, remuneration, centralization, scalar chain, order, equity, stability of tenure of personnel, initiative, and *esprit de corps* (VanHuss, 2014, p. 31).

According to Commerce Mates, advantages of Administrative Management are higher productivity, facilitating goal achievement, and data-based decisions. Disadvantages include being management oriented, borrowing concepts from military science, and ignoring environmental changes (Commerce Mates, 2021).

Administrative Management is a holistic approach. In contrast to the other observed methods, it addresses the greatest range of aspects in management. It accounts for the human condition, organizational priorities, and functional features while ensuring the system provides flexibility to grow and adjust. This is codified in the principle of equity. It may not have the focus of the other discussed theories, but it allows for the inclusion of concepts from both.

### Behavioral Management

Elton Mayo made several observations about workplace efficiency. Eventually, his observations equated to the human element in the workplace. Attention, socialization, and meaning are three surviving meaningful concepts that are still relevant today (VanHuss, 2014, p. 33).

There are many advantages to this approach and very few disadvantages. Humans finding meaning in what they are doing is important, and the human element should always be valued. A disadvantage to this system may be when it is implemented incorrectly. Meaning is not synonymous with feeling happy, but it often leads to a sense of happiness. "Contrary to conventional wisdom, meaningful experiences are not always pleasant. Seeking relevance through meaning is not about searching for the holy grail of happiness, but searching for depth and connection with others" (Peppercorn, 2018). If a manager is attempting to make their workers happy all the time, failure will be imminent.

This system of management simultaneously captures the most important aspect of management while also lacking structured information on other aspects. In the military, this contention is often referred to as "troop welfare and mission accomplishment." It is the ever delicate dance between accomplishing objectives and ensuring a healthy human condition. These two concepts can often be at odds with each other. Behavioral Management excels at prioritizing the human condition. It is not necessarily in absolute opposition to the Scientific Management theory, but its foremost priorities are relatively the most distant.

## Preferred System

While I believe that all systems have relevance and application in today's world, Scientific Management and Behavioral Management seem to me to be akin to contingency theories, in the sense that they should be implemented for the specific situations in which they may excel. To be even more bold, I would generally not favor using them exclusively as the foundation for a management system I would put in place. I would use them as pillars, or even as quality control analysis tools.

By far, I believe that the Administrative Management theory is the most fitting, broadly applicable, and relevant system. Aspects of the other two systems could (and do) augment the structure set forth by Henri Fayol. This is also the most time and stress tested system discussed, with roots in military organizational theory. Though its weaknesses may appear to suggest that it is too stiff and less agile, the reality is that it does not have to be.

The theory values initiative and a strong balance between personal creative problem solving and structured application of practices. The principles are less directives to execute than they are realities of management that must be accounted for responsibly. Ultimately, this is the most flexible, contemporary, relevant, and reasonable management system foundation. It is my recommended and preferred management system.
