## Abstract

There is a growing concern regarding sustainability in the domain of technology devices (Paska, 2010). Notably, cellular devices have increased consumption rates and disposal at end-of-life disposition pathways, especially the integrated circuit (IC) and display components (Ercan et al., 2016). Often, devices are destined for landfills, adding to toxic decomposition. Even attempts to recycle or remanufacture still require a great deal of energy and resources (Pamminger et al., 2021). Sustainable practices with well-designed product life cycle accountability considerations would reduce material and energy waste and remove harmful disposition pathway results. One way to affect the rate of new production and consumption of devices would be to create a secondary-use phase for the use phase in the product life cycles.

This document proposes conducting a Life Cycle Assessment study to learn the potential sustainability benefits of alternative secondary-use cases for cellular devices.

The Secondary-Use-Case categories will be three: Communications Augmentation, Learning Assistance, and Workstation Augmentation. The devices studied would be donated iPhones that are two years past release and repurposed to facilitate the described use-case categories. The study will assess university students in Washington and take place over the course of one year. Surveys before and after will be given to collect data on the viability and usefulness of devices. Compatibility data between the three use categories and newer products with similar use cases will be assessed to garner an understanding of potential sustainability gains. The study is requesting $100,000 for research labor, resources, and required materials over a two-year period.

Organizations may benefit from the information, as it could allow sponsors or stakeholders to buy into Circular Economy (CE) design approach changes for products internally or externally by modifying product strategies and design for the alternate use phase. Companies could divest in the production of products, invest in software development, and replace previous revenue streams with much more lucrative and sustainable services income with greater profit margins while gaining brand goodwill and avoiding costly late adaptation of compliance policies (Paska, 2010). Greater understanding could lead to cleaner environments that consumers would benefit from, more useful products, and increases in individual ethics and health (Stahel, 2016). Global ecology would benefit from sustainable considerations and reduced consumption and harmful disposal of resources (Brundtland, 1987).

The proposed study would be a step toward validating the necessary evolution to Circular Economy (CE) design models and approaches in place of linear economy (LE) Industrial Revolution design models, which will provide for a more responsible and sustainable future.

## Background and Overview

Understanding the key sustainability concepts of life cycle thinking, life cycle assessment, life cycle cost analysis, the triple bottom line, and circular economy is imperative to the consideration of the proposed study.

Life Cycle Thinking is the beginning of consideration of a manmade product or service informed through the lens of natural life cycles for the purpose of gathering information about obtaining all stages of a life cycle's materials to produce, manufacture, and use, and waste management, in order to educate and enact sustainable system designs (Vigon et al., 2020). Understanding sustainability, ecological footprint (Ecological Footprint, n.d.), and product life cycle is essential for making sustainable choices for product life cycles, as is being addressed in cellular phone devices. It establishes systems thinking and boundaries to define and manage elements.

Sustainability is a broad term that indicates that "humanity has the ability to make development sustainable to ensure that it meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs" (United Nations, 1987). In the context of ecological discussions, actions that align with future generations' consideration of meeting their resource needs would be considered sustainable actions.

The five stages of the product lifecycle are imperative in assisting in scope and determining the boundaries of a system (Matthews et al., 2014, p. 12). Systems thinking refers to the collection or set of interconnected parts. At the same time, boundaries are a subset of a system that is of interest or to be focused on for a specific purpose (Matthews et al., 2014). The stages inform this proposal's structure.

The study will be a Life Cycle Assessment, thus relying on quantitative methods and systems thinking to assess the inputs and outputs of a product or service's life cycle within a given scope. However, it is still important to implement qualitative elements. The Life Cycle Assessment Framework is utilized in the development of Life Cycle Assessments. International Standards Organization (ISO) compliance is sought so that LCAs may be sufficiently useful to the academic community.

The Life Cycle Assessment Framework utilizes four phases. Goal and scope define the study design parameters and system boundaries through statements of intent. The following more quantitative phases include inventory analysis, which is the collection of documented data, and the impact assessment, which addresses the point at which reporting of inventory results alludes to greater implications. The interpretation phase is the more qualitative aspect of an assessment that suggests a greater perspective and recommendations for change.

Standards such as goals, system boundaries, function, and functional unit, as well as data source and inventory, are some of the LCA design parameters closely scrutinized and must be considered carefully as they are applied to the Life Cycle Assessment framework in order for the proposed study to be ISO compliant. These elements must be standardized and agreed upon, as they allow for global pursuits to communicate and build a universally available and connected knowledge base and comparable results (Matthews et al., 2014). This study aims to be ISO compliant and will observe outlined standards.

Life Cycle Cost Analysis, as its name indicates, is a holistic understanding related to the total cost estimates throughout the life cycle duration pertaining to operating a system or product. The analysis includes many important factors to understand (Matthews et al., 2014). LCCA Domains, model types, and temporal accounting are key in understanding Life Cycle Cost Analysis. This type of analysis informs Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) in its Systems Thinking approach to cost-benefit consideration orientation and overlap in methodologies.

The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) considers sustainability in terms of environmental, social, and economic aspects, and it is important for our study to address expected benefits and trade-offs appropriately. Derived from John Elkington in consideration of business and sustainability, the concept posits that sustainability must be achieved by balancing three factors that drive the input and output of resources (Sustainability Illustrated, 2014). Through photosynthesis, all energy on the planet is derived; thus, the planet (environment) is a consideration of all resource existence. Nested within the planet is the people (social) consideration, those of us who require the resources (Sustainability Illustrated, 2014). The profit (economy) is an aspect of society that draws from resources and converts them into products and services useful to people.

Businesses and society must understand TBL, as it is a holistic approach to resource transactions and management. From a business perspective, a corporation's success should be measured by social, ethical, and environmental performance, not simply the traditional profit-centric financial bottom line (Norman & MacDonald, 2004).

According to Walter R. Stahel (2016), Circular Economy (CE) is a model for product life cycle design that aims to close loops in industrial ecosystems by closing end-of-life product life cycle stage loops into the resource needs of other products, to minimize or eradicate all waste. This study stems from the author's proposition that new technologies should trend toward circular and performance economies and achieve successful Circular Economy (CE) models for the sake of sustainability.

Equally, Deborah Andrews' (2015) assessment that narrows in on designers and education as the core elements required to realize the Circular Economy (CE) model influences this proposed study's goals to provide more academic material to inform product designer approaches. According to *The Circular Economy, Design Thinking and Education for Sustainability* (Andrews, 2015), the circular economy is an alternative to the status quo, the Linear Economy model. This model is roughly: take, make, use, and dispose of. The article states that the Linear Economy model's waste has been exacerbated by the first industrial designers' culture of planned obsolescence and is unsustainable, given the rising population and resource needs.

In the article *Modeling of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios for Smartphones* (Pamminger et al., 2021), Circular Economy (CE) is addressed as a concept integral to what are called circular end-of-use scenarios. It notes that Circular Economy (CE) is related to cradle-to-cradle product life cycle concepts and end-of-pipe treatment according to *Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter* (Matthews et al., 2014). Therefore, understanding this design model is integral to weighing the validity of the proposed study.

The ecological impact of cellular devices continues to grow as more devices are consumed and replaced in greater quantities (Paska, 2010). Disposal at end-of-life disposition pathways remains ill-conditioned to assuage the harmful environmental fallout. There are concerns about the integrated circuit (IC) as well as device display components (Ercan et al., 2016). Toxic decomposition or remanufacturing and recycling processes are poisonous and harmful to the environment (Ercan et al., 2016). Additionally, most cradle-to-cradle processes still require a great deal of energy and resources (Pamminger et al., 2021).

Sustainable practices with well-designed product life cycle accountability considerations would reduce material and energy waste and remove harmful disposition pathway results. One way to affect the rate of new production and consumption of devices would be to graft a Circular Economy design model over current products to superimpose additional use phase states on these troublesome products. These Secondary-Use-Case phases could be viable alternatives that influence adjustments in design thinking for current Product Life Cycles. This document proposes conducting a Life Cycle Assessment study with the goal of learning the potential sustainability benefits involving alternative secondary-use cases for cellular devices.

## Goal

The intended application will be to design alternate second-use phases for devices to extend their life and reduce the production of similar peripheral utility devices manufactured for more introductory and limited use cases. The study aims to discover the viability of considering repurposing as an End-of-Life Treatment (EoLT) strategy in conjunction with Recycling, Repair, and Remanufacturing in order to convince industries to consider Secondary Use Cases in initial product design, development, and production.

The study is proposed to promote sustainability through the adoption of the Circular Economy model of design and the implementation of secondary-use-case use phases in the product life cycles. The Secondary Use-Case end-of-life treatment would allow used or old stock previous-generation Primary Use-Case cellular smartphone devices to re-enter product lifecycles with new secondary use phases. This would supplant the demand for lower-capability devices, which would incur additional production, energy, and resource consumption.

Audiences for the study would include product developers and multiple quality-of-life support organizations. Product developers, such as businesses, can respond to circular economy constraints by developing devices that account for second-use scenarios. This will increase the consumer value of the product and allow greater profit for businesses that support the design philosophy, as it may require fewer resources and manufacturing while increasing their competitive edge through innovation and maintaining attractive ESG policies.

Quality-of-life support organizations would benefit from donations of devices that are unneeded by original owners and no longer have a use case, and could go to organizations that can distribute them to those with use case needs. The flexibility of second-use phase alternatives will increase the fulfillment of a broader range of demands from those in need. Aspen Women's Center, Salvation Army, Wounded Warriors, and Goodwill are examples of organizations that would benefit and assist in re-distribution.

Considering comparative results with other studies relating to recycling, remanufacturing, and repair End-of-Life Treatment (EoLT) strategies that are discussed in such works as *Modelling of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios for Smartphones* (Pamminger et al., 2021). Comparable studies such as these allow the propagation of more informed decisions that can prioritize not just a more sustainable option but also the most efficient sustainability option.

## Scope

The product or system to be studied will be a cellular smart device. Specifically targeted are iPhones that are two years past their original release and have also reached their primary Product Life Cycle "Use" phase as determined by the donation of the product from its original owner. This is because iPhones represent a notable share of the market, phone configurations are generally restrictive, and use cases are narrower than those of other devices.

Function and Functional units include one smartphone with a primary use-case that has reached end-of-life treatment and been repurposed to satisfy a secondary use-case. The primary use case is considered consumers' traditional use of daily cellular devices. The Secondary Use Case will be one of the following use-case categories: Communications Augmentation (webcam and additional video recording device), Learning Assistance (learning application platform service tablet), or Workstation Augmentation (desktop peripheral for additional controls — macros, media controls, mixers). The original use phase has expired. The average Primary Use-Case Phase is 2.5 years (Pamminger et al., 2021). Calculated valuations will be in monetary units. Also, in line with other comparable studies, the units of Global Warming Potential (GWP), energy consumption during all life cycle phases, and Abiotic Depletion Potential (ADP) resource depletion calculation will be utilized to determine ecological impacts to measure sustainability. Finally, two surveys will be conducted pertaining to the initial intended use phase application and post-application use of the second-use-case product after one year.

System Boundaries: Washington State University students who have an alternate Primary Use-Case Smartphone acquire a Second Use-Case Smartphone with a designated Second Use-Case function over the course of two years. The Study will focus on extending the Use Phase of LCA.

Resulting evaluations from monetary, Global Warming Potential (GWP), and Abiotic Depletion Potential (ADP) with Life Cycle Cost Analysis (LCCA) considerations will result in actionable data to assist in the potential sustainability of the second-use-case design philosophy. Compatibility assessments of the two conducted surveys will assist in establishing the demand and viability of the second use case use phase design philosophy. Further determining current implementation challenges and obstacles and discovering the most attractive second-use case applications will assist in further studies. These results will increase the academic body of knowledge that will inform the strategies for viable Circular Economy (CE) model implementations for the production of cellular devices in the future.

## Expected Benefits and Trade-offs

Benefits and trade-offs will be addressed by considering the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) concept. People will benefit from more flexible devices and platforms to increase the quality of daily life. Fewer devices will allow for less administrative upkeep and consideration from individuals. The solution should reduce consumer spending by reducing materials and product manufacturing overhead. More jobs can be created to support services, platforms, and innovations required for Circular Economy (CE) considerations (Stahel, 2016). Individuals may have a better account of their personal actions through the lens of their own sustainability ethics.

Profits for organizations should grow through the implementation of multiple second-use phase designs. Initially the argument could be made that less consumption would yield less profits. The counter is that lower resource and production costs in materials and manufacturing will lead to great savings in operational costs. This alone would not make up for profits. However, an increased focus on software and services for their platforms implemented to support the multi-use-case product would likely exceed traditional product turnover consumption profits, and likely by a large margin, as digital goods' limit on return on investment is capped very high. Due to second-use case applications and services, businesses will likely enjoy a greater market position. A competitive edge in initial device marketing will be had by those who implement Circular Economy (CE) changes more quickly than others. Greater compliance with sustainability policies will save money in the long term from politically pressured compliance (Paska, 2010). Lastly, company valuations will increase through goodwill and brand loyalty impacts.

Global ecological (planet) benefits will reduce resource consumption from the lack of demand for individual-purpose devices, as they would be supplemented by last-generation devices that still likely exceed the capabilities of the limited-use, lower-powered consumer products. Reduced energy consumption will be evidenced by less transportation during distribution phases of product life cycles, as less distribution will be necessary. Devices would be able to be updated to their second-use case life via online updates similar to the current Operating System (OS) upgrade actions, which are known processes practiced currently.

## Conclusion

Sustainability in consideration of resources for future generations, as indicated by the Brundtland Report (Brundtland, 1987), is an achievable goal assigned to the world in 1987. Today, with the evolution to Circular Economy (CE) models in place of traditional Industrial Revolution Linear Economy (LE) models through the creative implementation of second-use-case use phases in a Product Life Cycle, this goal becomes increasingly viable by slowing the rate of resource consumption and changing consumer behaviors. If approved, this study will yield significant findings that support the greater sustainability movement and inform decision-makers about Economic, Social, and Governance (ESG) considerations while ensuring a healthier future for the entire planet and all systems inherently associated.
