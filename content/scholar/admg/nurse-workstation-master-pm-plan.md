## Introduction

The Nurse Workstation Refresh Phase 1 is a technology refresh necessary to maintain high quality standards, reduce future maintenance costs, increase security, and increase efficiency for the stakeholders of Central Medical Center. Conducting Phase 1 of the greater initiative will begin the facility-wide refresh, prioritizing increased quality to areas that represent the first-line interaction with clients. 101 nurse workstations on floors 3, 4, and 5 will be updated to Windows 10. This document captures the costs, timelines, and scope necessary to achieve project success, with the clarity in their concept, execution, and control that the organization expects.

## Project Management Approach

Organizational standards in compliance with organizational core values for floors 3 to 5 of Central Medical Center require that the Nurse Workstation Refresh Phase 1 project updates facilities workstations and technology. The four-stage Phase 1 project will bring the organization in line with internal and external industry standards while saving on costly maintenance and potential failure expenditures.

The project will be completed in four stages led by Joshua Keyes (Project Manager), directed by Dr. Phylicia Patterson (Project Sponsor), and supported by the Nursing Coordinator, IT Coordinator, the Master Load Team, the Install Team, IT Assists, and the Clean-up Team. All teams report to Project Manager Keyes directly, and the staff of teams report to team leaders. Project Sponsor Dr. Patterson has executive authority over all project staff. Budgetary, timeline, and scope changes must be approved by Project Manager Keyes and authorized by Project Sponsor Dr. Patterson.

## Project Scope

**In scope.** Phase 1 of the Nurse Workstation Refresh Initiative is the complete updating of the operating system on all 101 systems currently servicing floors 3 to 5 of Central Medical Center to Windows 10. The refresh includes validation testing for the application ecosystem, established network communication, and cyber security standards. This process will require expenditure of not more than $500,000. Time to completion will be no longer than 6 months from initiation. Equipment will be focused on currently available on-site utilities. Staffing includes the Project Manager, Project Sponsor, Nursing Coordinator, IT Coordinator, Master Load Team, Install Team, IT Assists, and Clean-up Team. All directives will consider the input of stakeholders and the evaluation of the Project Sponsor.

**Out of scope.** The project must not prioritize other phases of the overall initiative over the scope of Phase 1. The project is not responsible for the budget, timeline, or quality of further progression of the overall initiative. Network infrastructure, software licensing deals, client capacity considerations, and hardware upgrade concerns are not within scope. Being part of a larger initiative, updating a digital ecosystem in a networked infrastructure for a large organization can lead to the desire to add tasks to the priority of an OS update project. These other considerations must be addressed in follow-up projects and cannot creep into the scope of this project. Take note of suggestions when pertinent, and maintain trust that further improvements will be addressed later.

## Change Management Plan

Changes are likely to occur during Phase 1. Focus on the primary project constraints (scope, budget, schedule) will be particularly important. The project implements a Change Control Board (CCB) as the method of reviewing and tracking petitions for change.

**Approach.** Change handling includes flexibility, responsibility, and communication within the organizational culture. Bottom-up understanding of project status and needs is required to gain accurate advisement for change. Priority order: (1) changes are within scope, time, and budgetary constraints; (2) change applications are paramount and carefully considered before execution; (3) execution of change is carefully controlled.

**Definition.** Any petition for action that may impact the three major constraints must be considered a change. Documentation and communication of changes are required before implementation. Changes are the responsibility of the Project Manager through the approved authorization of the Project Sponsor.

**Change Control Board.** The CCB is consulted on any change impacting the project's three constraints. Change requests will be logged and reported to the CCB by Project Manager Keyes. The CCB meets bimonthly to review change petitions. Approved changes are implemented and documented by the Project Manager. Updated documentation is made available to the Project Sponsor, the CCB, and select Stakeholders by approval of the Project Sponsor.

**Roles and responsibilities.** The Project Sponsor approves and authorizes all changes impacting the three constraints and chairs the CCB. The Project Manager manages petitions, analyzes potential impacts, mediates proposed changes, documents approvals, communicates changes to approved parties, and sits on the CCB. The Project Team and Stakeholders submit change request forms; forms must be filled out as completely as possible to ensure proper consideration.

**Process.** Stakeholders and team members may submit change forms made available by the Project Manager. Forms are considered and prepared for submission to the CCB by the Project Manager. The bimonthly CCB meeting assesses petitions, and resulting actions are authorized by the CCB Chair. Upon authorization, the Project Manager incorporates changes to project documentation and communicates updated documentation to affected parties. Responses to change forms (approved or denied) are communicated by the Project Manager back to the submitting parties.

## Cost Management Plan

Cost management is the responsibility of Project Manager Keyes and is authorized by Project Sponsor Dr. Patterson. Meetings between the Project Sponsor and Project Manager occur bi-monthly, with preparations and calculations of the budget being made by the Project Manager. Any deviations beyond anticipated variances from baseline are the responsibility of the Project Manager under the authority of the Project Sponsor.

**Approach.** Control Accounts (CA) are created for each work package of the WBS at the fifth level. This level is chosen based on the majority of project install materials being broken down into the fifth level of the WBS. CAs are given 100 percent credit at the beginning of their third-level phase to ensure readiness of resources. Costs may be rounded to the nearest 100th dollar and 8th hour (1 day) of work, to mitigate time investment in budget evaluations given the frequency of updating and auditing.

Cost variances of +/- 0.2 are indicated at yellow for cautionary, and +/- 0.5 is given to indexes considered out of anticipated budget. These alert-level variances are reported to the Project Sponsor, with the responsibility to validate and control resting with the Project Manager. Final changes to the budget are prepared by the Project Manager and approved through the authority of the Project Sponsor.

**Measuring project costs.** Schedule Variance (SV), Cost Variance (CV), Schedule Performance Index (SPI), and Cost Performance Index (CPI) are the primary means of calculating costs for the project.

- **SV** = EV minus PV. Positive SV means ahead of schedule, negative means behind.
- **CV** = EV minus AC. Positive CV means under budget, negative means over.
- **SPI** = EV divided by PV. SPI above 1.0 means ahead of schedule.
- **CPI** = EV divided by AC. CPI above 1.0 means under budget.

Variances between SPI or CPI above 0.2 require reporting by the Project Manager to the Project Sponsor. SPI or CPI above 0.5 require reporting and corrective planning by the Project Manager, with approval by the Project Sponsor.

Reporting format is MS Project plus written recommendations, calculations, and status updates by the Project Manager. Estimate at Completion (EAC) and Estimate to Completion (ETC) earned-value analyses are expected to be reported. The Cost Variance Response Process includes a meeting of the Project Manager and Project Sponsor to discuss and review a Cost Change Plan. Cost Change Control Processes are implemented and managed by the Project Manager and authorized by the Project Sponsor.

**Project budget.** The Project Budget falls within the mandated $500,000 budget, delineated as follows:

| Category | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| Fixed Costs | Usage of building resources to perform and displace current operations |
| Material Costs | Computer workstation replacement hardware |
| Labor Costs | Calculated using assigned resources to work packages via MS Project |
| Reserve | Remaining cost difference to the $500,000 mandate, held against unforeseen circumstances |

Technology projects tend toward significant variances. Unforeseen circumstances may allow for the request by the Project Manager of reserve funds, approved by the Project Sponsor. The project is currently projected to complete under the mandated budget.

## Schedule Management Plan

The Schedule Management Plan details the approach, control, resources, and adjustments of the schedule. Scheduling aligns with the predictive approach. The Precedence Diagram Method (PDM) is utilized. Critical Path Methodology is employed to derive and determine accurate and adaptable schedule management. Focus on the critical path lets the team prioritize the completion date of the project; ensuring critical paths are attended to appropriately mitigates or removes delays.

Schedule generation accounts for resources, durations, and dependencies of work packages. It is managed against the scope statement, WBS, WBS Dictionary, completion of the Baseline Schedule, Final Budget Approval, Project Start Date, Project Implementation approval, and confirmation of final deliverables. Microsoft Project 2016 is the schedule management software.

The Project Manager reports weekly schedule progress to the Project Sponsor. Any unforeseen delays or adjustments to the critical path are immediately reported to the Project Sponsor. Additional resources or significant re-assignments may be proposed by the Project Manager and authorized by the Project Sponsor.

Petitions for schedule changes that create a slippage, duration, or lag of 8 percent or greater compared to the Schedule Baseline require the express approval of the Project Sponsor. Changes below this threshold may be approved by the Project Manager. In the event a change in scope is necessary and approved, the Project Manager may request a re-baselined project schedule. A scope change or re-baseline of the project schedule may only be conducted with the approval of both Project Manager and Project Sponsor.

## Staffing Management Plan

The Technology Refresh Project has priority over team members in accordance with the project schedule, in order to maintain critical-path integrity. Designated office space is delegated to ensure process organization and resource efficiency. Teams are not required for the entirety of the project cycle but are needed within designated phases. Communication and coordination with functional managers is paramount.

**Staff acquisition.** The nature of the task allows for the utilization of current Central Medical Center staff. Functional Managers from the designated departments are procured in collaboration with standard facility operational needs. Nursing Coordinator, IT Coordinator, Master Load Team, Install Team, IT Assists, and Clean-up Teams are assembled from corresponding departments. Clean-up Teams are selected from administrative volunteer staff, vetted by the IT Coordinator for appropriate skills.

**Develop project team.** The team meets prior to project launch to orient. Members attend project launch and a monthly status meeting. This allows the members to communicate and associate. Engendering a positive team atmosphere and orienting the team to align with organizational goals is paramount. Interpersonal skills, networking, status, and assessment are priorities for the meetings.

**Training.** Functional Managers assess team members and request any additional training that may be required. Slack time is allotted for coordinators to execute remedial or additional training. Based on current and past OPA (Organizational Process Assets), staff possess the skills and knowledge to execute assigned tasks. The standard EEF (Enterprise Environmental Factor) of the organization promotes monthly training time per staff, organized by functional managers. Pre-arranged trainings are expected to suffice during the project life cycle.

**Recognition and rewards.** General recognition and rewards are delegated to functional managers during the life cycle of the project. Team members, upon successful completion, will be awarded both intrinsic and extrinsic benefits.

- Intrinsic: a smoother technology and software ecosystem, recognition from fellow staff and website publication, workflow efficiency, staff recognition on the website, and patient wellness and satisfaction.
- Extrinsic: yearly bonus dependent on project performance, written commendation in personal documentation for future reference and promotions, and vacation time dependent on performance.

**Organizational structure.** Functional Managers are in charge of their teams, while other teams report directly to the Project Manager. During the course of the project the Project Manager may assign management duties or delegate responsibility of one team or functional manager over another, as needed. The overall project team size is small, so flexibility is paramount during execution. A RACI (Responsibility, Accountability, Consult, Informed) matrix and Organizational Chart are attached.

## Risk Management Plan (Summary)

Risk management is necessary because of the potential for varied degrees of deviation from expected outcomes, primarily associated with the core constraints of the project (time, scope, quality, cost, in that order).

To identify risks the Project Manager conducted expert interviews, risk assessment meetings, and historical reviews of similar projects. These are industry-standard strategies for identifying risk. Risks are qualified and prioritized through the risk register and then continuously monitored. Risks are mitigated and avoided by following the contingencies identified per item on the register.

The overall risk of the project is low. The most common sources of known risk are technology, team, scope, business, economy, and competition. Unknown risks are factored into the project's budget and timeline as a 5 percent buffer, based on the overall risk of the project being low. The top three risks are Technology (software compatibility), Legal (meeting of regulations), and Business (space limits). The full Risk Register, qualitative matrix, and response plans are captured in the separate Risk Management Plan deliverable that accompanies this document.
