## Framing

These notes cover the earliest layer of what we now call Hinduism, from the Indus Valley civilization through the Indo-Aryan migration and into the Vedic period, then forward to the inward turn of the Upanishads. The arc is the move from a sacrificial, priest-mediated, outward-facing religion to a meditative, guru-mediated, inward-facing one without ever fully discarding the older layer.

## Terminology

The word *Hindu* itself has a long history that has nothing to do with self-identification at the start. It was first used by ancient Persians to describe the people who lived beyond the Indus River. When Alexander the Great crossed the Indus around 325 BCE, he called the river Indos and the land beyond it India. By the sixteenth century the word appears in Hindu literature itself to distinguish between Hindus and Muslims and other "foreigners." By the nineteenth century the term is widely used by Hindus to describe themselves. Some contemporary Hindus prefer the term *Eternal Dharma* (Sanatana Dharma). *Dharma* in the Rig Veda is the true way to live: it sustains, nourishes, and supports cosmic, religious, and moral order. It includes beliefs concerning karma and rebirth and covers caste duties, legitimate life goals, and the stages of life.

## Pre-Aryan India: The Indus Valley Civilization

The Indus Valley civilization was discovered by archaeologists in the 1920s. It appears to have flourished in the Indus River Valley, reaching peak development between 2300 and 2000 BCE. Among the archaeological artifacts are numerous stone seals that may depict the early development of Hinduism, but many of the seals carry a script that has yet to be deciphered, so our knowledge of the early religion is limited.

## The Indo-Aryans

The Indo-Aryan migration arrived from the northwest around 1500 BCE. Two theories explain the arrival: an invasion theory and a Hindu Nationalist view. The migrants are characterized as nomadic, warlike people who revered the horse, emphasized religious sacrifice, and brought a body of text, the Vedas. Their language was Sanskrit. They organized society into a three-part class structure: priests (Brahmins), warriors (Kshatriyas), and farmers (Vaishyas).

## The Vedic Period

The Vedic period is the earliest period in the development of Hinduism. Its predominant literature is known collectively as the Vedas. The Vedas are regarded as *Shruti*, meaning "revealed" or "heard." *Veda* itself means "wisdom." This is the most sacred literature in the tradition. The religion presented in these texts was polytheistic, with no fixed temples or statues, and sacred knowledge was held by the priests.

## Shruti Literature: The Four Vedas

The Shruti corpus has four Vedas.

- **Rig Veda.** The oldest and most important. It contains 1,028 hymns of praise to the gods. The most popular gods are Indra (storm and battle), Varuna (cosmic order), and Agni (earth and sacrificial fire). The hymns were chanted by priests during sacrificial rituals, which included the sacrifice of animals and food.
- **Sama Veda.** Music drawn from the hymns of the Rig Veda for chanting, used by trained musicians during sacrifice.
- **Yajur Veda.** Ritual formulas used by priests, with instructions for various rituals.
- **Atharva Veda.** Magic spells and incantations for healing, warding off demons, and harming enemies.

## The Rig Veda in Detail

The Rig Veda dates roughly from 1500 to 900 BCE. It was initially an oral tradition. Its content is largely songs of praise to the *devas*, the "shining ones." The devas are personifications of natural forces and human ideals, and they exist within three realms of the cosmos: sky, atmosphere, and earth. Early rituals were performed outdoors. The *asuras* are anti-gods who opposed the more benevolent devas.

Public rituals were presided over by the priest, the most important of whom was the *Brahmin*. The priest uttered sacred chants and sounds known as *mantras*.

## Ritual Sacrifice in the Rig Veda

The purpose of ritual sacrifice was to strengthen the devas, sustain order, and receive blessing in this life. Rituals were designed to ensure human security in changing situations, which made them malleable. There is a cosmic origin of sacrifice: a sacrifice was used by the gods to create humans and the world. Public rituals were called *Śrauta rites*, and they were primarily for the elite.

A ritual sacrifice is the offering of an edible substance to a deity, thrown into a fire with chanting and gestures. The four elements of a ritual sacrifice are the offering, the fire, the chanting, and the gestures. Typical offerings included ghee, animals, grains, soma plant extract, and the deva mushroom. The morning, midday, and evening sacrifices were associated with twenty-four, forty-four, and forty-eight years respectively.

## Gods of the Rig Veda

- **Indra.** Ruler of the atmosphere or intermediate air. God of the monsoons and the thunderbolt. God of war.
- **Varuna.** God of the sky. Associated with natural and moral order. Judged humans in their wrongdoings under the principle of *rita*.
- **Agni.** Liturgical deity. God of fire. Removes sin and guilt. Mediator between humans and gods. God of the home and the earth. God of cremation.

## The Brahmanas

The Brahmanas are the second layer of Shruti literature, dating roughly to 1000-800 BCE. They are directions for priests, commentaries on and interpretations of rituals. The Shrauta rites become increasingly elaborate at this stage. The concept of the *Deva Man*, or god-man, develops here, along with the rise of domestic rites such as *shraddha*. There is also increased speculation on the one ultimate reality, which sets the stage for the Upanishadic turn.

## Vedic Ritual Forms (Dharma)

- **Shrauta.** Public rituals. *Agnihotra* is the most frequently performed, the casting of ghee into fire.
- **Grihya.** Domestic rituals such as daily prayers, marriages, and funerals.
- **Shraddha.** Rituals to honor ancestors. Cannot be performed by women.
- **Atharvan.** Magic charm rituals, primarily to dispel diseases.

## Women's Roles in Vedic Rituals

Women's participation was vital in the Vedic period; the success of a ritual depended on the woman's presence. However, women were never allowed to be the officiant, and they were subject to restrictions justified through two stories: *Varuna's noose* and *Indra's curse*. The women were bound by grass in the temple to be restrained. A woman could not be present during her period. The narrative used to justify this restriction holds that a woman "killed" a priest, and that the priest transferred a portion of the resulting curse onto women, which is then offered as an explanation for menstruation.

A question worth holding here, which the lecture explicitly raised, is whether there is a power above the gods if the gods themselves must be fed.

## The Upanishadic Turn

The Upanishads are the final layer of Shruti literature, and they mark a real shift in what the tradition is doing. The title *Upanishad* itself means "sit down near" or "sit beneath." The texts feature gurus rather than sacrificial priests. The focus moves to the inner self and to one source, one spiritual reality. The rituals of the earlier Vedas are internalized rather than externally performed. There is an emphasis on asceticism, and the heat of ascetic practices is equated with the heat of the sacrificial fire, *tapas*. The purpose of the discipline is to learn how to ignore the body.

This is the move that matters. The same fire is still the central image, but the fire is now interior. The sacrifice is still the central act, but the offering is the self.

## Brahman and Atman

*Brahman* has ultimate reality. It is the one source of all existing things. *Atman* is the inner self, the truest essence of all things. The atman is distinct from the body, mind, and personality complex. The divine self (atman) is in all living things and is grounded in Brahman.

The Upanishadic phrase *Tat tvam asi*, "That thou art," compresses the whole turn into a sentence. The many deities of the Rig Veda are now understood as manifestations of Brahman. The traditional image is the jar: Brahman is the air outside the jar; atman is the air inside the jar. They are not two things.

## The Human Condition in the Upanishads

- **Maya.** Illusion, the magic play of the divine, the perception of separateness.
- **Karma.** The law of cause and effect; the cultivation of moral character.
- **Samsara.** The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth, marked by suffering. (Useful pop-culture handle: *Groundhog Day* with Bill Murray.)
- **Moksha.** Release from samsara.

## Achieving Moksha

The path is *yoga*, meaning union, to unite. The Upanishads center meditation along a disciplinary path. Instruction is given by a guru. These practices can lead to *darshan*, the spiritual insight that produces an understanding of the true nature of reality. The insight required to achieve moksha is found within the self.

## A Short Glossary

- **Asceticism.** Physical discipline for a spiritual goal.
- **Atman.** Breath; the inner self.
- **Maya.** Illusion, the magic play of the divine, the perception of separateness.
- **Samsara.** The cycle of life, death, and rebirth.
- **Moksha.** Release from samsara.
- **Brahmin.** Priest.
- **Deva.** "Shining one"; a god, often a personification of natural force or human ideal.
- **Monsoon.** Rain, buckets of it.

## What the Arc Says

The Vedic-to-Upanishadic arc is the move from outward sacrifice to inward sacrifice. The priest gives way to the guru. The fire on the altar becomes the fire of *tapas*, the ascetic's heat. The many gods become aspects of a single Brahman, and the individual self is no longer the petitioner but is recognized as the same thing being petitioned. None of the older layer is thrown out; it is folded into the new framing. The tradition keeps its furniture and reuses it.

That structural move, where a tradition turns its own tools on itself without breaking with its past, is what makes this section worth the work.
