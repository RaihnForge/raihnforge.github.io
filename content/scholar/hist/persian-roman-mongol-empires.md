Echoes of the greatest empires to span across history still chime resoundingly in the cultures, governments, and imaginations of today. The Persian, Roman, and Mongol methods of building, maintaining, and governing their empires held more similarities than differences. Though their fates were dissimilar, it was through frontier energy, astoundingly driven leadership, and innovative governing that all three secured not just war and bloodshed but long stretches of relative peace and prosperity (Davis 105). Studying these three empires' stories, successes, and failures may still yet inform human civilizations today and in the future.

## Frontier Origins

The adaptive, rugged, and resourceful frontier energy of the Aryans, Romans, and Mongols marked the first fateful steps of building each of their respective empires. The Persians began as Aryan equestrians, brandishing swords and arrows, who rode brazenly atop their horses, quickly conquering their homeland: Iran (Davis 89). The hardened Roman "Plebes" rose up to protect and defend their lands, first by winning veto powers from their "patricians," then onward to the surrounding tribes until they finally secured the majority of Italy. However, they were unsated and lusted for greater lands and triumphs (Davis 94). The tribal Mongols harnessed their brutality and unique hearty steeds to set off toward their destiny of world domination.

## Leadership That Set the Trajectory

These frontier peoples would not have made such a mark, however, had it not been for the strong, determined leaders that guided them to greater victories and conquered lands. Cyrus II led the Persians as he proclaimed his kingship over the four corners of the world (Davis 89). Scipio met and defeated the formidable Hannibal of Carthage's naval and land strategies (Davis 96), securing centuries of Roman supremacy. The Mongols' Genghis Khan, "ruler vast as the ocean" and ordained by the Eternal Blue Heaven to rule all men, would viciously and relentlessly defeat all who questioned his divine destiny (Davis 102). All three were great generals and accomplished tacticians who left a void upon their demise, but their momentum and vision sustained and carried forward for generations.

Shortly after the bright flame of some of history's most notable conquerors was extinguished, the degrees of success varied. The Persian Empire sustained, grew, and met its end after little more than two centuries (Davis 79), whereas the Mongols succumbed in little over a century (Davis 91) and the Roman Empire sustained in differing forms for over a millennium (Davis 101). Despite the differences in longevity, it cannot be said that the quake that shaped the world caused by these figures could be anything less than awesome.

## Governance That Bought Stability

The three empires were successful for a time, at least, in governing methods that provided stability, peace, prosperity, law, and structure. The Persian and Mongol Empires were more tolerant, allowing for provinces and conquered peoples to maintain regional culture, customs, and even rulers so long as they paid tribute (Davis 90, 104). Rome was a little less accommodating, and even less so as time wore on. Roman law was enforced over regions but largely to the benefit of their constituents: "towns boasted not only forts and victory arches but also law courts, schools, libraries, theaters, temples, piped-in water, public baths, and fountains, even sewers and central heating" (Davis 98). Roman medicine advanced greatly and assisted in public health, notably in pregnancy and childbirth, as it was "essential to the survival of the Roman Empire" (Ancient Discoveries 2 Medicine, 2005). The Mongols harnessed the wisdom of scholar-bureaucrats to help maintain prosperity (Davis 79). Marco Polo's travels were made possible under the Mongol Empire, which was more interested in outsiders than other dynasties. Marco Polo's account of the Tartar way of life stands as a testament to the positive living conditions during the rule of the Khans (Polo Ch. 47). Initially, all empires would benefit and grow the concepts of government and civilization.

Emperors, or Khans in the case of the Mongols, controlled the empires largely, though Rome stood strong for many years somewhat differently as a Republic. The Roman senate had power to ordain and largely enable the prosperity of the Romans while the soldier-despot generals vied for power and control throughout most of its existence. Representatives were more rigidly overseen as the Republic grew to an empire under Augustus (Davis 98). Emperor Diocletian split Rome's governing power again (Davis 98), only for Constantine to re-unify it before its demise (Davis 100). Though profoundly turbulent, the separation of some authority, for instance in the senate and emperor, and the turnover of singular rulers may be a reason for the longevity of Roman rule. These precedents become a common tenet of governance today.

The Persians and Romans established brilliant roads for the benefit of communication and ease of travel (Davis 90, 98), while the Mongol Empire maintained a massive system of couriers for continuous correspondence with other Khans (Davis 104). These communication lines cemented themselves as integral facets of the empires as the Romans and Mongols attempted to resolve their lurking, shared dilemma: they were too large, a facet ironically attributed to their success.

## Decline

As the Roman and Khan empires began to decline, they would at least once divide into sub-domains to find ways to manage the vastness of their conquests (Davis 100, 105). This extended the reign of the Romans substantially, at the cost of its western half. The Khans would fall to the rising Ming Dynasty, attributed not so much to overextension of their reach but more so to their loss of the taste for battle and victory. The Mongols who conquered a century before resembled the conquered more than their own ancestors and had lost their skill in battle (Davis 105). The Romans lost much of their cultural identity and the formidable effectiveness of their army. These far-flung borders required increased military and managerial assets, which often came from the convenience of the recently conquered. These armies were disloyal, lacked zeal for the Roman way, and were inadequately compensated (Davis 99).

In notable contrast, Persia buckled not to its size or lack of identity, but rather to the tenacious Alexander the Great and his indomitable will (Davis 91). It stands as the only empire of the three that failed less from within than from without. Though it could be argued that Persia's own pride was its downfall, as Alexander heralded from the Greeks: a thorn in Persia's side which it often attempted to remove, hoping to snuff Greece out of existence (Davis 55).

## What They Share

These great empires have more commonalities than differences. Common successes and failures demonstrated that governance lies within tolerance to culture, structured and firm legal systems, representation of the public, and the enabled catalysis of the people's prosperity. The loss of grit and the frontier spirit can lead to disaster, just as the lack of a unified cultural identity. There is undoubtedly much which can be critiqued about these past civilizations and their methods. Equally as definitive are the contributions left by those who conquered and instilled long periods of peace during the earliest stages of civilized human history. Their rise to power and fall from grace inform societies today about the qualms and tumultuous nature of leading a healthy, growing civilization. Strong leaders with vision, and their peoples, ultimately make impactful differences to all.
