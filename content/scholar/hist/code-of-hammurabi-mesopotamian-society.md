The Code of Hammurabi reads less like a list of rules and more like a cross-section of a society. The punishments are not interchangeable. Who you injured, and what class they belonged to, decided what you owed. Reading the code as a primary source means letting the price list do the talking.

## Social Hierarchy: Reading the Price List

There seems to be a patriarchal class social hierarchy based on the Code of Hammurabi. Men, women, plebeian status, or slave status are often noted in the punishments of violations. For instance:

> "If he destroys the eye of a plebeian or breaks the bone of a plebeian, he shall pay one mina of silver" (Hammurabi, Law 98).

> "If he destroys the eye of a man's slave or breaks a bone of a man's slave, he shall pay one-half his price" (Hammurabi, Law 199).

The following suggests the generous gap in valuation between different classes or groups of people. Clearly women were less than men, and slaves were subjected greatly by their plebeian owners.

> "If by a blow he has caused a plebian's daughter to have a miscarriage, he shall pay five shekels of silver" (Hammurabi, Law 211).

> "If he struck a freeman's female slave and has caused her to have a miscarriage, he shall pay two shekels of silver" (Hammurabi, Law 213).

The math is the message. A free man's eye costs a mina of silver. A slave's eye costs half of whatever the slave was worth. A miscarriage caused to a plebeian's daughter is worth five shekels; the same harm done to a slave is worth two. The hierarchy is not implied. It is itemized.

## Protections for Women, Inside a Patriarchal Frame

There are several protections and rights for women in the Code of Law. Women were protected, if not terribly well, from some abuses and those causing miscarriage (Hammurabi, Law 209, 211, 213). Laws also allowed women to divorce (Hammurabi, Law 142), and women had rights to own land and will them to their heirs upon death (Hammurabi, Law 155). In the case of infertility it was possible that a woman may recoup her dowry (Hammurabi, Law 138).

It is clear that the society is patriarchal because women were considered to a great degree the property of men and had very unequal representation in the entirety of the Code of Law. As a prudent example, a man may sell his wife and children for a period of time to another (Hammurabi, Law 117). The protections exist, but they exist inside a frame that treats the protected party as something a man owns and may, under the right conditions, sell.

## What the Code Lacks

It seems that there was no policing force. There is no dedicated investigation department or jury of note. There were no lawyers indicated, or anything suggesting Miranda rights of the accused. This is likely why false accusations were taken so seriously. Presumably a person could gain much and remove social competition through false accusations, since there was very little in the way of defense in place at this time. The process seemed to fall upon only a judge and witnesses (Hammurabi, Law 3-5).

The heavy emphasis on punishing false accusation is not moral squeamishness. It is structural compensation. When the only check on a charge is the witness who brought it, and the only judge is one man, the only deterrent left is to make the accuser bet their own life on being right. The code knows what it does not have, and it tries to make up for it at the front of the procedure.
