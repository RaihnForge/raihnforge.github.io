Bartolomé de las Casas's *Destruction of the Indies* is an appeal letter to the Spanish monarch, written by a friar who has seen what is being done in the king's name and decided he can no longer be quiet about it. As a primary source it is striking because the contrast is doing all the work: the people described as gentle, and the people sent to save them described as predators.

## How las Casas Sees the Two Peoples

The descriptions of the "Indians" given by las Casas are that of a very peaceful people, and the Spaniards of the New World are evil. He describes the native peoples as "Peaceable, hubble, and meek," and that they "offend no person." On the other hand, he describes the Spaniards as being like crazed animals, wolves, lions, or wildcats that haven't eaten. He remarks that they "dismember, slay, perturb, afflict, torment, and destroy the Indians by all manner of cruelty."

The framing is intentional. He is not balancing a ledger. He is reversing the expected one. The reader, sitting in Spain, expects to hear that the Spaniards are civilizing the savages. He is told the opposite, in language strong enough that it cannot be misread as a minor reform request.

## Who Was Allowed to Live, and Why

During the wars with the indigenous populations on various islands, las Casas claims that the Spanish allowed only the young boys and girls to live, though they became enslaved. The pattern is not random mercy. The Spanish allowed only the young boys and girls to live in order to abuse them and use them for labor, slaves, and subjugation to the horrors of the Spanish tyrants. They likely allowed them to live possibly long enough to procreate, to perhaps enable the supplying of future generations of slaves. Or as las Casas asserts, the goal was "to oppress them with the hardest, harshest, and most heinous bondage to which men or beasts might ever be bound into."

The age-selectivity is the tell. An army interested in conquest kills threats and spares non-threats. An army interested in extraction spares the future labor pool. The pattern in las Casas's account is the second one.

## The Real Object of the Expedition

The ultimate desire of the Spanish on the islands was to get gold. In the primary sources, las Casas describes horrors the Spanish would enact on those that did not bring them enough gold to sate their greed. Everything else in the operation, the conversions, the conquests, the bondage, is downstream of that single objective.

## Christianity as a Witness Against Itself

Conversion to Christianity was alleged as a reason for Spanish expeditions. The evidence brought by las Casas is that the Spaniards are horrible ambassadors of the Christian faith, and are indeed discouraging the natives from having any interest in converting to a religion in which the afterlife of heaven may include the torturous Spaniards. Recounted in the primary source is a priest's conversation with a native who claims "that he did not desire to go to the sky, but rather down to hell, so that he would not be where they were and would not see such cruel people."

The native's answer is the document's harshest line, because it is not las Casas's accusation. It is the verdict of the person the mission was supposedly aimed at. The entire testament is an appeal to bring justice and order to the New World, as what the Spaniards have brought was simply hell. Las Casas's case is that the Crown should care about this on its own terms, but if it will not, it should at least care that the conduct of its agents is making the stated mission impossible.
