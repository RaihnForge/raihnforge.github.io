## Course Snapshot

- **Institution.** Central Washington University.
- **Full title.** Change Ready: Technology Skills for Civic and Community Leaders.
- **Catalog description.** Develops practical productivity-software competency in Microsoft Word, Excel, Access, and PowerPoint, framed around the technology demands of civic and community leadership work. Paraphrased from the CWU course catalog.
- **Credits.** 5
- **Prerequisites.** None listed. The course is positioned as a general-education and ITAM-foundation offering open to students across majors.
- **Designation.** Core requirement in the CWU Information Technology and Administrative Management program. The course also satisfies a general-education breadth requirement in several program plans.
- **Thematic frame.** Office-suite literacy reframed as civic infrastructure. The course's stated frame is that nonprofit boards, neighborhood associations, city advisory committees, and community organizations all run on the same handful of productivity tools, and competence in those tools is a precondition for effective civic participation.

## Official Learning Outcomes

Paraphrased from CWU course-catalog and ITAM program materials. On completion, a student should be able to:

1. Produce professional documents in Microsoft Word, including structured reports, mail-merge correspondence, and accessible long-form documents with consistent styles and references.
2. Build functional Microsoft Excel workbooks that use formulas, named ranges, lookup functions, charts, and basic data analysis to support decision-making.
3. Design and query a small relational database in Microsoft Access, including tables, relationships, forms, and reports drawn from realistic civic or community datasets.
4. Develop and deliver Microsoft PowerPoint presentations that communicate clearly to a non-technical audience, using design principles appropriate for civic and community settings.
5. Select the right tool from the Office suite for a given problem rather than defaulting to the most familiar one.
6. Frame personal and team technology choices in terms of accessibility, inclusion, and the operational needs of community organizations.

## How the Course Is Usually Organized

IT 202 at CWU is taught as a hands-on skills-acquisition course paced by application-specific modules. Most sections run through the Office suite in roughly this order: Word, Excel, Access, PowerPoint. Each application gets several weeks. The dominant instructional vehicle is a Cengage MindTap or SAM-style courseware product that provides graded simulations, training tasks, and project assignments inside an autograded environment.

The civic-leadership framing shows up across the term in scenario design. Word assignments tend to be drafted as nonprofit reports, board minutes, grant cover letters, or community-meeting agendas. Excel assignments use budget data, volunteer rosters, donation records, and program-outcome tracking. Access assignments use small community-organization datasets. PowerPoint assignments are framed as community presentations, board updates, or public-comment briefings.

The capstone is the Social Innovation Project, sometimes branded Community Helping Community. The student selects a real or scenario-based community problem and produces a coordinated set of deliverables, typically a written proposal in Word, a budget or outcome workbook in Excel, a supporting database in Access, and a presentation in PowerPoint. The capstone is the course's argument that the Office suite is one integrated toolkit, not four disconnected applications.

## Major Content Blocks

### Microsoft Word

The longest single-application block. Coverage runs from baseline document hygiene (styles, headings, page setup, accessibility) through structured features that civic documents depend on. Standard topics include track changes and comments for collaborative editing, mail merge for community correspondence and event mailings, references and citations in MLA or APA format for grant-writing contexts, table of contents and cross-reference automation for long reports, and accessible-document practices including alt text and reading order. The teaching frame is that a board, a neighborhood association, or a small nonprofit lives or dies by the documents it produces.

### Microsoft Excel

The second large block and, for many students, the most career-relevant. Coverage includes formula construction with absolute and relative references, the family of lookup functions (VLOOKUP, HLOOKUP, XLOOKUP, INDEX-MATCH), logical functions (IF, IFS, AND, OR), basic statistical functions, named ranges, data validation, charting and chart design, conditional formatting, sorting and filtering, and basic PivotTables. The civic-leadership frame manifests in scenario data: program budgets, volunteer hour tracking, donor records, grant-outcome reporting, and small-scale program evaluation.

### Microsoft Access

The block where students are often meeting a relational database for the first time. Coverage stays at an introductory level. Standard topics include planning a small relational schema, building tables with appropriate field types and primary keys, defining one-to-many relationships, building forms for data entry, writing select queries and basic action queries, and producing formatted reports. Datasets are small but realistic, often a community-organization volunteer or program database. The block functions as conceptual scaffolding for later database courses in the ITAM and IT tracks.

### Microsoft PowerPoint

The shortest application block, treated as much as a communication course as a software course. Coverage includes slide-design fundamentals (signal-to-noise ratio, hierarchy, restraint), use of master slides and themes for consistency, incorporation of charts and data from Excel and Word, speaker-notes and handout production, and accessibility considerations for public-facing presentations. The civic frame shows up in scenarios: a board update, a community-meeting briefing, a grant-funder pitch.

### The Social Innovation Project

The capstone integrating deliverable. The project asks the student to select a real or near-real community problem, write a proposal in Word, support the proposal with quantitative work in Excel, organize the underlying data in Access, and present the package in PowerPoint. Grading rubrics typically weight integration as heavily as application competence: the deliverables are expected to reference each other, share a consistent visual identity, and read as one coordinated body of work rather than four parallel assignments.

## Cross-Cutting Concepts

- **Tool selection as judgment.** The course's recurring quiet lesson is that choosing the right tool for a task is itself a competence, separate from operating any single tool.
- **Accessibility as a default.** Headings, alt text, color contrast, and reading order are taught as baseline expectations across every application block, not as a specialty topic.
- **Data hygiene.** Clean inputs make clean outputs. The course makes a point of distinguishing well-structured data from formatted data and teaches the habits that keep the two separate.
- **Audience-aware communication.** Civic and community work means writing and presenting for non-technical audiences, often across generational and educational differences. The course pushes restraint and clarity over polish.
- **Reproducibility and version control at a beginner level.** File naming, document versioning, and the discipline of not destroying earlier work are introduced as professional norms.
- **The Office suite as an integrated workflow.** Excel data feeding Word reports feeding PowerPoint summaries is a workflow, not four separate tasks. The capstone exists to make that point concrete.

## Commonly Used Reference Texts

Most IT 202 sections do not assign a traditional textbook. The Cengage MindTap or SAM courseware product is the de facto required reading and lab environment. The following resources show up as supplemental or independent references for the same material.

- Various authors, *Microsoft Office 365 In Practice* series (McGraw-Hill). The standard step-by-step textbook for the Office suite at the introductory-college level.
- Various authors, *Shelly Cashman Series: Microsoft Office* (Cengage). The other dominant Office-suite textbook line, frequently paired with MindTap.
- Reynolds, Garr, *Presentation Zen.* The standard short book on presentation design, often referenced even in courses that do not formally assign it.
- Walkenbach, John, *Excel Bible.* The Excel reference of record for users moving past the introductory level.
- Lambert, Joan and Joyce Cox, *Microsoft Word Step by Step.* A Microsoft Press reference companion for Word users.
- Microsoft Learn (learn.microsoft.com), the official Microsoft training portal. Increasingly the primary reference for application-specific how-to material.

## Reading the Field as a Whole

- **Office-suite competence is a professional baseline, not a specialty.** The course's argument is that fluency in Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Access is the floor of contemporary professional life, not a separate skill set. The civic frame makes the case that it is a floor for non-paid leadership too.
- **The four applications teach four different mental models.** Word is sequential prose, Excel is relational arithmetic, Access is relational data, PowerPoint is performative communication. Switching between them is itself a cognitive workout.
- **Most professional knowledge work happens in productivity software.** The course quietly resets the question of "what is technical work" to include the documents and spreadsheets that organizations actually run on.
- **Civic infrastructure is software infrastructure.** Board packets, meeting minutes, budgets, volunteer rosters, and outreach materials are the medium through which civic organizations operate. The course treats that medium as worth teaching directly.
- **Skills-check pedagogy works for software.** Autograded simulation labs are an awkward fit for many subjects, but for office-suite skill acquisition the immediate feedback loop is genuinely effective. The course is honest about that fit.
