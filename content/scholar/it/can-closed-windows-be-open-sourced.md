Operating systems are no longer just helpful foundations for computers to be more functional. They are the digital landscapes many of us work, play, craft, and socialize inside on a daily basis. In my own case, working remotely with graphic design hobbies and keeping in touch with Veteran friends across the country, more of my time is spent in the OS environment than anywhere else. So what does it mean for individual privacy and liberty when this digital environment begins to aggressively exploit its user base's attention, time, and personal data for profit? Windows 11 is the next iteration of the most prevalent operating system, and its implementation raises real concerns about individual privacy. What alternative solutions exist, and can they be viable against Microsoft's goliath?

## Concerning Functionality

Generally an early adopter of technology, I have been hesitant to move to Windows 11 because of its in-OS advertising practices and general disrespect for user choice and privacy. Windows 10 introduced features that were alarming on release, and many were reeled back after user backlash (Kundu, 2023). Microsoft is making another run at intrusive features, harvesting from the seeds sewn of personal privacy degradation that continuously dislodge long-standing roots of norms, markets, legality, and architecture. Microsoft may find it more effective to rebuff user backlash and allow data greed to usurp integral ethics.

Simply installing the new version of Microsoft's OS requires an online account. That looks like a small step forward in user experience, but it is a large leap backward in the ability to opt out of online presence (Corden, 2021). Telemetry on freshly installed machines is clear: it points to increasingly invasive data tracking and reporting to data research management groups such as OneTrust and Scorecard. This information begins to flow in the background before the user has ever opened a web browser (Kundu, 2023).

Some say that opting out of foundational PC software is a choice, and that if there are issues one should avoid it entirely. That argument has aged into something that holds no optimism at all. To reference Lessig's modalities of influence (Lessig, 2006), it is increasingly unrealistic to be successfully integrated into society under social norms, legal, market, and architectural regulations without adopting a substantial measure of the digital domain.

Digital access is no longer an indulgent commodity; it is a necessity. From middle-class jobs to personal healthcare management and even children's education, Microsoft's software and ethical stance are deeply ingrained in the daily lives of well over a million Americans (Vailshery, 2023). The corporations that gate that access therefore have increasing influence over liberty. Privacy choices respected, or not respected, by leading tech giants like Microsoft shape the liberty of individuals.

## Possible Solutions

Microsoft is not alone. Many big tech companies continue to breach individual privacy and property rights. The jest has become truth: we no longer own our hardware and software, we simply lease it from corporations. The true payment is no longer our earned effort willingly traded. It is our attention and distraction from tasks we are trying to complete, while advertising is built directly into the Explorer interface (Crider, 2022). Our work is tracked, recorded, and delivered to whichever corporations deem it most profitable. Our attention and privacy are bought and sold without consent or appropriate knowledge.

As the grip of corporations on privacy has tightened, the foresight of liberty-lovers has spurred the designs of open-source into action. Founded by Linus Torvalds through his Linux project back in 1991, open-source has grown to be an aspect of every tech user's daily life. Open-source is a framework that promotes cooperation and innovation through transparent and communal efforts of volunteers around the world. Smartphones, coding languages, and yes, even operating systems are developed as open-source (Ivey, 2023). The freely available public coding architecture powers reliable software that underpins Google Chrome (Chromium), GitHub, WordPress, and the LibreOffice suite, an office suite with mirroring functionality to Microsoft Office.

The answer may lie in the rise of open-source, but is it a viable recourse? As liberties are trampled, the people will rise and find a way; but as is the way of things, the efforts of the public must be measured against those of corporations in the arena of competition. Americans may seem too reliant on corporate integration, but it is a tenuous relationship of utility. Technology is an ever-changing, merciless landscape where opportunity and adoption are valleys and mountains clashing close together. Open-source initiatives potentially provide the needed alternatives to predatory privacy policies through the emergence of effective and empowering options like the Linux OS.

## Alternative Viability

A prevailing game market platform may be one of the most recently highlighted examples of the necessary rise in open technologies. It is a careful dance that Valve, owner of one of the first software market platforms to emerge, conducts as it advances open-source solutions to reduce reliance on Microsoft and their closed Windows operating system. Ever since Steam originated in the late 1990s, Valve has been a visionary, future-focused company. Valve seems dedicated to righting their ship to avoid a catastrophic collision ahead.

The two companies have long had a comfortable synergy. Microsoft staked its claim and its success on operating systems and business suite software. Windows, Word, Outlook, and Teams are industry-standard tools and personal computing stalwarts (Vailshery, 2023). But technology is ever-changing, and so are the business practices of those bound to the market.

Over the last decade, Microsoft has entered the gaming market, originally as a console developer making an investment to maintain the relevance of Windows gaming through its DirectX libraries. The Xbox's success has sustained, and contemporary technology trends suggest that software and gaming will no longer be tied to hardware. Access will instead be linked to digital platforms like Microsoft's Game Pass (Dring, 2020). This shift has been sensed by Valve, and the disturbance is deep: the owners of Valve's foundational platform are fast becoming competitors rather than partners.

Valve has been making moves, though. Building gaming console systems like the Steam Machine and Steam Deck demonstrates the same near-future philosophical strategy as Microsoft: any device, but gated by software platform allegiance. While Valve has had the advantage over the last couple of decades, it is Windows that has been the operating system PC games run on.

This predicament has led Valve to emerge as one of the leading investors in open-source solutions. The investments are not in buying up software and components to build a closed empire of their own to compete with Microsoft (Wolens, 2022). Bespoke and communal efforts in open-source operating systems have long been successful at offering a usable alternative, but historically one that lacked broad support for the newest hardware and games. This is changing as Valve directly supports the open-source software developers who have been leading the way for years.

The backing and focus provided by Valve has let elements of the open-source ecosystem gain exponential ground. Support for contemporary hardware is closing fast to be comparable with Windows. Valve's Steam Deck already uses an open-source OS, KDE Plasma 5, with continuous improvements to increase viability and competence. These maneuvers may ensure their Steam platform will have a home. Just as importantly, the methodology ensures that the market remains free for any who long for alternatives in the daily digital environment we increasingly inhabit. Valve's enormous efforts edify not only themselves, but the ideals that support organizational liberties and ensure a free market where digital experience is not gated by corporate whims.

It is a long road yet for Valve, and I wager also for the important battle for digital liberty. Open-source Linux is only 1.27% of the current Steam user base against the massive 96.37% of Windows (Steam, 2023). Yet Valve, much like David against Goliath, persists in its fight and plans the continuation of its Steam Deck system. As some windows close, others must open to let the trapped free. Valve's Steam Deck and its open-source support may yet be truly viable alternatives. We may escape privacy's grim fate and emerge freer than ever.
