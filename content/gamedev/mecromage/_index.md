Mecromage is the longest creative thread in my portfolio. It began in 2009 as the flagship of an indie studio I co-founded, outlived that studio, and is now being rebuilt from the foundation up under a new framework. This page holds the whole arc — the original vision, the honest postmortem of a nine-year studio that didn't ship it, and the new approach that will.

## The Concept

The name blends "Mechromancer" and "Mage." The game sits at the intersection of mechanical and arcane: a side-scrolling Metroidvania where the protagonist navigates an interconnected world that rewards exploration and punishes complacency. Heavy armor, industrial environments, creatures that blur the line between built and born. Dark fantasy with a machine edge.

That creative vision has never been the bottleneck. The art direction, the mood, the world — those are all intact, and nine years of devlog proves it. What's been missing is the pipeline to get it across the finish line.

---

## Chapter One — Unchosen Paths (2009–2018)

**Unchosen Paths** was the studio I co-founded in 2009 with a guitarist known for Castlevania remixes. The name captured the philosophy: take roads that aren't mapped out yet. I served as producer, artist, game director, and software designer — not by choice so much as by necessity. Two-person studios require everyone to do everything.

Mecromage became the flagship. From 2013 onward, it absorbed the majority of our creative energy. Alongside it were exploratory projects — environment studies, character concepts, tool skunkworks — all of which fed the main game. The daily devlogs from that era (the "Daily UP" posts, Indie Dev Dues, the workflow essays) are preserved below as the studio's working archive.

### What Worked

- **The craft held up.** Concept art, sprite work, environment design — the visual foundation of Mecromage still looks right to me a decade later. The art direction was never the problem.
- **The problem-solving was real.** The devlog entries on UI resolution, dynamic 2D platforms, tile systems, and asset pipelines were genuine engineering work. The lessons from that era carry forward.
- **The partnership was productive.** Two people with complementary skills kept the project alive through multiple iterations.

### The Postmortem

Unchosen Paths formally wound down in 2018 without shipping Mecromage. The honest reasons, looking back:

- **Ambition outran velocity.** The scope of a Metroidvania — tileset libraries, enemy rosters, level design, animation, audio — is enormous for a two-person team doing everything from scratch. We built beautiful pieces; we couldn't assemble the whole at a sustainable pace.
- **Tooling of the era.** Every asset was hand-made, every system hand-coded. We had no force multipliers — no procedural asset generation, no AI-assisted animation, no code generation for boilerplate. Every hour produced roughly one hour's worth of output. The math on a full game at that rate never closed.
- **The Four C's gate, in hindsight.** We leapt past comfort and confidence straight to competence without ever establishing a sustainable delivery rhythm. ([The Forge Framework](/gamedev/forge-framework/) names this pattern explicitly — it's the primary failure mode of over-ambition.)
- **Process was continuous; the project was treated like one.** We never drew a clean line between *making a game* (project — has an end) and *running a studio* (process — continuous). That ambiguity blurred priorities and stretched the timeline until the studio itself ran out of runway.

Mecromage didn't die when Unchosen Paths closed. But it was clear that the same approach, repeated, would produce the same result.

## Chapter Two — The Dormant Years

From 2018 through 2023, Mecromage was worked on sporadically. Solo. The tooling hadn't changed enough to close the velocity gap. The project was too big for one person at the old pace, but the vision was too strong to abandon. So it waited.

During those years I built other things — product work, design work, the infrastructure of what would become my current studio ecosystem. The lessons from Unchosen Paths compounded. So did the tooling landscape.

---

## Chapter Three — Archkey Studio and the AI-Assisted Pipeline

The redemption path is not a new engine or a new art style. It's a new production model.

**[Archkey Studio](/gamedev/archkey/)** is the new vehicle for Mecromage — a learning-first studio structured around AI-assisted game development. The thesis is straightforward: the force multiplier that didn't exist in 2013 exists now. Code generation, procedural and AI-assisted asset workflows, automated tooling, rapid prototyping in Phaser 3 — these collapse the ratio between hours worked and output produced.

What that changes:

- **Scope becomes tractable.** Tileset variations, enemy behavior trees, level geometry iterations, animation frame-fills — work that used to consume weeks can now be explored in hours. A solo developer with AI-assisted tooling can operate at something close to small-team velocity.
- **The craft layer stays human.** AI generates drafts, variations, and boilerplate. The creative decisions — what *feels* right, what *fits* Mecromage's world, what earns a frame of animation — stay with me. The studio is explicitly structured so AI speeds execution without diluting authorship.
- **The studio itself is the learning ground.** Archkey is organized around the principle that every prototype is a study. The U2DTS retro-graphics research, the Phaser 3 experiments, the process notes — they all feed back into Mecromage's pipeline. Nothing is wasted; everything teaches.
- **The Forge Framework governs the operating model.** Project/process distinction, Four C's gating, the FORGE cycle — these aren't abstract theories. They're the rules under which Mecromage is being rebuilt, explicitly designed to avoid the failure modes of the Unchosen Paths era.

### Current Status

Active development under the Archkey pipeline. Foundational assets are being ported forward. The design doc is living and evolving. A playable vertical slice is the next tangible milestone — and for the first time in the project's history, the math on getting there actually closes.

Mecromage doesn't die. It finishes.
