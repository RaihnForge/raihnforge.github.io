## The Narwhals in the Waiting Room

I spent the day at the [Alma](https://getalma.com) studio, and I left with a stronger understanding of why the company has the reputation it does.

The first thing you notice walking in is that the waiting room is full of narwhals.

I love it.

The narwhal is one of those creatures that looks mythical, as though somebody combined a dolphin and a unicorn and somehow convinced the rest of us that it exists. If you encountered one in a fantasy setting, you would assume it had been invented by a writer looking for something whimsical and slightly absurd. Yet the narwhal is entirely real, which makes it all the more memorable.

That feels like an appropriate mascot for Alma, and I suspect that is not accidental. Their tagline is, **"Be so amazing that people think you are a myth."** On the surface, a school technology company that genuinely cares about teachers, students, and families sounds like exactly the sort of thing a marketing department would say. Most people who have spent enough time around enterprise software have learned to approach those claims with a healthy degree of skepticism.

Then you spend time with the people building it.

## What Alma Does

Alma is a cloud-based Student Information System, the operational backbone that supports much of what happens inside a school. Student records, attendance, scheduling, grading, state reporting, fee management, and communication between teachers, students, and families all run through systems like these.

The category itself sounds fairly mundane until you consider the people on the other side of those workflows. Every inefficient process becomes time taken away from a teacher. Every confusing interface becomes another obstacle for an administrator. Every unnecessary complication eventually finds its way to a student or family. Software at this level quietly shapes the daily experience of an enormous number of people.

What stands out about Alma is not simply the range of institutions they serve, which includes state agencies, districts, charter schools, independent schools, international schools, and school networks. What stands out is that their stated mission of being built for educators, by educators, continues to feel authentic after you have spoken with the team. That is a rarer quality than many organizations would like to admit.

## What the Studio Felt Like

You can learn a surprising amount about a company by walking through its studio and listening to how people talk about their work.

What struck me was not enthusiasm, because enthusiasm is easy to manufacture. What struck me was clarity.

The engineers, designers, support staff, and product teams I spoke with were able to explain exactly what problems they were solving, who those problems affected, and why they mattered. Conversations consistently stayed connected to real schools, real educators, and real students rather than drifting into abstract business language or technology for technology's sake.

The thing that left the strongest impression on me was how frequently discussions returned to the student experience. Student Information Systems naturally have to handle compliance requirements, reporting obligations, schedules, transcripts, and countless operational details, but those things are ultimately in service of something larger. Again and again, conversations seemed to circle back to a simple question: does this improve the experience of the people relying on the system every day?

When that question remains at the center of the work, it changes the character of the product.

I also appreciated the size of the team. They are accomplishing a surprising amount with a relatively small group of people, and I have always found myself drawn toward organizations that operate that way. Small teams tend to have less room for diffusion of responsibility, stronger ownership, and a closer connection between decisions and outcomes. The work either gets done or it does not, and everyone knows which is which.

## The Sticker

I also brought the team a sticker.

A friendly narwhal paired with the Alma mark and labeled "Norwahl," because every mascot deserves at least one terrible pun and because the narwhal already captures much of what makes the company memorable.

It is unusual enough that you assume it cannot possibly be real until you discover that it is.

## Why It Matters

Education software is one of the most consequential and least appreciated domains in technology.

Teachers notice when it wastes time. Families notice when it creates confusion. Students notice when it becomes an obstacle rather than a tool. Most people only think about these systems when something goes wrong, which means the best products often become invisible through reliability.

After spending time at the studio, my impression is that Alma understands the responsibility that comes with building software for schools. More importantly, the people there appear to care deeply about getting it right.

The mythical company that actually cares about educators turns out to be real.

It just happens to have a narwhal as a mascot.
