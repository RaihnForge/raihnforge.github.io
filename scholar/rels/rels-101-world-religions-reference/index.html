RELS 101 — World Religions

May 18, 2026

Course Snapshot

  • Institution. Central Washington University.
  • Catalog description. “Survey of the major world religions (Judaism, Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism, Daoism), including their tenets, practices, and evaluation of the human condition.”
  • Credits. 5
  • General-education designation. AH-Philosophies and Culture World (W); K5 — Humanities. Pathway: P6 Ways of Knowing.
  • Prerequisites. None.
  • Approach. The CWU Comparative Religion program describes itself as offering “a strong, pluralistic foundation in philosophy and religion” with notable departmental expertise in Asian philosophy and religion, so the Eastern traditions tend to receive proportionally rich treatment alongside the Western ones.

Official Learning Outcomes

Quoted from the CWU course catalog. Upon completion, a student should be able to:

  1. Describe the development of the major world religions within their contexts of origin and subsequent spread to other geographic regions.
  2. Articulate the primary tenets, practices, and symbolic systems of the major world religions.
  3. Describe the historical role of religion in shaping cultures and institutions.
  4. Articulate how religion functions in their personal, social, and historical lives and the lives of others.
  5. Recognize the diversity of manifestations of religious expressions.
  6. Develop analytical, reading, and writing skills.

How the Survey Is Usually Organized

Intro-to-world-religions courses typically run in one of two passes:

  • Geographic / historical. Western (Abrahamic) traditions first, then Eastern (Dharmic and Chinese) traditions, then comparative themes.
  • Thematic / comparative. Pick a theme (ritual, suffering, ethics, sacred text, community, the divine) and trace how each tradition handles it.

CWU’s RELS 101 reads as the geographic / historical structure based on the catalog list (Judaism → Christianity → Islam → Hinduism → Buddhism → Confucianism → Daoism), with thematic comparison threaded across.

The Seven Traditions in Outline

Judaism

  • Origins. Ancient Near East, traced to the patriarch Abraham (~1800 BCE) and the Mosaic covenant at Sinai (~1300 BCE).
  • Core tenets. Strict monotheism (one God, YHWH). Covenant relationship between God and the people of Israel. Torah as both narrative and law. Ethical conduct as central religious expression.
  • Practice. Shabbat (weekly sabbath), kashrut (dietary law), the festival cycle (Passover, Yom Kippur, Sukkot, Hanukkah, Purim), prayer three times daily, synagogue community.
  • Texts. Tanakh (Hebrew Bible: Torah, Nevi’im, Ketuvim). Talmud (rabbinic commentary, Mishnah + Gemara).
  • Symbol system. Star of David, menorah, Torah scroll, mezuzah, tallit, kippah.
  • Branches. Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, Reconstructionist; Hasidic and other movements within.

Christianity

  • Origins. First century CE, Roman-occupied Judea, around Jesus of Nazareth (~4 BCE – 30 CE). Spread through the Mediterranean via Pauline missionary activity.
  • Core tenets. Belief in Jesus as the incarnate Son of God, the Christ, whose death and resurrection redeem humanity. Trinity (Father, Son, Holy Spirit). Salvation by grace through faith (with major denominational variation on how works fit in).
  • Practice. Worship (liturgical to free-form), prayer, scripture reading, sacraments (baptism, eucharist; the count and nature vary by branch), the liturgical year (Advent → Christmas → Lent → Easter → Pentecost).
  • Texts. Christian Bible: Old Testament (largely the Hebrew Tanakh) and New Testament (Gospels, Acts, Epistles, Revelation).
  • Symbol system. Cross, fish (ichthys), dove (Holy Spirit), alpha and omega.
  • Branches. Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Oriental Orthodox, and the many Protestant traditions (Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican, Baptist, Pentecostal, Methodist, etc.).

Islam

  • Origins. Arabian peninsula, seventh century CE, around the Prophet Muhammad (570 – 632 CE), beginning with the Quranic revelation around 610 CE.
  • Core tenets. Strict monotheism (tawhid). Muhammad as the final prophet in a line that includes Adam, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. The Quran as God’s literal final revelation.
  • Practice. The Five Pillars: shahada (creed), salat (five daily prayers facing Mecca), zakat (almsgiving), sawm (fasting in Ramadan), hajj (pilgrimage to Mecca once in a lifetime if able).
  • Texts. Qur’an. Hadith (collected sayings and acts of the Prophet). Sharia (Islamic legal tradition derived from Qur’an, Hadith, ijma, and qiyas).
  • Symbol system. Star and crescent (cultural rather than canonical), Arabic calligraphy of the Qur’an, the Kaaba.
  • Branches. Sunni (~85–90% of Muslims globally), Shia (Twelver, Ismaili, Zaidi), Sufism as the mystical / inner-dimension tradition cutting across both.

Hinduism

  • Origins. South Asia, with roots in the Indus Valley civilization and the Vedic period (~1500 BCE onward). Not founded by a single figure; an aggregation of traditions that gradually came to be called “Hindu.”
  • Core tenets. Wide internal diversity, but recurring concepts: Brahman (ultimate reality), atman (self), dharma (right conduct / cosmic law), karma (action and consequence), samsara (cycle of rebirth), moksha (liberation). Many gods often understood as facets of Brahman.
  • Practice. Daily puja (worship), pilgrimage, festivals (Diwali, Holi, Navaratri), the four purusarthas (aims of life: dharma, artha, kama, moksha), the four stages of life (ashramas).
  • Texts. Vedas (four, with the Rig Veda the oldest). Upanishads (philosophical). Bhagavad Gita, Ramayana, Mahabharata (epics). Puranas.
  • Symbol system. Om (aum), swastika (in its original auspicious sense, distinct from later European political appropriation), lotus, trishula.
  • Major theological streams. Vaishnavism (Vishnu), Shaivism (Shiva), Shaktism (Devi). The six classical darshanas as philosophical schools.

Buddhism

  • Origins. Northern India, ~5th century BCE, beginning with Siddhartha Gautama, the historical Buddha.
  • Core tenets. The Four Noble Truths (suffering exists; it has a cause, craving; it can end; the end is the Eightfold Path). The Eightfold Path (right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration). The Three Marks of Existence (impermanence, suffering, non-self). The Three Jewels (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha).
  • Practice. Meditation (śamatha for calm, vipassanā for insight; Zen zazen; tantric practices), ethical precepts, monastic and lay paths.
  • Texts. Pali Canon (Tipitaka) in Theravada. Mahayana sutras (Lotus, Heart, Diamond, Pure Land, etc.). Tibetan tantras.
  • Symbol system. Dharmachakra (wheel), lotus, Bodhi tree, Buddha image, mandala, Om Mani Padme Hum.
  • Major branches. Theravada (Southeast Asia, “way of the elders”). Mahayana (East Asia, “great vehicle,” includes Pure Land and Zen / Chan). Vajrayana (Tibet, Mongolia, Himalayan; “diamond vehicle”).

Confucianism

  • Origins. China, ~6th–5th century BCE, beginning with Kongzi (Confucius, 551–479 BCE), elaborated by Mencius and Xunzi, then institutionalized as state ideology in imperial China.
  • Core tenets. Cultivation of moral character through ren (humaneness / benevolence), li (ritual propriety), yi (righteousness), zhi (wisdom), xin (integrity). Five key relationships: ruler / subject, parent / child, husband / wife, elder / younger sibling, friend / friend. Junzi (the exemplary person) as the goal of self-cultivation.
  • Practice. Education, ritual observance (especially ancestor veneration), public service, family piety (xiao).
  • Texts. The Five Classics (Yi Jing / Book of Changes, Shu Jing, Shi Jing, Spring and Autumn Annals, Li Ji). The Four Books (Analects, Mencius, Great Learning, Doctrine of the Mean) — the core curriculum after Zhu Xi’s Song-era synthesis.
  • Symbol system. Less iconographic than the others. Confucius portraiture, classical robes, ritual implements, the character 仁 (ren).
  • Position. Often discussed alongside Daoism and Buddhism as one of the “Three Teachings” (sanjiao) of Chinese culture, sometimes more philosophy than religion depending on definition.

Daoism (Taoism)

  • Origins. China, traced to Laozi (legendary, ~6th century BCE) and Zhuangzi (~4th century BCE). Later institutionalized into liturgical and monastic traditions.
  • Core tenets. Alignment with the Dao (the Way), the underlying pattern of nature. Wu wei (effortless action; non-forcing). Ziran (naturalness, spontaneity). Yin-yang complementarity, the qi (vital energy) cosmology, the Five Phases (wu xing).
  • Practice. Meditation, breath and energy work (qigong, neidan inner alchemy), ritual, talismans, dietary discipline, pursuit of longevity / immortality in some lineages.
  • Texts. Daodejing (Tao Te Ching). Zhuangzi. Daozang (Daoist Canon).
  • Symbol system. Taijitu (yin-yang), bagua (eight trigrams), trigrams of the Yijing.
  • Two strands often distinguished. Philosophical Daoism (Daojia) of the early texts and Religious Daoism (Daojiao) of the later liturgical and monastic traditions.

Cross-Cutting Concepts

Comparative work tends to keep returning to these.

  • The Sacred vs. the Profane. Following Mircea Eliade, religions partition reality into ordinary and sacred zones (time, space, action). Sacred space is where the ordinary touches something else; sacred time is participatory rather than chronological.
  • Myth. Not “false story” in religious-studies usage. A narrative that carries the worldview and the meaning structure of a tradition. Function over factual content.
  • Ritual. Patterned action that enacts a tradition’s view of reality. Often distinguished into rites of passage (van Gennep, Turner), calendrical rites, and crisis rites.
  • Symbol systems. Color, number, body, garment, building, gesture. Read the symbols and you read the cosmology.
  • Founder figures and canonization. When and how a tradition’s authoritative texts get fixed. Different traditions handle this very differently (open canon vs. closed canon, oral vs. written primacy).
  • Insider vs. outsider perspective. The classic methodological distinction between studying a tradition as a believer (theology, devotion) and as a religious-studies scholar (descriptive, comparative, suspending personal judgment).
  • The “world religions” category itself. A 19th–20th century invention shaped by colonial and Western academic interests. Useful as a starting frame; worth knowing it is a frame.

Commonly Used Reference Texts in Intro Surveys

These are the standard reference works in this domain. Any one of them is a usable companion if returning to the material.

  • Esposito, John L., World Religions Today.
  • Fisher, Mary Pat, Living Religions.
  • Smart, Ninian, The World’s Religions (and Dimensions of the Sacred).
  • Smith, Huston, The World’s Religions (the warmer, sympathetic-insider tour).
  • Prothero, Stephen, God Is Not One.
  • Pals, Daniel, Eight Theories of Religion (for theoretical / methodological grounding).

Reading the Field as a Whole

A few patterns worth holding onto:

  • Geography is destiny, mostly. Religions take the shape of the places they grew up in (climate, language, neighbors) and then re-shape themselves again as they spread. Hinduism in Bali is not Hinduism in Varanasi; Christianity in Ethiopia is not Christianity in Texas.
  • Authority structures vary more than the underlying experiences. Mystical experience, ethical concern, and ritual community recur across traditions; what differs is who gets to say what counts.
  • Modernity puts each tradition on the same shelf. Globalization makes every tradition a neighbor of every other. The modern person picks an inheritance more deliberately than any previous era could.
  • Religious illiteracy is expensive. The world’s political, ethical, and aesthetic conversations all run on religious vocabulary, whether the speakers know it or not. The course’s quietest payoff is the vocabulary itself.
