About this paper

A walk through the earliest urban experiments, from the ritual gathering ground at Gobekli Tepe to the dictatorship-in-embryo at Arslantepe to the proto-republic at Ur. The argument is that cities are a bargain: more people, more specialization, more capability, in exchange for inequality, and the cities that lasted were the ones that found a way to share enough of the surplus to keep their populations from walking away.

Why it matters to the practice

The civilization-shaped bargain (concentration produces capability and capability produces hierarchy) is the same shape as any growing organization or product team. Ur's survival recipe (a city that needs its people enough to share power with them) is the same recipe that distinguishes healthy studios from extractive ones. Useful both as worldbuilding reference and as a long-view check on how much of any system's success runs on whether the people inside it can leave.

Connections

Sets the foundation under the Empires essay — the frontier energy that builds empires is the same energy that built these first cities, and the inequality that ends empires starts here. Also feeds the Atlas worldbuilding work, where any believable settlement needs a credible answer to the question of why the population stays.

The story of the first cities is the story of the bargain humanity struck when it stopped moving. The bargain is a real one, with real costs, and the early sites preserve the moment-by-moment record of who paid and who collected.

Gobekli Tepe: A Meeting Place Before a City

The ancient city of Gobekli Tepe is thought to be a gathering place of hunters and gatherers in order to conduct rituals and feasts. It was important for migratory groups to meet occasionally in order to exchange information and goods, as well as to find marriage partners to broaden the gene pool.

To mark their meeting places, sculptures with art were constructed at Gobekli. The large T-shaped pillars displayed not just the expected animal sculptures but were themselves shaped uniquely as anthropomorphic human representations. They are thought to be depicting ancestors. The monuments were constructed in a social environment during feasts, which importantly notes the desire for cooperation and socialization of people. Gobekli is not yet a city. It is the rehearsal.

How Agriculture Bound People to Place

There is a type of wild grass that grows only in the region near Gobekli Tepe. The people there began to cultivate and selectively replant the grass known as wheat. In this way they began to domesticate wheat, and agriculture emerged. Farming is essential for civilization and is staple food even today. To farm, a group needs to tend to the field on a year-round basis. Because of this, the nomadic way of life had to be sacrificed.

Asikli Huyuk is one of the oldest farming communities in the world. The houses there were typically entered from above, where we would usually expect just a roof, and the residents would bury their dead in a fetal position under the floors of their homes. Asikli Huyuk shows evidence that it was, in fact, an egalitarian society, where everything was very equal and shared among all.

The agricultural diet, however, was a downgrade in some ways. Hunter-gatherer diets of animal meats, nuts, and berries were very different from those of farmers. Agricultural diets are high in starches, carbohydrates, and sugars that cause very bad dental hygiene. The average height of farmers dropped by five inches to about 5'3". Additionally, the toil of farmers was so great that their back bones would fuse, and arthritis began to be prominent.

So why did farming win? Farmers have more children, because they are settled and don’t need to worry about toting children along. This population growth meant farming spread as well, as farmers doubled the production of hunter-gatherers. This led to a focus on communities and settlements. The bargain was bad for the individual body and good for the population, and populations are what propagate.

The Road to Civilization, and to Inequality

Humans became specialists. Specializing is what cities and civilization are good at. The greater the populations, the more focused specializations become. But though there are many benefits, specializations create differences, and division. Different jobs, classes, locations, and the ownership of land and food are divided. This causes inequality, as some become more rich and powerful than others. Power concentrates to the ruling elite.

Arslantepe: An Embryonic Government, and a Revolt

In Arslantepe, central institutions controlled by the elite owned the means of production. The video calls them the elite, which asserts status over the population. This sounds very similar to a government, but since the video did not specifically state this, I might assume this could also be considered something else, perhaps a cult.

This question is illusive. I scoured the transcript. It never calls the institutions a “government,” but alludes to it being an embryonic form of dictatorship, which might be the beginnings of a government. Obviously, dictatorship is a governance method, but “embryonic” means that it has not actually been born yet, and thus may not be considered an actual government. As I continue further into the video, it avoids again calling the structure of power a government, but a “system.” The video seems to be intent on not using the word government.

Upon further research, Mesopotamia seems to post-date Arslantepe, as it was thought to take form in 4000 BCE. The first government is considered to be in Egypt in 3000 BC. Arslantepe’s temple is considered to be dated at 5,500 years old. It predates the first government. Yet even the definition of government is “the governing body of a nation, state, or community.” So could the elite at Arslantepe be a government? If that is true, then why is Egypt, and not Arslantepe, considered the first government? The answer hinges on whether “government” requires a recognized state apparatus or merely a recognized concentration of power, and the historiographic tradition keeps the term reserved for the former.

The black marks on the buildings at Arslantepe are burn marks from fire damage. This suggests that the ruling class faced a violent revolt from the commoners that were oppressed. The first concentration of power produced the first uprising against concentrated power. The two arrive together.

Ur: The First City That Got the Bargain Right

The first time a city got the integration “formula” right was at Ur, in Mesopotamia in modern-day Iraq. The population in Ur was largely middle class with greater equality. This meant that Ur had a say in the running of the city, despite its having a King. Ur needed people to be there for the city to be successful. Canals were labor-intensive, and keeping people happy was essential to keeping them from leaving. This meant sharing more with others.

The Ur recipe is the inverse of the Arslantepe recipe. Arslantepe extracted until the people revolted. Ur shared enough that the people stayed. The difference is not moral; it is structural. Ur’s elites needed the labor more than they could replace it, so the labor had a voice.

What Mesopotamia Gave Forward

Your morning cereal is brought to you by southern Mesopotamia: Mesopotamia invented large-scale wheat production, as well as the potter’s wheel. The first standardized measurements in Mesopotamia were based on the anatomy of the world’s first ruler, Mesopotamia’s King. The first standardized measurement was from elbow crease to the tip of the king’s finger. The second is the king’s arm span. The system of using figures to divide up numbers was a base-60 system of mathematics. It is seen in our 60-second minute, 60-minute hour, and two 12-hour periods of the day.

The bargain of the first cities, the trade of nomadic health for settled capability, the trade of equality for specialization, ran forward through every measurement system, every clock, every loaf of bread. The terms of that first deal are still in the contracts we live inside.
