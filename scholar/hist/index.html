HIST

History

HIST coursework gives me the long view. The patterns of organization, conflict, exchange, and meaning-making that show up across centuries are the same patterns that show up inside a studio, a product team, or a creative practice. The history work feeds the worldbuilding side of my creative output and tempers the contemporary-tech-first instincts of the rest of the curriculum.

  1. HIST 101 Empires Short Essay Final Jul 29, 2021

    Persian, Rome, and Mongol Empire Compare and Contrast

    A compare-and-contrast essay across three of history's longest-shadowed empires: Achaemenid Persia, Rome, and the Mongol Khanate. The argument is that despite very different fates, all three were built on the same three legs: rugged frontier origins, exceptional leaders, and governance models that bought stretches of real peace before they failed.

  2. HIST 101 PSS05 Jul 23, 2021

    Destruction of the Indies — las Casas and the Gap Between Profession and Practice

    A primary-source study of Bartolomé de las Casas's account of Spanish conduct in the New World. The argument is simple and brutal: the stated mission was conversion and civilization, the actual mission was gold and subjugation, and the gap between the two is the whole document.

  3. HIST 101 V&W02 Jul 2, 2021

    First Cities — From Gobekli Tepe to Ur, the Bargain of Civilization

    A walk through the earliest urban experiments, from the ritual gathering ground at Gobekli Tepe to the dictatorship-in-embryo at Arslantepe to the proto-republic at Ur. The argument is that cities are a bargain: more people, more specialization, more capability, in exchange for inequality, and the cities that lasted were the ones that found a way to share enough of the surplus to keep their populations from walking away.

  4. HIST 101 PSS01 Jun 26, 2021

    Code of Hammurabi — Reading a Mesopotamian Society Through Its Laws

    A primary-source study of the Code of Hammurabi, treating the law text itself as a window into how Mesopotamia organized class, gender, and the absence of state apparatus we now take for granted. The argument is that the punishments map the hierarchy: who counts, who half-counts, and who is property.
