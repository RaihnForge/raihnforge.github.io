About this paper

Winter 2024 capstone-internship reflection covering my first quarter at Kelvin Education, Inc. as the project manager embedded with the development and administration teams. Nine sections walk learning objectives, organization, skills, field-experience benefits, self-analysis, advice, suggested employer improvements, knowledge gained, and conflict resolution. This is the predecessor paper to IT 490B: the place where I first put Agile, Jira, SOC 2 Type 2, and remote leadership through real production, and where I first started naming the patterns (space and grace, double-loop learning, servant leadership) that 490B then refined into a working framework.

Why it matters to the practice

This is the paper where the gap between classroom project management and live software project management becomes visible. The honest admissions matter, that strict scrum points distort behavior, that I am drawn to producer or product-management work more than implementation accountability, that my biggest weakness is self-care, that the program under-prepared me on industry-standard tooling like Jira. Those calls are the seed of every choice 490B later validated: build systems people actually use, protect creative bandwidth, lead through clarity rather than ceremony.

Connections

Sets up the IT 490B reflection directly. The frameworks I lean on by 490B (ETTO, triple constraint, Ikigai) are not yet named here; instead the early-stage vocabulary is double-loop learning, servant leadership, and space and grace from CRO Craig Tuschhoff. ADMG 479 (Agile Project Management) shows up as the formal training I was actively re-shaping in practice; the SOC 2 Type 2 work cross-references the compliance and risk lens later used in RMT 340 and ADMG 285. The conflict-resolution section also previews the relational repair and remote-team communication themes that recur across the program.

1. Learning Objectives

My first learning objective was to gain experience as an Agile project-management scrum master for the development team. Though I assist in prioritization and help unblock the development team throughout sprints, our team does not ascribe to strict scrum implementation. I also prefer to avoid the scrum points system, as it creates a distraction and an alluring incentive for team members to prioritize the gamified system over the actual applied outcomes. My objective was modified to increase confidence and organization during developer daily standups and bi-weekly meetings. I incorporated weekly one-on-one meetings with individual developers in order to assess and assist with progress and prioritization.

My second objective was to use Jira to manage issues, stories, and Epics. I have learned a great deal through this experience regarding Jira and its live implementation. Jira is a flexible, robust ticketing system that allows for extensive accountability and communication. I developed several projects aligned with organizational and functional roles throughout the company, and we are successfully using Jira with the entire development team. Its external integrations to GitHub, Slack, and Google Workspace impress me.

My final objective was to interface with stakeholders to remove project and task blockers. This has been my primary function at the company and has been challenging, given the breadth of tasks across multiple departments in the organization. I enjoy helping my team find solutions and reduce their stress by applying organization, structure, communication, and expectation-setting techniques.

2. Organization Description

Kelvin Education, Inc. specializes in web-based SaaS applications designed for educational institutions and school districts. Our flagship web application, Pulse, is a comprehensive platform that efficiently administers and reports on surveys conducted among students, families, and school staff. Pulse fosters wellness and wholeness, promoting Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) and enhancing school climate.

Established in 2018, Kelvin is an Irvine, California-based company dedicated to serving the Education Technology industry. The organization is run by 10-25 employees servicing over 800,000 students, mainly in California but with clients nationwide.

Kelvin Education strives to be the servant leaders people turn to for remarkable solutions to create a kinder world. Our mission is to create tools people love using to achieve success. Kelvin’s solutions are born from a mindful blend of passion and action. Through expert guidance, humble collaboration, and the implementation of cutting-edge technology, we work to create transformative solutions that foster happiness and well-being.

As a project manager, I lead our SOC 2 Type 2 certification efforts, a website redesign project, and various developer projects and initiatives. I assist in monitoring, control, and communications while constantly applying creative problem-solving skills. I have many other administrative, security, and marketing responsibilities, but my primary unit is the development team, followed by the administration team. As a team, the developers and I complement each other’s skills that drive support for the product and services while conducting research-and-development actions for potential product enhancements and new products. In the administration team, more skills overlap with less variation, but the variations are used to assist each other in growing and strengthening weaknesses. The Admin team experience is much more linear, while the Dev team experience is more creative, collaborative, and reactive.

3. Skills and Competencies Developed

This experience has afforded me the opportunity to enhance a full range of skills. I have learned how to approach and conduct vendor reviews, state compliance, and administrative HR tasks, including onboarding and training design and implementation. I am learning technical skills, including Jira Software, Google Workspace, GitHub, and SecureFrame. I am sharpening my leadership skills by running many meetings and organizing teams and tasks to align with the organization’s mission. Through the remote environment, I am also gaining new remote-specific strategies and soft skills in order to increase my wellness and understanding.

4. Benefits of the Field Experience

The benefits of the field experience have assisted me in coming to many realizations. I realized that despite the benefits of the remote environment, I can struggle with a lack of socialization and team-environment conceptualizations. I have adopted strategies and check-ins with fellow team members who have similar challenges, and we share support and techniques to acquire the missing information that would be present in a traditional work environment.

I have also learned that I am less excited about Project Management as a vocation and more interested in producer, director, operations, or product-management roles. I find I am less drawn to the implementation-accountability side of project management and more drawn to being one of the team members driving direction, strategy, and creative approaches to solutions across a wider gamut of applications.

5. Self-Analysis

My working experience has greatly challenged and improved me. I am surrounded by a mentally well and wellness-oriented team. That affords and challenges me to be introspective and to constantly use double-loop learning for personal growth. It is clear that my greatest weakness at the moment is in the domain of self-care. With the experience I have gained and the generous mentorship from my work family at Kelvin, I am confident that I have and will continue to gain greater health.

I plan to pursue UX/UI Google certification to be more effective in my discussions and design contributions for the research-and-development teams. I have a good sense of this subject matter and a lot of experience in implementation, but I lack the academic vocabulary that would allow better communication and standardization of concepts, making them more comparable across the industry.

6. Advice

The best piece of advice I have received is to allow space and grace. My life experience has taught me the sharpness of competition in very unforgiving environments. That has made professional aspirations wrought with stress and unhealthy cadences. Craig, our CRO, continuously reminds me, “Space and grace, man.” What I have realized is that this is not a selfish quest to be more comfortable but an exercise in patience and understanding that yields higher quality and longer-lasting results.

For those planning their internship, I encourage you to be very deliberate about the process and carefully guard your time. Be mindful of your learning objectives and orient your experience toward what will most edify you. The most important thing you will learn is whether or not your passions align with your chosen vocation or training. Reflect on your weekly assignments authentically and plan for adjustments in your goals and career development.

7. Suggested Employer Improvements

The company is growing at an awesome rate, and with more success can come more financial security. I would implore that we be mindful of our cadences and expectations of individuals throughout the company to ensure workforce health and, by extension, organizational security. Beyond surface-level interaction, conversations with many other employees reveal a brimming exhaustion at the pace of work, driven by ever-increasing self-imposed expectations of required problem solving, flexible reactions, and the innate and considerable outpouring of personal investment for sponsors and stakeholders alike. Without concerted recovery initiatives in place, and continuous space-and-grace reminders, friction, mistakes, and disillusionment may cause a slow underlying degradation of organizational mission and values.

As we grow, growing pains are inevitable, and change will always be a constant part of our organizational culture. These need to be directly confronted and addressed consistently. We have many, if not all, team members that strive for high-performance results. Delegating authority while maintaining responsibility and accountability is the key to healthy, high-performance team consistency over time. Considerations of consistency, expectation-setting, and deliberate, expressed in-scope and out-of-scope work will continue to produce impressive outcomes.

To be clear, these are not inevitabilities, and the company is addressing these potentialities deliberately and in good faith. As a wellness-culture-climate company, we have no lack of resources, incentives, or aspirations for only the best for everyone, as outlined in our company values, ethics, mission, and culture.

8. Knowledge Gained

My greatest knowledge gained would be the implementation of project management in the live information-technology software-company industry. It is a flexible, creative space requiring bespoke approaches that belie many traditional teachings. While traditional waterfall and Agile methods are implemented on various levels, unless you are working with one of the big tech companies, you will more likely need to be adaptive and flexible in the implementation and practices of the art of project management. I am most disappointed in my lack of preparation in terms of the software systems I would be encountering. Jira is the industry standard. There are others, but they are more closely related to Jira than to Microsoft Project. Exposure and practice in industry-standard systems would have been beneficial.

I can appreciate the fundamentals taught through traditional project-management techniques and am glad to have learned them as a foundation. However, more could be incorporated to identify the pivot points in those systems as they relate to ITAM. Software options similarly provide a foundation of learning and historical understanding to a point, but at least an introduction and exposure to industry-standard contemporary applications would have been appreciated.

9. Conflict Resolution

During a very high-stress week, and an even more stressful day where many requests overlapped and deadlines closed in, a colleague of mine responded through Slack to work I had turned in with feedback that seemed very inappropriate to me. This was confusing because this person is normally very considerate and mindful, so it shocked me at a moment when I was already experiencing a great amount of biological stress reactions. I countered professionally but not overly sensitively in the moment, and we both ended up not happy with the interaction and the miscommunication.

I reached out to another colleague, who has been a pillar of support for my issues, and she simply told me I needed to take a moment, go on a walk, and listen to some music I liked. That was very helpful. I was able to apologize and explain my overreaction immediately. My colleague also apologized, as they had been in a similarly stressful state with high stakes. The frustration was immediately assuaged through text messages. We waited for our weekly one-on-one meeting to address any remaining concerns.

We both came to the table humble, naming where we had personally gone wrong while also being able to stand up for what we believed could have gone better. I am privileged, and I have grown in trust with all of my colleagues, as they have been supportive and recognize that we are all humans who can be affected by multiple stimuli. We genuinely appreciate that, especially in the remote work environment, where being heard can be extremely challenging.

The outstanding issue was miscommunication in our individual moments of stress, and neither of us felt we had the time for active listening to take place. Once we both took a moment to recenter and apply double-loop learning, we were able to communicate more comprehensibly and reflect on the communication errors that took place on both sides.
