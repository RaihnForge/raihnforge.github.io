About this paper

The IT 165 final paper. It frames data science and Big Data as a chain of responsibility shared by three groups (producers, communicators, and audiences), surveys the failure modes that have produced the reproducibility crisis, and proposes concrete improvements at each stage: PPDAC discipline for producers, third party accreditation for communicators, and critical thinking and emotional intelligence education for audiences.

Why it matters to the practice

The pressure points the paper names show up in my professional life constantly. EdTech survey rollups, broadcast audience analytics, and product dashboards all live or die on whether the numbers in front of a stakeholder actually represent reality. The framework I built here is the same one I reach for now when a chart is asking me to draw a conclusion I'm not sure the data supports.

IT 165 · Seeing Through the Data

Concerning Data: Ethics, Big Data, and the Three Groups Who Shape What We Believe

Author
Joshua Keyes
Date
May 30, 2021
Module
M9
Editor
Claude (Opus 4.7)

Data Science and Big Data are growing rapidly, and their impact on modern technology now operates on a global scale. From watching the news, to tracking viruses like COVID-19, to gathering information for our entertainment, data science and Big Data have a huge role in everyday life. The power of information impresses the necessity for ethical application. Producers, communicators, and audiences must be proactive in order to mature toward greater values and higher standards.

It would be challenging to understate the importance of data. It helps us understand the world around us, and helps society and individuals make better choices for what will hopefully be a brighter, more productive future. Data by itself is not necessarily significant or impactful. It needs to be produced in order to find identifiers and meaning before it becomes useful information. From information we find knowledge that may be considered and forged into wisdom (Bellinger et al., 2004). Understanding data enables informed, empowered, meaningful action (iScoop, 2021).

According to SAS Insights, Big Data refers to data so large, fast, or complex that it is nearly impossible to process using traditional methods (SAS Insights, 2021). The information is gathered primarily through online interactions and devices connected to the internet. It is information about everyone and nearly everything, and it is growing. In just two years from 2016 to 2018, the world gained 90 percent more data (Miller, 2018). Statista estimates that in 2021 Big Data will be a 64 billion dollar market, and by 2027 it will be 103 billion dollars (Statista, 2021).

Big Data is collected through a myriad of venues. Social media, consumer marketplaces, and smart personal and home devices are some examples (Miller, 2018). The possible applications are nearly as endless. Commercial targeting and understanding of customers, healthcare, scientific research, security, and city planning are areas that are benefiting from the data revolution (Marr, 2021). Despite all of the positives that data can bring, there are complications and drawbacks that must be considered throughout collection.

Challenges

Appropriate application of data science is complicated and emergent. The use of Big Data in politics may potentially have equal negative and positive effects. Optimization in campaign strategies is potentially widening the divide between Americans and discouraging policy makers from progressive compromise (Illing, 2017). Taking responsibility and incorporating smart use of data, while holding those who wield information accountable, is essential to progressing responsible data applications.

There are many ways data analysis can go wrong, and it is important to understand the process of information flowing from the source to the public. Three key groups carry the chain. Producers of statistics collect, study, and analyze the data. Communicators frame studies and reports in a narrative fashion. The audience receives the narrative that has been formed from the studies, and may include the general public, policy makers, or professionals (Spiegelhalter, 2019, p. 362).

The part each group plays in statistics can be motivated by the others. There is pressure on scientists to publish findings that are significant to journals that can in turn provide stories the media may monetize by attracting audiences. This can turn scientific findings away from determining discovery via the sizes of estimated effects and more toward statistically significant findings (Spiegelhalter, 2019, p. 342).

Often data is deliberately skewed toward finding certain outcomes. This includes p-hacking, where researchers run multiple tests and report only the ones found to be significant. The design of studies and tests has many variables, including sample sizes, outcome measure focus, and which groups to emphasize. These aspects fall into researcher’s degrees of freedom (Spiegelhalter, 2019, p. 350). There are generally two important classifications for studies, exploratory and confirmatory. Many of the problems arise when exploratory freedoms, such as HARKing, Hypothesizing After the Results are Known (Kerr, 1998), are implemented in confirmatory studies (Spiegelhalter, 2019).

These problems have led to what is known as the Crisis in Reproducibility. The crisis came after Daryl Bem published a paper on the likely prospect of ESP. The questioning of Bem’s paper by the scientific community led to a greater questioning of statistical science practices. That in turn led to a more critical look at how even deliberate fraud may be carried out utilizing statistical tools (Spiegelhalter, 2019).

Necessary Steps Forward

Each of the groups bears responsibilities in ethical data practices. Producers need transparent methods so that their data may be reproducible. Communicators must attempt to be responsible when fitting findings into a narrative format, which is rarely an easy task given that data is rarely given to provide definitive conclusions. Audiences must hold producers and communicators accountable and be vocal and critical about the information they are intended to consume (Spiegelhalter, 2019). Ultimately, to improve the current situation we must raise standards in each of the three core conceptual groups.

Producer’s Recommendation

Spiegelhalter (2019) posits the importance of a problem-driven approach that broadens statistical analysis from just statistical tools into a complete cycle of investigation. I agree, and would recommend that producers adhere closely to the PPDAC structure for analysis integrity. The cycle name is an acronym for its five stages: Problem, Plan, Data, Analysis, and Conclusion (Spiegelhalter, 2019). It engenders critical thinking at each step, and focuses on transparency, reproducibility, and the framing and communication of results.

All studies should be held to the PPDAC standard, in order to allow reproducibility and validation to occur. Findings should be transparent and accessible so that they may be challenged by others to weed out potential bias or fraud. A strong scientific community, supported by communicators and an audience that includes policy makers, would hold producers to the standards of the PPDAC cycle. That accountability would empower findings and allow greater trust in actual discoveries, rather than the propagation of merely significant ones.

Communicator’s Recommendation

In the mass media information age, responsibility for accuracy in data presentation cannot be left to the moguls themselves. To address ethics failings within the communicators group, I propose accreditation reviews for journalistic integrity, established through governmental and third party endorsements. This would hold communicators to a higher degree of integrity and would also allow for the generation of clout and the obtaining of notable outstanding reputations.

Third party non-profit certifications and vested governmental authentications should be standardized in the same way secure websites or seals are. The mission would be similar to FactCheck.org’s (2021), in being an advocate for the public to reduce deception and confusion for the audience. The desire to use data to generate news articles through AI solutions (Stone, 2014) could be stemmed or appropriately marked through the same organizations. Recognizing and respecting the fallacies and ethical recommendations noted in Spiegelhalter’s (2019) text could assist these organizations in holding communicators accountable for ethical journalism and in enriching audiences.

Audience Recommendation

We must increase the audience’s accessibility to critical thinking and emotional intelligence programs. For audiences to hold communicators accountable, we must impress and provide the tools for diligent critical thinking in our societies, especially in response to the mass information age. Improving early education to include emotional intelligence skills and awareness would be extremely helpful. Offering more online seminars and interactive exercises would also be beneficial. Policy makers and community leaders should help encourage and implement programs that instill critical thinking skills and raise emotional intelligence.

With improved critical thinking skills, individuals are better equipped to translate their emotions into actions (Clinicians Corner, 2016). Knowing one’s self is a way of understanding how our emotions might be manipulated, especially regarding social media and the provocation of mob mentality. Reflective critical thinking education is beneficial to the self as well as the society they live in (Hitchcock, 2020), and could help encourage intelligent discourse while maintaining accountability for those communicating information.

Conclusion

Integrity and ethics in data science are essential for positive social progression. Producers must be held accountable and adhere to the PPDAC structure for analysis and transparency for validation through reproducibility. Communicators must find better methods of ensuring accurate information through third party accreditation reviews for journalistic integrity. Audiences must be afforded better programs designed to empower critical thinking and emotional intelligence in order to gain awareness and hold communicators and producers accountable.

Data science is an ever growing field that will continue to have enormous impacts on the future of our world. There are many amazing findings and helpful tools to utilize, but it is important to maintain high values and ethics to prevent misuse and fraudulent findings from causing malign outcomes in society. Amnesty.org (2016) wields analytics to garner understanding from audiences and to impact global and national perceptions in order to help provide free and healthy futures for refugees. By being proactive in understanding the methods for holding Data Science and Big Data more accountable, our chances of having a more successful and brighter future rise significantly.
