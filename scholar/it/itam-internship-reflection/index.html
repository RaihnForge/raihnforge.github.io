About this paper

Capstone internship reflection covering Spring 2025 work at an EdTech employer as Lead Production Designer and acting Product Manager. Eleven sections walk learning objectives, organization, skills developed, field-experience benefits, self-analysis, advice received, AI as a tool, career development, problem solving, knowledge gained, and conclusion. Built on top of foundational experience from ITAM 490A and anchored in three frameworks I now use to actually run work: the project-management triple constraint, the ETTO principle, and Ikigai.

Why it matters to the practice

This is the most consequential paper of the ITAM track because it is the one where the theory hits real production. The decision points described — pausing a delivery to rebuild templates, refusing to overcommit across teams, adopting AI selectively for early-stage creative work without surrendering creative judgment — are the same decision points I keep arriving at in every studio and product role. Naming the frameworks I lean on (ETTO, triple constraint, Ikigai) makes them transferable to the next role.

Connections

Closes the ITAM degree's loop: ADMG 479 (Agile Project Management) gave me the Scrum vocabulary, ADMG 285 (Sustainable Decision-Making) gave me the systems-thinking and life-cycle lens, RMT 340 (Influencing Customer Decisions) gave me the trust-and-communication frame, and IT 229 gave me the workstation literacy beneath it. The internship was where all of those got tested at once on real production. Bridges directly into the studio practice and the Forge Framework manuscript, where the same principles get extracted into reusable tools.

A note on role and title

Although my official title during this internship was Lead Production Designer, I operated as a Product Manager and was treated as such by both peers and leadership. The titling was a mutually understood compensation-banding decision, not a reflection of scope or authority. Where the original paper named the role as filed, this archive keeps the operational reality alongside it.

1. Learning Objectives

My Spring 2025 internship at an EdTech employer focused on three core learning objectives that guided my development as a systems-oriented project manager and visual communicator. Building on foundational experiences from ITAM 490A, I sought to translate theory into practice by enhancing tool proficiency, streamlining operational workflows, and elevating cross-team communication through visual storytelling.

Objective 1 — Become Proficient with Jira

I deepened my knowledge of Jira by managing sprint cycles, grooming backlogs, and setting up project-specific dashboards. These systems were implemented across both the internal content-system product pipeline and the Upper Elementary microlesson content initiative. I structured Epics and Stories to reflect evolving production workflows, enabling greater task traceability and faster throughput. Dashboards became key tools in daily team syncs, reinforcing accountability and aligning output with strategic objectives.

Objective 2 — Automate Slack-to-Jira Task Integration

To minimize manual task creation and reduce context switching during production sprints, I helped build and test Slack-to-Jira automation flows using Atlassian and native integrations. This automation enabled real-time Slack conversations to generate actionable, trackable Jira tickets, reducing delays and ensuring consistent follow-through in feedback cycles.

Objective 3 — Communicate Project Status Visually

Using Canva, I created visual communication assets that summarized production timelines, stakeholder feedback, and delivery milestones. These visuals enhanced clarity during internal check-ins, executive reviews, and planning sessions. This approach reflected lessons from 490A, where communication gaps often contributed to rework. By contrast, the visual briefings I developed in 490B improved team alignment and stakeholder engagement.

Together, these objectives helped me evolve from a task executor into a systems-level contributor, applying frameworks such as the ETTO principle that help to communicate balance in execution (Hollnagel, 2009) and project management’s triple constraint to balance efficiency, quality, and resource utilization.

2. Organization Description

The employer is a California-based EdTech company founded in 2018 that develops web-based SaaS tools for school districts, with a focus on school climate and social-emotional learning (SEL). Its flagship survey platform helps schools gather feedback through stakeholder surveys to support engagement, well-being, and equity.

The company’s mission to “create tools that people love to use to achieve success” is reflected in its collaborative culture and rapid, user-centered development. The organization encourages autonomy, creative problem-solving, and thoughtful iteration across teams.

During my internship, I served as Lead Production Designer while operating as Product Manager in practice. My work focused on two core initiatives: the Microlesson Content suite and the company’s internal content control system. In this dual role, I had multiple vectors of responsibility. I managed visual standards and updated brand elements. The production of animated content using CreateStudio 3 and RISE 360 constitutes a significant portion of my product contributions. Coordinating cross-functional communication and project pacing for the internal content system and content implementations, as well as company branding management, were assignments I enjoyed. Ultimately, contributing to planning documentation for leadership and team project progression was a rewarding endeavor.

The company’s dynamic structure allowed me to engage with leadership, including our CTO and our COO/CRO. These weekly syncs helped connect production-level insight to strategic goals.

The company’s culture provided me with the space to improve systems, design processes, and directly contribute to meaningful product outcomes. It also served as a proving ground for applying the frameworks and leadership principles I’ve developed through the ITAM program.

3. Skills and Competencies Developed

This internship significantly advanced both my technical capabilities and leadership competencies. Serving as a Lead Production Designer in a fast-paced edtech environment required continual learning, adaptability, and systems thinking. Three areas were critical to my development.

Project Management and Systems Thinking

Managing sprint cycles, conducting backlog grooming, and designing Jira dashboards helped me prioritize tasks and maintain production flow. I applied the project management triple constraint to balance time, scope, and quality, while also using the ETTO principle to guide when deeper attention was needed for long-term outcomes. These frameworks helped me pace projects more strategically and build systems that were both resilient and scalable.

Visual Communication and Design Systems

Using Canva, CreateStudio 3, and Articulate RISE 360, I created branded graphics, animation assets, and instructional visuals to support both learner engagement and stakeholder planning. My design systems work included building reusable templates, visual dashboards, and presentation assets that clarified timelines and product decisions. These visuals supported alignment across engineering, content, and leadership.

Collaborative Leadership and Strategic Communication

My role frequently placed me between departments and required strong communication across creative and technical domains. I became a bridge between design, engineering, and operations. I also contributed to executive meetings where I presented visual updates, discussed delivery planning, and advocated for process improvements. These experiences built my confidence as a communicator and helped me see leadership as both influence and integration.

Together, these skills strengthened my ability to lead through clarity, systems design, and purposeful collaboration.

4. Benefits of the Field Experience

This internship offered more than technical growth. It allowed me to apply, test, and evolve my thinking in a real-time, high-stakes environment. Through my work on the Microlesson Content project and the internal content system planning, I gained a full view of project ownership, from early ideation to final delivery.

Working across departments helped me develop agility and initiative, especially when priorities shifted or feedback cycles intensified. Tight production deadlines taught me to manage ambiguity while preserving quality. These moments prompted me to appreciate the importance of pacing and effective communication, particularly in remote settings where alignment relies on clarity.

One challenge involved being assigned to multiple teams with competing needs. Instead of overcommitting, I used sprint data and deliverable timing to advocate for more sustainable planning. This taught me that protecting creative bandwidth is part of leadership.

I also explored ways to improve project tracking. While I initially tested Slack-to-Jira automation, some collaborators preferred manual tracking methods. Rather than force a transition, I adapted workflows to match the team’s pace. This reinforced the importance of designing practical systems, not just ideal ones.

Beyond tools and outputs, the most lasting benefit was how this experience reshaped my understanding of value. I began the internship focused on producing. I finished with a clearer view of what it means to contribute, through systems, strategy, and relationships.

5. Self-Analysis

This internship helped me move from contributor to systems-minded leader by showing how creative, technical, and strategic skills come together under pressure. Working across both the the internal content system initiative and the Microlesson Content project, I faced challenges that required more than execution. They required prioritization, reflection, and leadership. One of the most challenging parts of the experience as a cross-functional team member (Atlassian, n.d.) was balancing expectations across multiple teams. I was often pulled between content production and systems coordination, which forced me to manage my time, energy, and communication more intentionally. By referencing sprint data and deliverable history, I learned to advocate for realistic timelines and protect project integrity.

The ETTO principle shaped the shift from rapid delivery to sustainable planning. When our microlesson prototype evolved into a full content suite, I paused production to rebuild visual templates and handoff systems. This choice delayed early delivery but reduced rework and improved long-term consistency.

Applying the Ikigai framework (Government of Japan, 2022) gave me confidence that I was aligning my role with both my purpose and my strengths. I found that my real value was not in doing more, but in helping the system run more clearly. That meant building workflows others could follow, translating feedback into design, and guiding projects through complexity with clarity and empathy. This experience helped me see leadership not as a title but as the ability to create structure, communicate value, and protect focus in complex environments.

6. Advice

Two pieces of advice shaped my internship more than anything else. The first came from our Chief Technology Officer: “Make it work. Make it right. Make it efficient.” This principle taught me to focus on functionality first, refine over time, and resist the trap of early perfectionism. I used it often during the microlesson production cycle. Early drafts did not need to be flawless. They needed to move forward. By delivering value early and refining through feedback, I was able to sustain momentum and reduce creative burnout.

The second came from our Chief Operating Officer: “We should strive not for a full day, but for a fulfilled day.” This changed how I managed my time across competing projects, such as the internal content system and content production. Instead of trying to do everything, I focused on the work that mattered most to the team and where my contribution had a lasting impact.

For future interns, I would advise starting with progress, not polish. Perfect comes later. Ask why, not just how. Purpose makes the work meaningful. Finally, track your growth. Each week is a story worth documenting.

And most importantly, reflect often. Frameworks like Ikigai or ETTO are more than ideas. They help you lead with clarity, pace yourself with purpose, and turn an internship into transformation.

7. AI as a Tool

While AI was not a formal part of the employer’s production workflows, I used it frequently to accelerate early-stage creative work and explore alternate directions. Tools like ChatGPT and image generators helped me brainstorm visual metaphors, summarize long planning documents, and refine character dialogue or scene structures during Microlesson development.

AI supported my process, but never replaced it. For example, I used AI to draft visual storyboards, then refined those drafts to match the employer’s tone and brand. When researching competitor platforms or building slide content, AI helped me organize dense information quickly, saving time during planning cycles.

That said, I also encountered the limitations of AI. Outputs require close review for accuracy, voice alignment, and relevance to the end user. Prompts had to be carefully written, and not all ideas generated were viable. The tools worked best when paired with a clear creative vision and strong design judgment.

Using AI in this way taught me the value of selective integration. I see potential for the employer to explore AI more deeply, particularly for content tagging, survey response grouping, or even generating instructional feedback templates. The key will be thoughtful implementation that supports, rather than replaces, human insight.

AI will not define the work we do, but it can support how we think, explore, and build. Used well, it becomes a partner in creative momentum.

8. Career Development

This internship helped me clarify not just where I want to go, but how I want to grow.

Going into the quarter, I knew I wanted to work at the intersection of creativity, systems design, and education. By leading visual development for the Microlesson Content project and contributing to cross-functional planning for the internal content system, I was able to test what that blend looks like in practice. I found that I thrive in roles where I can build structure, design meaningful visuals, and translate between creative, technical, and operational priorities.

This experience confirmed my interest in pursuing a long-term role as either a Product Manager or Senior Systems Designer. I enjoy being at the center of complex conversations, where design meets development, where timelines intersect with user needs, and where strategy becomes tangible.

The Ikigai framework helped me make sense of that alignment. I realized I was doing work I loved, that I was skilled at, and that served a real need in the organization (Garcia & Miralles, 2017). That insight gave me the confidence to stop chasing roles based solely on title or function and instead focus on making a contribution, being clear, and having a positive impact.

The path forward now feels more defined. My goal is to keep building toward roles that combine systems leadership with creative problem-solving. This internship gave me not just experience, but direction.

9. Problem Solving

One of the most complex challenges I faced involved the transition from a vertical-slice prototype to a complete set of animated microlessons. Our original format assumed a unified course structure. But after reviewing early feedback, we pivoted to a gated, card-based format that required a complete redesign of visuals, flow, and voiceover pacing.

This shift created a difficult choice. I could try to adapt existing assets and rush delivery to stay on schedule, or I could pause production to redesign the entire system. I applied the triple constraint framework (scope, time, and cost) to evaluate the trade-offs, and used the ETTO principle to weigh immediate efficiency against long-term consistency.

I chose to pause and rebuild. I redesigned visual templates, adjusted the production pipeline, and coordinated updated timelines with both internal teams and external contractors. This decision resulted in a temporary delay but led to fewer revisions, greater alignment between lessons, and a scalable format that supported future content.

What seemed like a setback became an opportunity to build infrastructure. I also created visual dashboards and modular asset libraries to improve production tracking. In the end, the project launched on time, with higher quality and fewer support issues post-release.

This experience reinforced my belief that effective problem solving requires both systems thinking and the courage to slow down when it counts.

10. Knowledge Gained

This internship deepened my ability to think in systems and reflect on the broader impact of my work. I moved beyond tools like Canva, Jira, and RISE, and began focusing on how tasks connect, scale, and support long-term goals. Through projects like the Microlesson Content pipeline and the internal content system coordination, I learned to identify dependencies, manage feedback loops, and build workflows that reduced rework. I also gained experience aligning stakeholders by using visual updates to bridge gaps between design, engineering, and leadership.

Applying frameworks like ETTO and Ikigai helped me balance competing priorities and understand where I create the most value. That shift from task execution to reflective contribution has become the foundation of how I plan to grow moving forward.

11. Conclusion

This internship was a defining chapter in my professional development. It challenged me to think critically, lead thoughtfully, and build systems that support creative and strategic goals. I contributed to real initiatives, including the Microlesson Content suite and the internal content system coordination, while refining my voice as a systems-oriented designer and project leader.

Along the way, I learned to strike a balance between ambition and intention. I applied frameworks like ETTO, Ikigai, and the triple constraint not only to projects, but also to my personal growth. I leave this experience with a more profound sense of purpose, stronger skills, and a clearer direction for the next stage of my career. the employer Education gave me the space to learn, lead, and reflect. I now move forward not just as someone who can manage tasks, but as someone who can shape systems, support teams, and lead with clarity.
