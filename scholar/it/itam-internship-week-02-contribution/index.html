About this paper

Week 02 weekly memo from my Winter 2024 ITAM Internship A at an EdTech employer. Five-section breakdown of what the project-manager seat actually covered in practice: SOC 2 Type 2 lead, a website-redesign initiative migrating from Photoshop to Figma, compliance-monitoring platform work and Information Security Team meetings, state-compliance and HR administration, the development-team standups and Jira-ticket triage, and the marketing and design support that filled the rest of the week. Written six weeks into the quarter, before any of the frameworks from later courses (Ikigai, the triple constraint as a named lens) had stabilized in my vocabulary.

Why it matters to the practice

This is the most useful Product-Owner-positioning artifact in the entire ITAM track because it documents breadth-of-role honestly. The same week I am leading a SOC 2 Type 2 audit and learning Figma for a public-facing redesign, I am also onboarding employees, ordering corporate assets, mediating dev and data Slack questions, and reviewing marketing collateral. The piece also names the cost of that breadth (working past forty hours, work bleeding into holidays) and surfaces the ETTO trade-off as the operational principle I was already reaching for. Everything in IT 490B's reflection was already showing up here in raw form.

Connections

Direct predecessor to the IT 490A final reflection (scholar/it/itam-internship-reflection-a.md) and the IT 490B capstone (scholar/it/itam-internship-reflection.md). The Tuckman stages (Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing) referenced here come from ADMG 372 Leadership & Supervision and recur throughout the program. The ETTO principle cited here in passing is the same framework I lean on heavily in 490B. The Lessig Framework mention pulls in IT 301's Market, Law, Norms, Architecture lens applied to EdTech regulatory funding. Together with the Week 05 sustainability memo, this anchors the weekly-memo layer of the internship below the two reflection papers.

A note on role and title

Although my official title during this internship was Lead Production Designer, I operated as a Product Manager and was treated as such by both peers and leadership. The titling was a mutually understood compensation-banding decision, not a reflection of scope or authority. The weekly memos use 'project manager' the way the original assignment did; in practice the role was closer to acting PM.

1. Contribution to the Company

As a project manager at the EdTech employer, I oversee SOC 2 Type 2 certification and lead a website redesign initiative. The SOC 2 Type 2 work is moving slowly but ramping up as we commit to audit dates in collaboration with our SOC 2 auditor. The website redesign has entered phase two. Phase one’s deliverable was confirming that the current site was up to date, compliant, and information-accurate. The redesign itself requires graphic-design collaboration with our design team, and because I have a graphic-design background I was chosen to execute it. The work is being done in Figma, a new tool for me, since past web designs only required Adobe Photoshop. I continue to work on these initiatives, though I have not yet been able to commit the time and attention I would prefer.

On the cybersecurity side, I oversee our use of the compliance monitoring platform and support the company as a member of the Information Security Team. The role covers access reviews, policy acceptance, background-check status, cybersecurity training, MDM status, corporate assets, vendors, risk management, and an array of controls, tests, and framework checks. I create, conduct, and submit security meetings. Incident response, analysis, and adjustments come up periodically. Tracking, monitoring, and developing processes, practices, controls, monitors, and supporting evidence in compliance with the SOC 2 Type 2 framework is also part of the work.

Administrative duties include recordkeeping, meeting coordination, and general communications. From a governmental-compliance perspective, I assist with state business licenses, state employee registration, and regulatory obligations. I help schedule and run sync and inquiry meetings with a state-registration vendor, then file and document those activities. For HR, I assist with onboarding new employees: receiving and processing required documentation, walking them through key company systems and practices, ordering required corporate assets, and submitting background checks. I follow up to confirm access and service acquisition. Right now, I am auditing and updating documentation across all employees to keep us aligned with company policy and SOC 2 Type 2 requirements. I also monitor communications, answer questions on our policy and cybersecurity Slack channel, and submit Jira tickets in the Admin project to track and resolve employee issues during bi-weekly administration meetings. Maintaining our Employee Status and Information log does not take much effort but is vital to operations. I monitor the corporate asset inventory and order any materials related to daily job operations. Finally, I conduct vendor-review requests from clients when delegated.

The development team is a joy to work with daily. Communication is essential, and I run three standup meetings weekly with one product meeting bi-weekly. Ad-hoc meetings to remove roadblocks and problem-solve UI/UX and execution issues are frequent. I help develop and maintain organizational structure and communication norms through department-specific culture guidelines. I use Jira to manage issue submissions and task flow through Agile-like scrums. During bi-weekly product-development meetings we document and communicate roadblocks. To keep the team supported, I monitor and mediate questions in the dev and data Slack channels, escalating and documenting issues through Jira and surfacing latent or proactive work during standups. The bi-weekly development meeting with our product manager is essential for prioritization and direction on product changes; I lead, prepare, and execute the meeting documentation and line items. I am also developing practice and process control documents for our product development and data management work, and I stay closely involved in problem solving, UI/UX direction, and executing development directives.

Work with the clientele departments covers clients, sales, and marketing. Most of that work involves resolving and communicating issues between clients (represented by our customer success managers) and developers. I also fulfill marketing-centric activities on request: ordering resources for conferences, designing materials for public events, sending email communications to requisition production resources, and organizing the media files that make all of that move quickly. Recently I have been asked to review, log, and assist in developing marketing resources and social-media activities. I meet weekly to discuss media activities and help facilitate marketing processes that fit the organization. Developing these resources is the most fun part of my week, since it overlaps most directly with personal creative work.

In the future, I will be able to commit more time to R&D initiatives that positively affect student well-being.

2. Challenges and Possible Solutions

My most prominent issue is time-to-task allocation and self-care. Against the organization’s own policy preferences, I consistently work past my allotted forty hours each week. I regularly work through holidays and breaks. My concern is that despite the extra investment, the breadth and depth of my tasks are still not as well attended to as I would like. Sometimes I feel as though I am not keeping the ship from sinking, only slowing its descent enough that the leak is not visible.

In a remote environment, perceptions sit further from reality. Because I work across so many disparate parts of the company, no one person sees the entirety of my duties and contributions. That means it is easy to be perceived as only just satisfactorily handling what I have. I have to triage carefully to the most pertinent and necessary tasks. The trade-off is real: improving the grade of work pulls from personal time, because the efficiency-thoroughness trade-off (ETTO, from ADMG 372) means addressing every task requires giving up some thoroughness in favor of efficiency.

My near-term solution is to keep investing extra hours where added thoroughness reduces the cost of the same task in its inevitable next iteration. Experience and skill on a recurring task increase with dedicated repetition, which compounds back into efficiency. In that way I keep pushing the grade of my work upward over time (the distinction between grade and quality is one I picked up in IT 165’s Seeing Through Data).

I have also drawn my superiors’ attention to this for the last several months. We are onboarding another administrative-focused employee who can take over some of my tasks, and once they are onboarded I expect to recover time for higher-grade work.

3. ITAM Course Contributions

I cannot think of one ITAM course that has not contributed to my current role. Every course in the program has provided clarity and sharpened or expanded my perspectives and skills. Even the painful experience of working in Cengage during the Change Ready Tech Skills courses ended up useful: it burned in a deep consideration and empathy for what happens when the human element is not properly accounted for in UX/UI.

Yesterday, while pulling references from IT 301 into a training document on what social-emotional learning is, how it works, and why it matters to our organization, I leaned on the Lessig Framework. Lessig’s lens (Market, Law, Norms, Architecture) is a useful way of understanding how modalities influence individuals. Our EdTech work depends substantially on governmental funding of student-wellness initiatives in educational institutions, so the Market and Law modalities are doing as much shaping of outcomes as the Architecture of the product itself.

The general pattern is that CS courses, leadership and administration, management, sales and consumer relations, statistics, and even my general-education courses all show up in daily decisions. Naming each one is too much for this forum, but their fingerprints are everywhere in the week.

4. Steps the Company Has Taken to Make Me Comfortable

The employer is a small, wellness-focused, data-driven SaaS organization. The team is personable and caring, and we are all passionate about our role in student health. That alignment carries us through difficult periods. Because the culture is emotional-intelligence-focused, we are implicitly committed to being empathetic, conscientious, and self-reflective. There is no one at the company lacking the essential skill of double-loop learning (also from ADMG 372). The soft skills across the company are abundant (a frame I picked up in ADMG 371 and RMT 379). People who are rougher around the edges are still well received because others step in to help bridge any challenges.

My own onboarding is a good example. I was hired by the current CEO, whom I have known for many years, but I immediately began working closely with the founding CEO, whom I had only briefly met before. We became fast friends and a formidable team, implementing solutions quickly.

5. Best Advice This Week

There is so much fantastic advice every week. I want to recognize our customer success manager and product peer, who is moving into a product-manager role for a new endeavor. We have had challenges getting to know each other intuitively. We have both worked on it, on communicating and on understanding the teacher’s perspective inside our product. Our friction stems from both of us being protective of our people and not shy about confrontation on their behalf. We needed time to Storm, Form, Norm, and Perform (ADMG 372 again, on building high-performance teams). I was willing to be vulnerable about my anxiety, and she recommended exercise and, more than anything, took a moment to listen. The advice in short: “Everything is okay.” How wonderful is that?

I also need to acknowledge our founding CEO, who still mentors me consistently. We have had a lot of product questions from newer customer success managers that end up landing as open-ended product advice in the form of suggested solutions. These questions are often aimed at me even though I have no authority on the product (I am not the product manager). The founding CEO reminded me that asking questions can be better than providing answers. Doing so lets the other person walk through the situation as a team, both of you learning as you go. I may be confident in my solution, but I can still uncover valuable information from the conversation.

My initial reflex on why I had not been doing this, since I am usually aligned with that methodology, was time pressure. But as I tried to relay the thought, I realized the real issue. The two customer success managers in question have worked with me the least and are the newest in their roles. This is Tuckman again, Forming and Storming, still very early. Trust is the prerequisite of high-performing teams, and trust has not yet been built. I cannot expect what we have not yet earned. I set up a follow-up meeting to discuss further and execute the advice. The meeting did not end with as much understanding or trust as I had hoped, but everyone is different and some people need more time. My authoritative and expert-knowledge influence are both lower in that group, and the charismatic-influence channel had limited effect on seasoned customer success managers. We will get there, learning together.

6. Workspace

My workspace is my home office in Ellensburg. The employer is fully remote, and most of the team is based in southern California.
