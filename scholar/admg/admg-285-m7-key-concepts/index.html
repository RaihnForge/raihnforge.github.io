About this paper

A consolidated walkthrough of the five foundational sustainability concepts the course was organized around: Life Cycle Thinking, Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), Life Cycle Cost Analysis (LCCA), the Triple Bottom Line (TBL), and Circular Economy (CE). Each concept is defined, then applied to a working example — the cellular smartphone product life cycle — so the vocabulary lands against a real system. This paper is the conceptual scaffolding that the M9 sustainability proposal sits on top of.

Why it matters to the practice

These five concepts have become the lens I use to evaluate any product decision. Life Cycle Thinking forces a wider boundary than 'ship date.' LCA gives a structured way to ask what the inputs and outputs actually are. LCCA reframes cost over the full ownership window rather than the sticker price. TBL is the reminder that people and planet are not optional columns. Circular Economy is the design move — closing loops instead of leaving open ends. In EdTech and in the studio, these show up every time I argue for refurbished hardware, software-led upgrade paths, or long-supported tools over disposable ones.

Connections

Direct upstream of the M9 Sustainability Proposal, which uses every concept defined here. Lateral to ADMG 477 (Project Performance Reporting) on the topic of scope and boundary definition, and to ADMG 479 on systems thinking. The cellular smartphone example threads into the studio's own secondary-use thinking on hardware (donated iPhones as workstation peripherals, learning devices, capture rigs) and informs the way I write product roadmaps for software with long support windows.

Key Sustainability Concepts

It is imperative to understand the key sustainability concepts of life cycle thinking, life cycle assessment, life cycle cost analysis, the triple bottom line, and circular economy in order to enable sophisticated and impactful sustainable decision-making.

Life Cycle Thinking

What is Life Cycle Thinking

Life Cycle Thinking is the beginning of consideration of a manmade product or service informed through the lens of natural life cycles for the purpose of gathering information about obtaining all materials to produce, manufacture, and use in order to educate and enact sustainable system designs (Matthews et al., 2014). Sustainability, ecological footprint, and product life cycle understandings are essential for making sustainable choices for product life cycles such as cellular phone devices. It establishes systems thinking and boundaries to define and manage elements.

Sustainability is a broad term that indicates that “humanity has the ability to make development sustainable to ensure that it meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” (United Nations, 1987). Actions that align with future generations’ consideration of meeting their resource needs would be considered sustainable actions in the context of ecological discussions.

In order to measure sustainability outcomes, an Ecological Footprint is often used as a metric of impact. Eco Online (What Is an Ecological Footprint?, n.d.) defines Ecological Footprint as a measure that indicates how many environmental resources are required to support a specific way of life or business. This is not the only metric used. Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter (Matthews et al., 2014) references Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) as another, while the study Modeling of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios for Smartphones (Pamminger et al., 2021) treats Global Warming Potential (GWP) as a measurable metric associated with sustainability.

The five stages of the product lifecycle are imperative in assisting in scope and determining the boundaries of a system (Matthews et al., 2014, p. 12). Systems thinking refers to the collection or set of interconnected parts, while boundaries are a subset of a system that is of interest or to be focused on for a specific purpose (Matthews et al., 2014). The stages include material extraction, material processing, manufacturing, use, and waste management (Vigon et al., 2020). Upon the end-of-life of a product, a disposition pathway is inevitable. End-of-pipe treatment accounts for the resulting outputs of a product at the end of its product life cycle. The referenced cradle-to-cradle phrase indicates that the end of a product life cycle is remanufacturing a product into something new. Conversely, the cradle-to-grave idea is that a life cycle will begin at production and terminate when the use-case of the product has run its course (Matthews et al., 2014). Unfortunately, many companies are still only concerned with cradle-to-gate linear design model accountability, which limits considerations to production only. Reuse, remanufacturing, or recycling (Matthews et al., 2014, p. 12) are the potential sustainable pathway terminations.

Life Cycle Thinking Implementation for Cellular Phones

An excellent and pertinent example of life cycle thinking pertains to cellular phone products. These devices are ubiquitous as a global consumer norm, and production has continued to increase over the years (Ercan et al., 2016). The following is a summary of the life cycle information of cellular phones as sourced from the Life Cycle Assessment of a Smartphone (Ercan et al., 2016) and Modeling of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios for Smartphones (Pamminger et al., 2021).

Emission Factors are energy requirements needed for the production of smartphones. The amount of fuel utilized in the generation of the product must be accounted for in association with the cellular phone’s product life cycle. Energy sources differ based on locations and energy generation systems (Ercan et al., 2016). Raw Materials Acquisition of smartphones requires the accounting of all materials associated with Print Board Assemblies (PBAs) and Application Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs). The glues and troublesome resources required to produce screens (Pamminger et al., 2021) must be accounted for as well. Additionally, considerations for Packaging Materials from virgin and recycled materials should be considered (Ercan et al., 2016). Production of parts, integrated circuit production, display production, parts transportation, assembly, ICT manufactured support activities, and distribution make up production aspects (Ercan et al., 2016), as well as the application of all materials indicated in previous stages.

The Use stage of cellular devices averages 2.5 (Pamminger et al., 2021) to 3 years (Ercan et al., 2016). Associated use of networks is an aspect of the use case, as there is a great amount of infrastructure dedicated to supporting the device’s cellular capabilities (Ercan et al., 2016). End-of-Life Treatment (EoLT) of cellular devices can be difficult to attribute, as finding exact accountability for disposal actions is challenging due to consumer control after the use phase. Conservative projections estimate that 19% of devices are recycled, while 81% of device disposal is attributed to landfill waste (Ercan et al., 2016).

A life cycle thinking approach allows for the consideration of sustainability by beginning to understand the possible inputs and outputs associated with a given product or service. It is, however, not a pathway to sustainability or a panacea, but merely assists in informing one.

Life Cycle Assessment

What is LCA

Life Cycle Assessments lean on quantitative methods and utilize systems thinking to assess inputs and outputs of a product or service life cycle within a given scope, but qualitative elements are still important to implement. The Life Cycle Assessment Framework is utilized in the development of Life Cycle Assessments. International Standards Organization (ISO) compliance is sought so that LCAs may be sufficiently useful to the academic community.

The Life Cycle Assessment Framework utilizes four phases. Goal and scope define the study design parameters and system boundaries through statements of intent. The following more quantitative phases include inventory analysis, which is the collection of documented data, and the impact assessment, which addresses the point at which reporting of inventory results alludes to greater implications. The interpretation phase is the more qualitative aspect of an assessment that suggests a greater perspective and recommendations for change.

Standards such as goals, system boundaries, function, and functional unit, as well as data source and inventory, are some of the LCA design parameters closely scrutinized and must be considered carefully as they are applied to the Life Cycle Assessment framework in order to be ISO compliant. These elements must be standardized and agreed upon, as they allow for global pursuits to communicate and build a universally available and connected knowledge base of the subject matter (Matthews et al., 2014).

The International Standards Organization (ISO) has this specific purpose and includes standards for other domains, such as cyber security. Authors of Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter (Matthews et al., 2014) note this standardization is called the ISO LCA Standard and formalizes quantitative requirements in implementing life cycle thinking; the current iteration is ISO 14040:2006. Under ISO Standards, an LCA Scope and Goal Statement must have twelve specific elements, but the particulars of the following are especially important to comprehend.

Goals must address four key aspects: the intended application, the reason for the study, the intended target audience, and any ambition the study may have for comparative assertions in conjunction with other products or systems that will be publicly released (Matthews et al., 2014).

Establishing the scope of which an LCA is considering is referred to as System Boundaries. Elements of System Boundaries are many. The product is any service or good, and processes are the transformation of inputs to outputs. Stages in these processes can have direct effects or indirect effects, which are things that happen within the process or outside of the process, respectively (Matthews et al., 2014). Elementary flows have non-human transformations derived from natural effects (Matthews et al., 2014) that must also be noted. Product system diagrams or process flow diagrams illustrate these interactions and System Boundaries.

Function and Functional Unit information are needed to ensure compatibility and conversion with other studies and for the reproduction of the research results by others. The components to consider are the product system, “a collection of processes that provide a certain function” (Matthews et al., 2014); the function, which describes what the product system does; and the functional unit, a quantitatively defined measurement pertaining to the product system’s functional inputs and outputs (Matthews et al., 2014).

Data Source Inventory is important as it denotes the particular inputs and outputs that a specific study focuses on. This further establishes the boundaries of a given study. The Impact Assessment establishes comparative factors, methods, and units of measurement that are being made. This better informs the audience of the scope of impact in conjunction with other potential studies and domains.

There are many other important core considerations when approaching LCA. For instance, Quantitative Methods are described by Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter (Matthews et al., 2014) as involving reasoning through manipulating numbers and statistics to gain results, often through math and statistics. The Qualitative Method involves critically applying concepts beyond numerical application to communicate the relevance of findings and facts. The textbook notes that Life Cycle Assessment is focused on quantitative methods, but qualitative methods are also required in order to convey the importance and impact of study results in order to enact change by influencing the public, economy, and government.

The three-step Quantitative and Qualitative Assessment is a method of communicating to audiences how one may describe methods, present results, and commentate on the results. According to Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter (Matthews et al., 2014), the three steps include a description of the method used to complete the task, the result or output (quantitative or qualitative) of the task, and a critical assessment, validation, or thought related to the result.

Variations when approaching data include many concepts to be mindful of. Accuracy measures objectively how close results are to actual values, while precision describes how consistent multiple samples are. Associated variability is a constant difference in measurement factors that are outside of control. Uncertainty, however, is due to missing information that may be obtained if the complexity of variations is more closely studied (Matthews et al., 2014).

Data sources must also be considered. Primary sources are definitive data collected from the source directly, while secondary sources are information derived from anything that may be citing a primary source but could include inconsistencies or misrepresentations. Multiple primary sources are groups that collect similar primary source information but then normalize it in some fashion in order to arrive at a consensus (Matthews et al., 2014).

Life Cycle Assessment Implementation

The LCA article (Pamminger et al., 2021) utilizes a unified metric that considers first-life and second-life parameters of a standardized cellular device with an average 2.5-year use time. The first-life is taken from other LCA studies and is impacted by this study’s focus on the second-life of the cellular device. By focusing on the second-life aspect, the study determines which of three options, repair, refurbish, or remanufacture, is the best strategy in consideration of sustainability impacts.

The study performs an LCA on smartphones in order to generate a baseline for what is considered the smartphone’s first-life. Baseline is an essential term because the study does not want to expand the scope of study to include the LCA of second-use scenarios but desires to comply with ISO recommendations of an attributional approach and to reduce system expansion. The second-life assessment depends on one of the three possible circular end-of-use, or cradle-to-cradle, scenarios. These three are repair, refurbishment, or remanufacturing. This means that the study issues first-life and second-life valuation attributes that aggregate into a total economic valuation parameter in order to more effectively compare the three scenarios it plans to test.

The goal of the article (Pamminger et al., 2021) is to assist in guiding decisions concerning Circular Economy strategies of smartphones after their first-life when multiple life cycles might be considered and which strategy might be the best option in consideration of sustainability. Essentially, the goal is to determine what might be the most sustainable option at the end of the life of cellular devices between repair, refurbishment, or remanufacturing of cell phones.

The System Boundaries (Scope) include the three above-mentioned end-of-use scenarios only as second-life options. The devices being tested are not those that have been designed specifically for second-life product life cycles. Furthermore, the study (Pamminger et al., 2021) suggests the information is normalized into an aggregated valuation for later comparison.

The Function and Functional Unit for this study is “one working smartphone for a use time of 2.5 years” (Pamminger et al., 2021). This unit is derived from annotated previous works. The second-life use time is an additional parameter that is scaled into the average of the functional unit. The study further defines the constraints of what a working smartphone is.

Data Sources and Inventory for the study (Pamminger et al., 2021) indicate the life cycle inventory by detailing the reference smartphone model used in the studies. The article details comparative life cycle maps of the three different overlaid end-of-life strategy processes. Each process step is consistently considered but not necessarily applied (where appropriately unnecessary) throughout the three possible second-life scenarios. Each of the steps is detailed, with accountability metrics utilized to determine the input and output resource implications. These metrics are aggregated for scoring each of the end-of-life strategies.

Furthermore, to indicate the environmental impact, the study utilizes Global Warming Potential (GWP) as a metric assigned to each of the three strategies. Over the course of 2.5 years of use time considered, the results for each are 10 kg CO2-eq for repair impacts, 15.5 kg CO2-eq for refurbishing, and 25.8 kg CO2-eq for remanufacturing.

According to the study (Pamminger et al., 2021), the repairing and refurbishing options are far more effective than the remanufacturing option. Repair is the first and best choice for sustainability, with some variance in consideration of the part that is being repaired and the success of said repair. The metrics here, despite not having a guaranteed success rate of repair, are still better than other options. Also noted is that if the repair is unsuccessful, the device may still fold into one of the other competing options. Refurbishing is a close second choice. The contention with refurbishing is the extended reallocation phase, which may include various transportation costs. Incursions and resource consumption at refurbishing locations are also cited. In comparison, repairing has a near-null location and transportation impact, as repairs can often be done locally or at preexisting multipurpose locations (at home). Remanufacturing is the least advised option, as it has the most likely resource consumption in transportation and disassembly with the most negligible value reclamation.

Studies like the Life Cycle Assessment of a Smartphone (Ercan et al., 2016) make clear why the components of LCA and the ISO LCA framework are essential to enhance understanding as findings propel innovation and change for a better, more sustainable, designed future. The study mandates the burden companies must bear and implores them to enact take-back programs with accountability to production life cycle methods in order to comply with governmental policies and respect market reactions to brand conduct. These findings provide actionable information for individuals, organizations, and policymakers.

Life Cycle Cost Analysis

What is Life Cycle Cost Analysis (LCCA)

Life Cycle Cost Analysis, much as its name indicates, is a holistic understanding related to the total cost estimates throughout the life cycle duration pertaining to operating a system or product. The analysis includes many important factors to understand (Matthews et al., 2014). LCCA Domains, model types, and temporal accounting are key in the understanding of Life Cycle Cost Analysis.

In the Engineering Domain, Status Quo refers to a project that is already undertaken or is in place. Alternative Designs are variations in approach, usually to reduce costs estimated by LCCA (Matthews et al., 2014). First (or initial) costs are the costs incurred at the beginning of a project, often referred to as “shovel to ground” costs (Matthews et al., 2014). Future costs are the costs attributed after the initial completion of a product or project. These would be after the “shovel to ground” costs (Matthews et al., 2014). Recurring costs are the costs that happen with a certain frequency, such as maintenance (Matthews et al., 2014). Total costs are the sum accounting of all costs: first cost, future cost, and recurring costs (Matthews et al., 2014). Sunk costs are previous costs that are not attributed to total costs for new life cycle design alternatives (Matthews et al., 2014). In essence, design alternatives should not account for the Status Quo costs.

In the Public Projects Domain, Owner Costs are the element of costs that are accrued by the implementing or sponsoring party of a project, while User Costs are costs attributed to the stakeholders or those involved in the use or participation of the product, system, or project (Matthews et al., 2014). This delineation assists in understanding the pros and cons of alternative models. Understanding which party might accrue a greater burden of differing variables attributed to costs assists in a greater understanding of potential issues that may present throughout the comprehensive life cycle model.

Particularly important to the information technology domain, Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) extends the scope of analysis to purchasing, maintaining, facilities, training, and current hardware-software upkeep (Matthews et al., 2014). This concept assists in obtaining a more exhaustive account of the implementation of designs.

Temporal Accounting is an effort to gain even more accurate information from cost modeling, as the effects of time must be accounted for in costs. Concepts important to understanding the temporal effects on LCCA include the Break Even Analysis, which solves for the input value that has a prescribed effect on a model (Matthews et al., 2014). Usually, this is the point at which two opposing values meet, as in profit and loss. When the profit meets or overcomes losses, the project has broken even. Also, the Payback Period (PP = First Cost Investment / Annual Recurring Cost Savings) is the time it will take for an initial investment to pay for itself in terms of future savings.

For proper accounting of monetary values, there needs to be an understanding of the decaying value of monetary units that are represented. This is where Discounting Future Values to the Present is key to LCCA modeling (Matthews et al., 2014). Essentially, it attempts to account for the variations in the valuation of currency over time by considering Discounting Factors (F = P (1 + r)^n, P = F (1 + r)^-n), or future and present dollar conversions (Matthews et al., 2014). The Discounted Payback Period (DPP) compares the initial cost of investment against the resulting cumulative discounted cash flows but does not necessarily account for the diminishing valuation of currency or discounting factors (Matthews et al., 2014).

Finally, there are two life cycle model types that are deemed either Deterministic or Probabilistic / Stochastic (Matthews et al., 2014). Deterministic models are those that consider fixed values and assumptions, while Probabilistic / Stochastic models are those that assume variability or chance (Matthews et al., 2014). Probabilistic / Stochastic models are more robust but require greater design investment.

LCCA Implementation

Examples of LCCA implementation in the workplace are overwhelming. Responsible Information Technology industry professionals should be assessing projects and business initiatives continuously in relation to life cycle impacts and value propositions. Civil engineers build models, test them, and present them to lawmakers and government agencies to decide based on the Owner Costs (Publicly Funded Agencies) and User Costs (The Public) (Matthews et al., 2014). Every decision-maker should consider LCCA models to enhance their understanding of choices. For the specific example of cellular phones, Facilitating Substance Phase-Out Through Material Information Systems and Improving Environmental Impacts in the Recycling Stage of a Product (Paska, 2010) expresses that companies like Sony Ericsson have respected these considerations along with LCA in order to ensure that the company may remain profitable and economically sustainable while also pursuing their sustainable resource substitutions over the years.

Triple Bottom Line (TBL)

What is the Triple Bottom Line (TBL)

The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) is the consideration in sustainability for environmental, social, and economic aspects. Derived from John Elkington in consideration of business and sustainability, the concept posits that sustainability must be achieved by balancing three factors that drive the input and output of resources (Sustainability Illustrated, 2014). Through photosynthesis, all energy on the planet is derived; thus, the planet (environment) is a consideration of all resource existence. Nested within the planet is the people (social) consideration, those of us who require the resources (Sustainability Illustrated, 2014). The profit (economy) is an aspect of society that draws from resources and converts them into products and services useful to people.

Triple Bottom Line (TBL) Implications

Businesses and society must understand TBL, as it is a holistic approach to resource transactions and management. From a business perspective, a corporation’s success should be measured by social, ethical, and environmental performance, not simply the traditional profit-centric financial bottom line (Norman & MacDonald, 2004).

Cellular phone manufacturers must follow suit with what Sony Ericsson has started in sustainability (Paska, 2010). Over time the wastes of rare natural resources and waste production will poison the environment, societies will increasingly become aware of bad business practices and empower governmental agencies to pass stricter policies, and these factors will result in devastation for the bottom line of device manufacturers.

This is reflected in the growing understanding in literature and empirical support that sustainability aspects are essential to consider in supplier selection. What is being called Sustainable Supplier Selection (SSS) is “a process by which the best suppliers (in terms of the triple bottom line of economic, social and environmental objectives) are selected in order to improve the purchasing process, help strategic partnership selection, enhance product quality, increase customer satisfaction and, ultimately, to gain a competitive advantage in the market for a buyer” (Rashidi et al., 2020).

To succeed in business or as a community, we must all be considerate and responsible with resources. Companies developing cellular devices can make more sustainable choices for the long term so long as they can remain profitable, and people responding well to those choices will help ensure continuous business strategies account for sustainable measures. This is evidenced by Andrew Savitz (Savitz, 2013), who recounts the business disasters of Hershey in following the traditional bottom-line profit pursuit, citing poor social and environmental company choices in 2002 and 2012. Savitz claims that “executives must consider the social, economic, and environmental impacts on anyone with a stake in the outcome.”

Circular Economy

What is a Circular Economy

According to Walter R. Stahel (2016), Circular Economy (CE) is a model for product life cycle design that aims to close loops in industrial ecosystems by closing end-of-life product life cycle stage loops into the resource needs of other products to minimize or eradicate all waste. The author proposes that new technologies, trends toward circular and performance economies, policies, and information communication strategies will be needed to achieve this model.

Deborah Andrews (2015) narrows in on designers and education as the core elements required to realize the CE model. According to The Circular Economy, Design Thinking and Education for Sustainability (Andrews, 2015), the circular economy is an alternative to the status quo, the Linear Economy model. This model is roughly: take, make, use, and dispose of. The article states that the Linear Economy model’s waste has been exacerbated by the first industrial designers’ culture of planned obsolescence and is unsustainable, given the rising population and resource needs. Further detailed is the concept of Biomimicry (Andrews, 2015), the scientific innovation rooted in the CE model. It is the parity (or mimicry) of biological, ecological systems in which waste from one element supplements the resource needs of other elements in the system to the point where no resource is wasted.

In Modeling of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios for Smartphones (Pamminger et al., 2021), Circular Economy is addressed as a concept integral to what are called circular end-of-use scenarios. CE is related to cradle-to-cradle product life cycle concepts and end-of-pipe treatment according to Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter (Matthews et al., 2014).

Circular Economy Implications

A reuse philosophy attributed to discarded cellular devices through repurposing initiatives in order to fulfill a new use case could drastically lower the frequency of cellular devices arriving at end-of-life states in landfills. Following the studies of End-of-Life Treatment (EoLT) possibilities addressed in Modeling of Different Circular End-Of-Use Scenarios for Smartphones (Pamminger et al., 2021), repair, remanufacture, and recycling are excellent options for the EoLT stage of smartphones. Circular Economy is important in the application of life cycle stages. By understanding and applying CE concepts to cellular phone life cycles, we can begin to derive methods of improvement to close loops in the current cellular phone life cycle.

Sustainability in Practice

It is imperative to understand the key sustainability concepts of life cycle thinking, life cycle assessment, life cycle cost analysis, the triple bottom line, and circular economy in order to enable sophisticated and impactful sustainable decision-making. Mastering these concepts will empower professionals, individuals, and organizations to build sustainable futures through deliberate action in their careers, personal lives, and communities.
