About this paper

A full Agile project plan for a real initiative at my EdTech employer: producing eight five-part animated microlessons in Articulate RISE 360 and integrating them through an internal content control system into the survey platform. The plan walks the project-specific vision and success criteria, the interpersonal team dynamics, the product roadmap, the sprint planning, and worked examples of a daily stand-up and a sprint review plus retrospective.

Why it matters to the practice

This is the applied counterpart to the ADMG 479 Agile theory papers. The earlier discussion posts named the Manifesto values and the Scrum ceremonies; this plan shows what they look like when wired to a real product team with real people, a real product owner, and a real release deadline. The 'learning loop' framing (Feedback, Reflection, Learning, Application) is the same framing I bring to my own studio work.

1. Project-Specific Information

The employer is exploring, through research and development, the creation of content that complements the data gathering it does to assess and monitor student wellness. This utilizes the learning-loop method. The four steps are Feedback, Reflection, Learning, and Application (Hubkengroup, 2025). The company’s survey platform constitutes the Feedback and Reflection portion of the loop. By developing content, the organization can incorporate Learning and Application to complete the learning loop.

By closing the learning loop, the company’s product offerings promote continuous growth in school climate and culture through student wellness. Supplying the demand for each stage equates to positive change at a high impact threshold. Applied appropriately, continuous monitoring and improvement will result in improved product effectiveness, providing a notable increase in value to clients.

High-Level Vision

Create and deliver a series of interactive microlessons that effectively translate educational video content into scalable, engaging, and modular learning experiences using Articulate RISE 360 and an internal content control system for integration into the survey platform.

Mission / Objectives of Project

  1. Create microlearning lessons that align with SEL principles and usability standards and are responsive to student engagement data through the internal content system.
  2. Create and embed eight five-part animated videos that adhere to defined Content Director instructional design scripts within microlessons.
  3. Develop a consistent and repeatable design for RISE 360 microlessons that includes a title, videos, encouragement, interaction, process steps, and a salutation.
  4. Ensure a scalable process for the content pipeline to account for possible additional content.
  5. Ensure integration with the internal content system and the survey platform.

Success Criteria

  • All eight microlessons completed and deployed by the internal deadline.
  • Positive feedback from internal QA and pilot educators.
  • Smooth functioning across devices and browsers.
  • Reduced turnaround time for future lesson creation through reusable templates.

2. Interpersonal Team Dynamics

High-level team communication, direction, and coordination guidelines between the Content Team and the Development Team are established as the foundation for the duration of the project. They may be modified and expanded with the approval of the team leaders and the Scrum Master. This is a baseline communication plan; team members should communicate more regularly on specific tasks. The shared channels (Slack huddles and Jira dashboards) hold the structure, and the cross-functional standups carry the rhythm.

3. Agile Product Roadmap

The product roadmap is a high-level visual guide that outlines the strategic direction of the product over time. It captures key milestones, goals, and deliverables across multiple releases, aligning the team around shared priorities and the evolving product vision. As work progresses and new information becomes available, the roadmap is updated to reflect current insights, emerging themes, and refined estimates.

4. Agile Release and Sprint Planning

Outlines how features are delivered incrementally through time-boxed sprints, organized into broader release cycles aligned with roadmap goals. Each release is scoped around a coherent learner-facing milestone: from functional pilots to MVP launch to scope-expanded content sets.

5. Agile Daily Stand-Up Meetings

Daily stand-ups are short, focused meetings that promote team alignment, transparency, and continuous collaboration. Held at the same time and place each day, these 15-minute syncs allow each team member to quickly address three key questions:

  1. What did I do yesterday?
  2. What will I do today?
  3. Are there any blockers or impediments?

For this content initiative, stand-ups are essential for coordinating a multi-role, cross-functional team that includes design, engineering, research, and executive stakeholders. As we progress through each release cycle, from functional pilots to the MVP launch, these meetings ensure that content development, system integration, and client feedback remain aligned.

Facilitated by the Scrum Master, stand-ups support real-time awareness of project velocity, identify risks early (e.g., pending dependencies or resource limitations), and reinforce Agile values like accountability and shared ownership. Even in a hybrid remote setting, we maintain a culture of co-location through centralized channels (Slack huddles and shared Jira dashboards), allowing for efficient coordination between content, development, leadership, and sprint facilitation.

Stand-ups are especially vital during transitions between releases, as they help recalibrate focus, reassign priorities, and keep momentum high while adapting to stakeholder feedback and evolving product requirements.

6. Agile Sprint Review and Retrospective

Each sprint ends with two key meetings: the Sprint Review and the Sprint Retrospective. Although often paired, they serve different purposes and target different audiences.

The Sprint Review is a product-focused session where the team demonstrates the completed work to stakeholders, including Owners, clients, and customer success managers (CSMs). For this content initiative, this might include showcasing new microlesson videos, content-system features, or updates to survey-platform integration. Stakeholders such as the Product Owner and the Content Director provide feedback on functionality, content tone, and alignment with project goals, helping guide future iterations.

The Sprint Retrospective is an internal meeting for the Scrum team, led by the Scrum Master. Here, team members reflect on what went well, what did not, and how processes can improve moving forward. Topics often include communication, tooling (Jira, Slack), and coordination across content and development efforts.

Together, these processes drive both product improvement and team growth, key to sustaining Agile momentum throughout each release.

Sprint 18: Completed

  • What’s Next: Confirm MVP feature list. Begin scoped feature enhancement and field testing.

Prepping Release 4

  • Things to Demo: Initial outline for new SEL theme alignment. Template for updated RISE interactions.
  • Updates: Internal alignment on content-system + survey-platform roadmap expectations. Awaiting research synthesis for tone revisions.
  • What’s Next: Re-assign story points for prioritized backlog. Incorporate reviewer suggestions into the next sprint.

Sample Retrospective — June 6, 2025

What Went Well

  • Final two microlessons delivered on time with consistent quality.
  • Slack-to-Jira automation reduced task handoff time.
  • Content-system + survey-platform integration meeting internal review criteria.

What Could Have Gone Better

  • Last-minute clarifications delayed Lesson 8 tone lock.
  • Some ambiguity around Reduced Scope scene expectations.

What We Will Try Next

  • Formalize a checklist for Reduced Scope tasks.
  • Add daily thread summaries to Slack after stand-ups.
  • Pre-tag open questions in Jira to surface blockers sooner.

Open Questions

  • How do we plan to onboard external reviewers for the MVP pilot?
  • Are current lesson formats flexible enough for mid-cycle content pivots?
