ADMG 490 — Cooperative Education

May 22, 2026

Course Snapshot

  • Institution. Central Washington University.
  • Full title. Cooperative Education.
  • Catalog description. Supervised work experience that applies classroom learning in a professional setting. Students earn academic credit for structured, mentored work in an industry-aligned role. Drawn from the CWU course catalog entry for ADMG 490.
  • Credits. Variable, typically 4 to 12 credits depending on the agreed scope of the work experience and hours logged per term.
  • Prerequisites. Department permission. Students usually need standing in the Administrative Management major and a formal learning agreement signed by both a workplace mentor and a faculty supervisor before enrolling.
  • Designation. Major-track experiential credit. ADMG 490 is the Administrative Management version of CWU’s cooperative-education offering. The Information Technology and Administrative Management department also runs IT 490A on the IT track. Students in dual-track programs sometimes carry both, applying the same workplace hours through different academic lenses.
  • Thematic frame. Bridging classroom theory and professional practice. The course treats the workplace as the curriculum, with faculty and supervisor partnership as the mechanism for translating raw experience into structured learning.

Official Learning Outcomes

Paraphrased from CWU cooperative-education materials. On completion, a student should be able to:

  1. Apply academic concepts and skills from the Administrative Management curriculum to authentic problems encountered in a professional work setting.
  2. Document and reflect on the relationship between coursework and on-the-job practice, identifying where theory holds, where it bends, and where it falls short.
  3. Demonstrate professional behaviors expected of an entry-level practitioner, including communication, reliability, ethical conduct, and collaborative work.
  4. Set, pursue, and evaluate measurable learning goals negotiated with a workplace mentor and faculty supervisor.
  5. Build and use a professional network, including soliciting and receiving constructive feedback from a supervising practitioner.
  6. Articulate, in writing, the career-development value of the experience and how it informs next steps in education and employment.

How the Course Is Usually Organized

Cooperative Education is structured as a tripartite partnership among the student, the workplace mentor, and the faculty supervisor. The credit-bearing structure runs around the work experience rather than replacing it. A typical ADMG 490 term begins with a learning agreement, continues through weekly or biweekly reflection deliverables, and closes with a supervisor evaluation and a synthesizing final paper.

The student is expected to log a set number of work hours per credit (commonly thirty to forty hours per credit, varying by department policy and the nature of the placement). Faculty contact during the term is light by design. The course assumes the workplace is the primary teacher and the faculty supervisor is there to keep the academic frame around the experience honest.

Internally, the term moves through three phases. The opening phase is contracting: identifying learning goals, scoping projects, and aligning expectations with the workplace mentor. The middle phase is sustained work, threaded with structured reflection. The final phase pulls the experience together into a piece of reflective writing that names what was learned, names what was attempted but did not work, and points toward the next professional step.

Major Content Blocks

The Learning Agreement

The contracting document at the start of the term. The learning agreement names the workplace, the supervisor, the planned scope of work, and the specific learning goals the student commits to pursuing inside that work. It also sets the hours per week, the duration of the placement, and the criteria by which the supervisor will evaluate the student at term end. The agreement is co-signed by the student, the workplace mentor, and the faculty supervisor before the term begins. It becomes the reference document against which everything else in the course is measured.

Reflective Practice

Recurring reflection is the academic content of cooperative education. Most ADMG 490 instructors require weekly or biweekly journals, structured around prompts that connect the week’s tasks back to ADMG coursework. Standard prompts ask the student to identify a specific decision made, a specific tension noticed, a specific theory or framework from coursework that did or did not apply, and a specific question that arose for the student’s own development. Reflection is treated as a deliverable in its own right, not a side note.

Supervisor Evaluation

The workplace mentor’s formal assessment of the student. The evaluation rubric typically covers professionalism, communication, technical competence, initiative, collaboration, and progress against the learning agreement. The faculty supervisor reviews the evaluation alongside the student’s own materials when grading the course. The evaluation is the experience-side counterpart to the reflective writing.

Final Reflective Paper

The capstone deliverable. The paper synthesizes the term’s work into a structured, often academic-format reflection. Common required elements include a description of the host organization and the student’s role in it, a narrative of the major projects worked on, a discussion of the learning goals set in the agreement and the extent to which they were met, an analysis of how the experience connected to specific Administrative Management coursework, and a forward-looking statement on how the experience shapes the student’s continued education and career direction.

Professional Development

A standing theme rather than a discrete unit. The course expects students to treat the placement as a network-building, identity-forming experience as much as a task-completing one. Outputs may include resume updates, a refreshed LinkedIn presence, informational interviews with colleagues at the host organization, and a personal-development plan written against the student’s longer career arc.

Cross-Cutting Concepts

  • Theory and practice as a two-way exchange. Coursework is not a finished body of knowledge being deposited into the workplace. The workplace pressure-tests theory and sends material back the other direction.
  • Professional identity formation. The course quietly asks the student to begin operating as a practitioner, not just a learner. Voice, decision-making, accountability, and self-presentation all shift.
  • Mentorship as a structured relationship. The workplace mentor is a teacher with a syllabus they did not write. Students learn how to be mentored, how to ask for feedback, and how to convert critique into action.
  • Reflection as a competency. Writing about practice is itself a professional skill, used in postmortems, project retrospectives, performance reviews, and continuing-education portfolios.
  • Documentation discipline. The course rewards students who keep clean records of what they did, when, with whom, and to what effect. Documentation habits established here travel into every later project.
  • Career strategy. The experience is read as a data point in a longer trajectory, not as a self-contained event. Students leave with a sharper sense of the next step.

Commonly Used Reference Texts

These are standard companions for a cooperative-education or internship-credit course at the upper-division level. Any of them work as a reference if returning to the material.

  • Schon, Donald, The Reflective Practitioner. The foundational text on reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action that most reflective-practice course design ultimately traces back to.
  • Kolb, David A., Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development. The Kolb learning cycle (concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualization, active experimentation) is the spine of most cooperative-education program design.
  • Sweitzer, H. Frederick and Mary A. King, The Successful Internship: Personal, Professional, and Civic Development. A standard student-facing internship handbook.
  • Inkson, Kerr and Michael B. Arthur, Careers in Organizations. A widely used reference on career-development theory applicable to early-career professionals.
  • National Association of Colleges and Employers (NACE), Career Readiness Competencies. The de facto rubric for what entry-level professional competence means in 2020s American higher education.
  • Boud, David, Rosemary Keogh, and David Walker, eds., Reflection: Turning Experience into Learning. Source text for many of the reflection prompts that appear in cooperative-education courses.

Reading the Field as a Whole

  • The workplace is a curriculum that has to be read. The placement does not deliver learning on a schedule. The student has to identify what is being taught, by whom, and through what events. The reflection deliverables exist to force that reading habit.
  • Generosity from a supervisor is a teachable, askable thing. Students who do best in cooperative education learn to ask for what they need from a mentor, including time, feedback, exposure to higher-stakes work, and honest critique.
  • Cooperative education is where soft skills become measurable. Communication, reliability, judgment, and collaboration are vague terms in a classroom and concrete artifacts in a workplace. The course is one of the rare academic settings where those skills are graded in their natural habitat.
  • The artifacts outlast the credit. The supervisor evaluation, the final reflection, the resume update, and the professional network built during the term tend to be referenced years later. The course’s outputs are durable in a way most coursework is not.
  • Cross-track placements teach the same job twice. When the same workplace experience supports both an Administrative Management credit and an IT credit, the student is forced to see the role through two professional vocabularies at once. That double-vision is part of the point.
