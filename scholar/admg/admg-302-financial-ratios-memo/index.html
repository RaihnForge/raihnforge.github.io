TO: ADMG 302 Faculty FROM: Joshua Keyes DATE: 8 October 2023 SUBJECT: Profitability Ratios, Microsoft and Apple

This memo discusses the profitability ratios of Gross Profit Margin Percent and Net Profit Margin Percent. It walks the use cases and notable differences of each, then references and compares the resulting ratios from the FY2019 10-Ks of Apple and Microsoft.

Profitability Ratios

Profitability is the ability of a company to generate sales and control expenses. Profitability ratios are powerful tools for reading the performance of a company without deep-diving into every line item on the accounting statements, because by nature the ratios are innately comparative. Setting line items from the Income Statement against one another begins to generate meaning. Prioritizations and relative financial implications start to surface, and comparing the same ratios across reporting periods gives a narrative of performance over time.

Gross Profit Margin Percent

Gross Profit Margin Percent is calculated by dividing gross profit by revenue. The resulting percentage reveals how much of each dollar of revenue an organization gets to keep after the direct cost of goods sold (COGS) or cost of services (COS). It is a clean signal for whether the products or services being sold are profitable as a category, and by how much.

Gross profit is the indicator most directly tied to how well a company manages the cost of producing what it sells. The higher the gross margin, the more efficient the production-and-delivery cost structure is, relative to revenue.

For smaller companies, it is generally more important that this ratio be higher, because the buying power that sales provide has to cover operating expenses against a smaller base. A larger company can run a lower gross profit margin and still produce significant gross profit in absolute dollars, giving it more buying power against standard operating expenses.

Net Profit Margin Percent

Net Profit Margin Percent is calculated by dividing net profit by revenue. It is a strong indicator of a company’s ability to retain revenue through all expenses. The ratio accounts for COGS or COS, operating expenses, liabilities, one-time purchases, taxes, and more. Net profit is known as the bottom line because it represents the amount the company actually keeps after every cost of the business has been recognized, and the margin reflects that result as a share of revenue.

Microsoft and Apple Profitability Ratios

Microsoft’s Income Statement is found in Part II, Item 8 (Financial Statements and Supplementary Data, Income Statements) of its FY2019 10-K. Apple’s Income Statement is found in Part II, Item 8 (Consolidated Statements of Operations) of its FY2019 10-K.

Microsoft’s Gross Profit Margin Percent is high, and its Net Profit Margin also reads as very healthy. Apple’s profitability ratios likewise look strong, but the comparison surfaces meaningful differences.

MetricMicrosoft FY2019Apple FY2019
Gross Profit Margin (higher reads better)Notably higherLower
Net Profit Margin (% of revenue retained after operating expenses)~46% (31.2 / 66.7)~56% (21.2 / 37.8)

Microsoft’s much higher Gross Profit Margin is consistent with it being primarily a software company, where COGS is comparatively light. Apple is deeply rooted in hardware production, so its COGS carries the full weight of materials, manufacturing, and shipping, which compresses gross margin.

Net Profit Margin tells the opposite story. Apple retains a higher percentage of revenue after operating expenses than Microsoft does. The most plausible read is that Microsoft is investing aggressively in R&D. It competes in productivity software against Google, in operating systems against Apple’s macOS, and in gaming against Sony and Nintendo. Sustaining position in those highly competitive environments requires constant reinvestment of revenue, which shows up as a lower net margin even when gross margin is excellent.

Conclusion

Profitability ratios are excellent tools, but they have to be read with awareness of the artistic license that accountants carry, especially in how revenue is recognized and how depreciation, amortization, and liabilities are estimated. GAAP provides considerable leeway as long as the method is reasonable and consistent. Over-focus on a single ratio is how companies get themselves into trouble. Ford under-appreciated inventory costs. Sunbeam cut jobs and leaned on other short-term tactics to present better near-term results at the cost of long-term company health. The discipline is to read the pair, then read it again against time, and only then form an opinion.
