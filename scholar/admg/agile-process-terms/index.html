About this paper

A working definition of the ten core Agile roles and ceremonies, each annotated with the project manager's responsibility and the specific challenge inside that responsibility. Closes with a sketched-out lifecycle flowchart and a candid pick for the term that is hardest to do well.

Why it matters to the practice

Agile vocabulary is a load-bearing part of how I lead studio and Ed-Tech work. Naming the roles and ceremonies precisely keeps a team out of the trap of doing Scrum-the-process-theater without Scrum-the-discipline. The piece is the language I want everyone on a team to share before we start the first sprint.

Connections

Direct continuation of M02's Agile Manifesto values and principles paper. Together they form a baseline of Agile literacy that the rest of the ADMG track (project performance reporting, managing project uncertainty, executing project management) builds on. The retrospective callout at the end of this paper foreshadows ADMG-side themes around psychological safety and team health that recur across the program.

Product Owner

The Product Owner represents the customer or stakeholder and is responsible for prioritizing and refining the Product Backlog to deliver the most value. As a project manager, the supporting role is to ensure backlog clarity, facilitate prioritization, and align deliverables to stakeholder goals. The challenge is managing evolving priorities while maintaining a clear development path.

Agile Project Manager / Scrum Master

This role facilitates Agile processes, removes blockers, and ensures the team adheres to Agile principles. As a project manager, the work is to act as a servant-leader: supporting team autonomy, encouraging collaboration, and keeping momentum steady. The challenge is balancing traditional project leadership with Agile facilitation without overstepping into micromanagement.

Product Backlog

The Product Backlog is a dynamic list of desired features, bug fixes, and technical work, prioritized by the Product Owner. The PM helps maintain transparency and supports the grooming process to ensure alignment with strategic goals. A key challenge is keeping the backlog focused and manageable amid shifting stakeholder inputs.

Sprint Planning Meeting

This meeting starts each sprint, where the team commits to a subset of work from the Product Backlog. The PM helps ensure clarity of scope, assess team capacity, and facilitate a realistic commitment. The challenge is guiding the team toward achievable goals without overpromising.

Sprint Backlog

The Sprint Backlog contains all tasks and stories the team plans to complete during the sprint. The PM assists in visualizing progress and supporting workflow management. The challenge is maintaining up-to-date visibility and staying responsive to blockers without disrupting the team’s rhythm.

Sprint

A Sprint is a fixed time-box, usually one to four weeks, where the team builds a potentially shippable product increment. The PM monitors progress, protects the team from external disruptions, and ensures delivery focus. The challenge is balancing responsiveness to change with commitment to the sprint goal.

Daily Stand-Up Meeting

A brief daily check-in where team members share updates, plans, and roadblocks. The PM’s role is to facilitate or participate, identify blockers early, and support momentum. The challenge is keeping these meetings concise, meaningful, and free from status-report fatigue.

Sprint Review

The Sprint Review is a demonstration of completed work to gather stakeholder feedback. The PM coordinates the review, helps translate feedback into actionable items, and adjusts priorities accordingly. The challenge is navigating feedback constructively while preserving team motivation.

Retrospective

A reflection meeting after the sprint to identify successes, pain points, and process improvements. The PM encourages open discussion, documents takeaways, and fosters continuous improvement. The challenge is creating psychological safety and accountability without blame.

Release

A Release is the delivery of a working product increment to users, which may follow several sprints. The PM oversees readiness, coordinates QA and deployment, and ensures communication across teams. The challenge is syncing multiple moving parts while delivering a stable product.

Most Challenging Term

Retrospective. Encouraging psychological safety while prompting accountability and continuous improvement is both nuanced and essential. People are varied and unique. This stage requires a great deal of social-emotional intelligence to deliver in the most productive way possible.
