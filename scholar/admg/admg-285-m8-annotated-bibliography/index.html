About this paper

The annotated bibliography I built as the research scaffolding for the M09 Sustainability Proposal Final. Each entry lists the bibliographic citation, the key takeaway in my own words, and a short note on how I intended to use the source inside the final proposal. The bullet form is deliberate — it is a working researcher's document, not a formal essay, optimized for moving claims between drafts.

Why it matters to the practice

The annotated bibliography is the part of research work that nobody sees but everything else rests on. Putting it on the portfolio is honest about how I write longer arguments: gather, summarize, position, then compose. The same workflow shows up in every product spec, design rationale, and proposal I write today — including studio work on Forge Framework and the Mecromage postmortem.

Abstract

The following is my annotated bibliography for my final sustainability Life Cycle Analysis study proposal. The bullet points are not a part of the strict formatting of an annotated bibliography, but they are much more helpful to my process when referencing and moving information between applications and documents. The annotated bibliography should include the bibliography reference citation first, followed by cited references and important related takeaways, summarizing the reference and concluding with potential use cases for application to the paper to be written.

References (draft)

Andrews, D. (2015). The Circular Economy, Design Thinking and Education for Sustainability. Local Economy, 30, 305–315. https://doi.org/10.1177/0269094215578226

  • (Andrews, 2015) The Circular Economy has not been widely adopted yet. The proposed solution is to educate designers to change the culture from the Industrial Revolution’s Linear Economy thinking to sustainable Circular Economy (CE) Thinking.
  • Great source discusses the problem with traditional design trends that have led to the current issues with sustainability.

Brundtland, G. H. (1987). Report of the World Commission on Environment and Development. World Commission on Environment and Development (WCED).

  • (Brundtland, 1987) “humanity has the ability to make development sustainable to ensure that it meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.”
  • Defines Sustainability and global resource problem.
  • The foundation of the entire concept of sustainability, definition, philosophy, and urgency.

Ercan, M., Malmodin, J., Bergmark, P., Kimfalk, E., & Nilsson, E. (2016, January 1). Life Cycle Assessment of a Smartphone. https://doi.org/10.2991/ict4s-16.2016.15

  • (Ercan et al., 2016) Actual data acquired by LCA of cellular devices.
  • Fantastic for citing any sources related to the impact and identifying the life cycle of cellular phones.

Matthews, H., Hendrickson, C., & Matthews, D. (2014). Life Cycle Assessment: Quantitative Approaches for Decisions that Matter.

  • (Matthews et al., 2014) A comprehensive account of definitions and functions regarding Life Cycle Assessments.
  • Utilize definitions and processes as well as foundational approaches.

Norman, W., & MacDonald, C. (2004). Getting to the Bottom of “Triple Bottom Line.” Business Ethics Quarterly, 14(2), 243–262. https://doi.org/10.5840/beq200414211

  • (Norman & MacDonald, 2004) notes what the Triple Bottom Line is in great detail while also accounting for its faults.
  • Excellent steelman device, propelling a deeper look into actual change agents while keeping a critical perspective on buzz words as a placation of business.

Pamminger, R., Glaser, S., & Wimmer, W. (2021). Modeling of different circular end-of-use scenarios for smartphones. The International Journal of Life Cycle Assessment, 26(3), 470–482. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11367-021-01869-2

  • (Pamminger et al., 2021) compares the value of three end-of-life strategies for cellular devices, repair, recycle, and remanufacture in a Life Cycle analysis that concludes what the most effective and efficient loop closing method(s) are.
  • Fantastic comparable study looking into alternative end-of-life disposition paths.
  • All aspects of the thesis statement should be analogous to LCA study proposal.

Paska, D. (2010). Facilitating substance phase-out through material information systems and improving environmental impacts in the recycling stage of a product. Natural Resources Forum, 34, 200–210. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1477-8947.2010.01304.x

  • (Paska, 2010) is a comprehensive, if not exhaustive, look at the waste problems associated with cellular devices. Addressed are specific challenges with traditional end-of-life disposition pathways, including toxic breakdown processes that have immediate and long-term human and environmental effects. Noted is the contrasting method by Sony Ericsson, where the design of alternative, non-toxic materials, designated by careful tracking throughout the product life cycle, can reduce negative environmental effects during various end-of-life disposition pathways.
  • The source is great for noting the particular harms of e-waste and demonstrating the successful implementation of TBL by Sony Ericsson.

Rashidi, K., Noorizadeh, A., Kannan, D., & Cullinane, K. (2020). Applying the triple bottom line in sustainable supplier selection: A meta-review of the state-of-the-art. Journal of Cleaner Production, 269, 122001. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclepro.2020.122001

  • (Rashidi et al., 2020) critically analyzing supplier literature that calls out the lack of change in suppliers’ practices when considering sustainable practices.
  • Potential call to action that supports Paska’s need for LCA materials accountability, Andrews’s position that it is the designers that must be educated first, and Norman’s scrutiny of hollow business placation through buzz concepts like the TBL trend.

Savitz, A. (2013). The Triple Bottom Line: How Today’s Best-Run Companies Are Achieving Economic, Social and Environmental Success and How You Can Too. John Wiley & Sons, Incorporated. http://ebookcentral.proquest.com/lib/cwu/detail.action?docID=1495625

  • (Savitz, 2013) “executives must consider the social, economic, and environmental impacts on anyone with a stake in the outcome.”
  • (Savitz, 2013) recounts the business disasters of Hershey in following the traditional bottom-line profit pursuit, citing poor social and environmental company choices in 2002 and 2012.
  • Call to action source that embraces the core idea of the TBL through authenticity.
  • Important to establish an association of necessary consideration of sustainability concepts in business.

Stahel, W. (2016). Circular economy. Nature, 531, 435–438. https://doi.org/10.1038/531435a

  • In-depth commentary on the importance of sustainability and factors associated with changing the contemporary traditionalist conceptualization’s course to a circular economy approach. It posits that changing the relationships of goods and materials would save resources and energy while creating local jobs.
  • Call to action content with backed status-quo examples that at the least endorses TBL core concept of considering people, planet, and profits or policy, sustainability, and economy.

Sustainability Illustrated (Director). (2014, April 8). Triple bottom line (3 pillars): Sustainability in business. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2f5m-jBf81Q

  • (Sustainability Illustrated, 2014) This video outlining the TBL pillars explains how they overlap in consideration of sustainability while denoting the hierarchy of dependency of the three: environmental over Social and Social over Economy.
  • Call-to-action resource that presents a common-sense approach to the dependency of TBL’s profit requiring social support and societies’ innate reliance on nature, logically organizing ecological resources as the primary dependency for those looking to make a profit in business.

Vigon, B. W., Tolle, D. A., Cornaby, B. W., Latham, H. C., Harrison, C. L., Boguski, T. L., Hunt, R. G., & Sellers, J. D. (2020). Life-Cycle Assessment: Inventory Guidelines and Principles (1st ed.). CRC Press. https://doi.org/10.1201/9781003075554

  • (Vigon et al., 2020) provides guidelines for all stages and processes associated with Life Cycle Analysis.
  • An authority on considerations for LCA processes, standards, and functions, helpful in adding authority and credibility to the proposal.

Ecological Footprint. (n.d.). Global Footprint Network. Retrieved March 11, 2024, from https://www.footprintnetwork.org/our-work/ecological-footprint/

  • (Ecological Footprint, n.d.) explains the process and metric of Ecological Footprint and how it factors into Sustainability.
  • Potential consolidated metric to summarize accounting for sustainability impacts with GWP and ADP.
