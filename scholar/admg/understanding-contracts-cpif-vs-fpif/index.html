About this paper

An ADMG 476 Assignment 3 walkthrough across five parts: working three incentive-fee contract scenarios (one Cost Plus Incentive Fee, two Fixed Price Incentive Fee), choosing the right procurement option across five demand and risk situations, sorting ten make-versus-buy triggers, validating ten contract-type selection criteria, and closing with an essay on the advantages and disadvantages of single-source supplier strategy.

Why it matters to the practice

Procurement and contract type selection is one of the project management decisions with the longest tail. CPIF and FPIF mechanics, sharing ratios, ceiling prices, and where incentive fees actually trigger are the kind of detail you only really understand once you've worked the numbers by hand. The make-versus-buy and contract-criteria checklists in Parts 3 and 4 are the practical reference that keeps showing up in studio work, especially around when to take a contracted project versus build a capability in-house. The single-source essay is the trade-off conversation in long form.

Part 2. Procurement Option Selection

For each of the situations below, select the most likely option that would be selected.

  1. You have the capability to produce the product. If the marketplace likes your product and demand increases sharply, the demand will most likely exceed your manufacturing capability. To minimize future risks, you decide to select one supplier as a backup and give them a small portion of the production contract. In this case, you would most likely be using option (C) Procure only a small portion of the goods/services.
  2. The demand for your product has exceeded expectations and you have decided to outsource all of the work. To minimize your dependency (the risk of one contractor eventually “holding you hostage”), you would most likely select option (B) Procure all goods/services from multiple sources.
  3. You have performed a make-buy analysis and discovered that it is more economical for you to manufacture the product yourself. The most likely option would be (D) Procure none of the goods/services.
  4. Your company maintains a list of preferred suppliers, all of whom could individually satisfy your production requirements. However, one supplier has continuously tried to keep costs down for the benefit of both you and the supplier. The most likely option would be (A) Procure all goods/services from a single source.
  5. Your company had decided to manufacture all of the goods in-house. However, you have just won a large contract which may necessitate that the remaining portion of the production be outsourced so that manufacturing capability will exist for the new project. The most likely option selected would be (C) Procure only a small portion of the goods/services.

Part 5. Single-Source Supplier Strategy

Using a single source supplier has many benefits, including lower costs due to less management overhead (Costantino & Pellegrino, 2010). Utilizing single suppliers reduces supply chain risks such as quality, contractual, and management risks (Nelson, 2013). Since the manager of partnering arrangements is tasked with developing and aligning the organizations around common goals and processes (Darnall & Preston, 2018), it is beneficial to management to have the time investment concentrated in a single source. This ensures correct tracking, communications, and alignment, and allows closer monitoring of quality. The trust relationship between buyer and seller may be allowed to grow with greater reliance on each other.

Since a records management system will be required to track all paperwork, it will be less complex to incorporate one supplier than many, which positively impacts budget and schedule. Single-supplier contracts will be easier to control and more predictable to manage for costs. Additionally, there are increased chances to innovate processes, improve bargaining power, and improve supplier responsiveness (Nelson, 2013).

Disadvantages do present themselves. Organizations are at greater risk if the supplier defaults or mismanages and does not fulfill their responsibilities (Costantino & Pellegrino, 2010). There is increased dependency on a single vendor and increased capacity risks (Nelson, 2013). If a single supplier experiences an unknown risk, then enacted risk responses will likely be more challenging, as gaining new relationships and contracts quickly may be difficult and costly.
