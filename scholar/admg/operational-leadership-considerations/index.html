About this paper

A three-part operational-leadership case study: organizational culture at a major Pacific-Northwest game studio (drawing on an informational interview with friends who worked there), a critique of the Central Washington University Veterans Center diversity policy as the embedded student worker, and an assessment of the DiversityInc Top 50 framework against the Portolese, Upperman, and Trumpy textbook.

Why it matters to the practice

This was a working leader's view of culture and diversity, not a checkbox exercise. The studio analysis still informs how I think about studio culture for Archkey, and the Veterans Center critique fed directly into Battle-Buddy's product thinking: short statements feel safe, but they often skip the value diversity actually delivers, which is better service, better communication, and better solutions.

Connections

Threads into Archkey Studio's culture pillars and the Forge Framework's section on values and team identity. Also informs Battle-Buddy's Veteran-services voice: speaking from inside the Veterans Center as a worker, not from outside as an observer.

Company Culture: A Major Game Studio

Having close friends that work at the organization and having conducted an informational interview, I was interested in taking a closer look at the organizational culture. The studio’s values are known and adhered to by employees largely. As I assess the list of values, I find them interesting and atypical compared to other organizations: teams are stronger than heroes, player experience first, strong ideas loosely held, closing is an everyday practice, widen your perspective, keep it fun, put a dent in the universe (employer review platform, 2021).

Through my informational interview and research, including industry-press coverage of working conditions at the studio (industry press, 2020), I observed that the organization does hire people who are in keeping with their values. “Strong ideas loosely held,” trusting teams, and keeping it fun are particularly consistent with a positive organizational culture. They are also noted as having a competitive benefits package (employer review platform, 2021). From my own personal experience, they “provide things that make your team comfortable at work, ergonomic chairs, coffee, soft drinks” (Portolese et al., 2018), and the office area includes even more with snacks and food. In terms of keeping stress down, they have a solid work-life balance (industry press, 2020) and include free membership to the gym located just next door to the offices.

Diversity Policy for an Organization

I chose to look closer at the diversity policy for the organization where I work, the CWU Veterans Center. The policy reads:

We value diversity and ensure our work is reflective of an inclusive environment. We are committed to: ensuring equitable access to applicable resources, advocating on behalf of students to ensure their accommodations are met. We are committed to providing an inclusive space to enhance learning, inspire creativity, and positively support the individual’s overall success (CWU, 2021).

I think that the statement is well formed and effective. It includes concepts of multiculturalism, as it focuses on inclusiveness. It notes a dedication to access and accommodations. The statement is short, and could likely be more effective if there were additional statements pertaining to the value diversity offers, such as having better service, communication, and the development of more effective ideas and solutions.

DiversityInc Top 50

Studying DiversityInc’s criteria reveals that it is in keeping with the information gained from reading The Art of Leadership and Supervision (Portolese et al., 2018). Particularly interesting is the adherence to aspects of social responsibility. DiversityInc uses “six key areas of diversity and inclusion management” (DiversityInc, 2021): Human Capital Diversity metrics, Leadership Accountability, Talent Programs, Workplace Practices, Supplier Diversity, and Philanthropy (DiversityInc, 2021). The current (2021) top five companies for diversity are: 1. Hilton, 2. Accenture, 3. Eli Lilly and Company, 4. Abbott, and 5. Mastercard.
