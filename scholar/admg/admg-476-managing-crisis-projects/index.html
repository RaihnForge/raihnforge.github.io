About this paper

Capstone assignment for ADMG 476, written around three life-cycle phases of crisis: accountability, response, and integration. The paper studies four real cases at length, Johnson & Johnson's 1982 and 1986 Tylenol response, Air France Concorde, the Russian submarine Kursk, and Intel's Pentium chip, and argues from them that crisis behavior is what determines whether an organization ends up framed as victim or villain in public memory.

Why it matters to the practice

ADMG 476 is the course where the distinction between risk and unknowns finally lands as a working idea, not a definition. Most of what I now treat as risk is actually known and tractable. Crisis is what happens when the unknowable converges with leadership that hesitates. The paper's framing, quick accountability, transparent response, and disciplined lessons-learned integration, is the same loop I now use for studio incidents and client emergencies.

Connections

Pairs directly with the Schwalbe seven habits and Tuckman material in ADMG 374 and ADMG 477, and with the cost and scope discipline of ADMG 474. Carver's three R's, regret, reason, remedy, has become the default frame I reach for in any incident write-up. The accountability standard set by James Burke during Tylenol is the standard I now hold sponsors and PMs to in the Forge Framework's incident protocol.

Managing Crisis Projects

Editor
Claude (Opus 4.7)

An organization must be accountable, responsive, and integrate understanding in a crisis situation for appropriate management. It is important to understand the difference between risks and unknowns and how to approach their management differently (Paladin Risk, 2014). Project risk denotes factors that may benefit or negate the viability of maintaining the project’s baseline and successfully completing deliverables within constraints, most often scope, cost, and schedule (Meyer et al, 2002). Unknowns are defined as unforeseeable occurrences (Darnall et al, 2018). Unforeseen circumstances may still be addressed through proper understanding of crisis management (Paladin Risk, 2014). Crisis management is defined as “a significant threat to operations that can have negative consequences if not handled properly” (Coombs, 2007). Failing to consider these factors and how they correlate to the life-cycle phases of a crisis may determine that the organization are villains in the eyes of the public instead of the victims of a crisis (Kerzner, 2017). This discernment may determine the fate of an organization.

Accountability

During a crisis management situation, it is imperative that an organization quickly designate a leader and accept responsibility for their actions. According to the life cycle phase, early warnings may have already been observed, but not acted on adequately enough to prevent a crisis from occurring. There may have been known unknowns that weren’t correctly accounted for, or perhaps an unknown that could not be foreseen. Despite the contributing factors the organization must define leadership and recognize responsibility during a crisis (Kerzner, 2017).

Leadership of a crisis team should comprise of the executive project sponsor and the highest executive viable. Organizations whose top-level management and sponsors immediately take on leadership of a crisis fair far greater than those that do not (Kerzner, 2017). The examples of steadfast leadership accepting their role and taking immediate control of the situations reveal a human-centric philosophy that puts aside business mentalities and increases their successful handling of dire circumstances. As it is the case that stakeholder relationships are extremely important during a crisis, having strong leadership ensures that stakeholders are prioritized, and needs are met with the greatest possible care.

It is essential that a company immediately assumes responsibility for a crisis. Neglecting responsibility slows reaction and may cause the situation to progress even more negatively, causing even greater harm, as evidenced by the Russian submarine Kursk incident where lives could have been saved had President Vladimir Putin allowed outside assistance more swiftly (Kerzner, 2017). Additionally, the public will have more unfavorable opinions of the organization if they disregard or do not recognize the crisis, as is the case regarding the errors found in Intel’s Pentium chip (Kerzner, 2017).

These qualities were exemplified by Johnson & Johnson CEO James Burke during the Tylenol poisonings of 1982 and 1986. Burke took immediate action and brought himself to the forefront of the situation and took responsibility (Kerzner, 2017). He organized his crisis committee and prioritized stakeholder needs quickly by following the organization’s credo (Kerzner, 2017). Chairman of Air France, Jean-Cyril Spinetta, is another example of adhering to appropriate leadership and accountability for the Air France Concorde flight that crashed on takeoff (Kerzner, 2017).

In contrast, these lessons were not observed by President Vladimir Putin during the sinking of the nuclear-powered submarine Kursk that claimed 118 crew members as casualties (Kerzner, 2017). Putin’s neglect, lack of leadership, unaccountability, and dismissive behavior during this event resulted in much anger from his constituents, especially upon his visit to the home base of the crewmembers where he encountered open hostility (Kerzner, 2017).

Response

Timely action, transparent communication, and implementing a human-centric and compassionate response is crucial to responsible management of a crisis. The life-cycle components of problem understanding, damage assessment, and crisis resolution cannot be effectively achieved without being enthusiastically addressed. It is important to consider initial crisis response guidelines that include being quick, accurate, and consistent (Coombs, 2007). The court of public opinion is a deciding factor that must be ethically approached during the response, as it is often what determines if the organization is vilified or considered a victim (Kerzner, 2017).

It is imperative that an organization acts hastily and honestly when a crisis occurs. Acting quickly is vital to mitigate damaging effects and potential victims of the situations. Preparation and assessment of known unknowns can be done similarly to the more common risk management actions of a project (Paladin Risk, 2014). Organization and action is pivotal to ensuring success.

To be as prompt as possible, a crisis committee should be formed and composed of the senior most levels of management (Kerzner, 2017). This allows for the organization to take quick action by removing communication barriers and hesitation over executive intent. Leaders may also more effectively and quickly form relations with external organizations, including governmental, in order to determine problem understanding, damage assessment, and crisis resolution more hastily.

Effective communication is extremely important. In order to come to a proper understanding of the problem and thus an effective damage assessment and crisis resolution, it is imperative to have open and honest communications. All information should be considered open to the public to increase integrity. It should also be noted that words must be followed through by actions that reflect their authenticity (Kerzner, 2017). It is recommended to apply internet communication directly to address circumstances and deliver this pertinent information via a dedicated website (Coombs, 2007).

When handling the media, communicating clearly and openly allows the generation of trust from the public as well as other stakeholders. Stephen Carver advises to adhere to the three R’s rule. Regret, reason, and remedy comprise the three components (Cranfield School of Management, 2013). Demonstrating regret in a human and honest way communicates proper prioritization of the human element over financial aspirations. Providing reason, even if it is unknown, demonstrates openness and willingness to take corrective action. Presenting a planned course of action to remedy the crisis situation exemplifies the motivation and willingness to develop and take part of a meaningful solution to the circumstance.

Showing authentic compassion for those who may have been affected or injured during a crisis is imperative. In the case of the Air France Concorde crash, the CEO Jean-Cyril made the effort to attend the memorial of the victims of the crash. Conversely, British Airways did not act or assume responsibility and did not show compassion. The aftermath of this event highlights the importance of empathizing with those affected by crisis, as Air France’s organization faired much better in the aftermath (Kerzner, 2017).

CEO of Johnson & Johnson James Burke has become the standard in handling these phases in the crisis management life cycle as he demonstrated that corporate social responsibility must be a much higher priority than corporate profitability. Burke responded quickly and took charge of his crisis team and became the spokesman for his organization during the Tylenol poisoning crisis. He quickly orientated and assembled a crisis response committee, developed phases to work through the project that closely mirror what is used today. Burke also used his position to communicate to the public and authorities openly and honestly, developing trusting relationships with both simultaneously. His adherence to the organization’s credo allowed him timely prioritization of stakeholders and clarity of action (Kerzner, 2017).

Putin’s handling of the Russian submarine Kursk event as well as Intel are examples of dishonesty and ambiguity making crisis situations much worse. Putin allowed misinformation to be disseminated to the press, refused to be visible at the crisis site, and did not show adequate remorse for those affected. Intel did not respect the impact that chip errors had on the public and allowed the situation to become much worse (Kerzner, 2017). These occurrences reflect a dismissive, unconcerned attitude toward ethics and lack a human-centric response that caused organizational failure and setbacks.

Integration

Capturing lessons learned is essential for ensuring future success, adapting organizational credos, and conducting to adhere to more responsible behavior while continuing to achieve success. In order to avoid or better address future crises, it is vital that organizations learn from their own experiences, others in their industry, and the history of crisis (Kerzner, 2017). The life cycle phase of lessons learned can only be properly addressed if there is a well-documented paper trail in order to review the situation and gain insight and perspective on how to prevent and react to future situations. Action plans in cybersecurity incidents require after-action responses and adaptation of lessons learned during an attack in order to better anticipate and protect against future events and gain greater security (State of Connecticut, 2018). This is a similar approach to what we must take in order to avoid potential crisis from occurring, and to modify our organization’s plan and preparation to address them properly.

Conclusion

It is important to spend the time in consideration of crisis management and prevention. It is also understood that investment in prevention is much less costly than management of a crisis once it initiates (Paladin Risk, 2014). There are no organizations that are immune to crisis. They will occur (Cranfield School of Management, 2013). Interestingly, extremely successful and large organizations are more apt to experience crisis, and these are more affected by public opinion. Management master Peter Drucker coined the phrase, “the failure of success,” to highlight this phenomenon. It is paramount that we all take heed to the principles of managing crisis to ensure unhesitating accountability, quick, open, and effective response, as well as responsible integration of lessons learned.
