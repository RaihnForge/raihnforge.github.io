Teaming: Tuckman's Model and the Agile / Waterfall Split

Author
Joshua Keyes
Professor
Professor Dr. Laura Williamson
Date
May 20, 2023
Module
L4 — Mod 04
Editor
Claude (Opus 4.7)

This article will discuss the shared management and development of high-performance teams in relation to Tuckman’s Model for Nurturing a Team to High Performance. The model has five clear stages or factors: Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing, and Adjourning. The important factors to building a high-performance agile team and a traditional waterfall project management approach team can be subtly different in focus of characteristics desired in team members.

Five Factors

The five stages of Tuckman’s Model were developed by a psychologist named Bruce Tuckman in 1965 (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023). The concept aligns closely with other team forming theories such as the US Army’s stages of team building: formation, development, and sustainment (Portolese et al, 2018). Both the US Army model and Tuckman’s Model coincide with the team-building stages defined in The Art of Leadership and Supervision as Formation Stage, Enrichment Stage, and Sustainment Stage (Portolese et al, 2018). The following outlines Tuckman’s Model in more depth.

Forming. This is the beginning of the process of gathering a team in hopes of becoming high performance. It requires most from the leader of the team to ensure that expectations and goals are communicated well. The leader helps to identify roles but also allows enough space for all members of the new team to feel out where they are most valuable and how well their skills fit in the team. Purpose unites the team as they find their place (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023).

Storming. As with any new formation there will initially be some aspects of communication that are missing from previous situations that the members had grown used to. Different people may have filled differing roles and responsibilities, and accountability expectations and norms have changed. This often leads to friction amongst the team, and boundaries will be pushed as members are uncomfortable (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023). The team leader’s role here will be very important to solve issues and strategize around the unique identity of the team. Again, the aim is to align the team to the purpose while promoting the I to T transformation of allowing each member to understand the benefits of their deep knowledge and how it applies to the broader team and even broader project delivery (Karaj, 2013).

Norming. The norming phase is the result of the hard work done in the Storming phase. Friction should be drastically reduced as problems are solved carefully. Members will recognize their value and the particular skill sets they should accentuate and those they allow others to fulfill, which reduces friction (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023). The team leader will have proven their worth and respect for decisions will have grown along with the team’s understanding of the needs of individuals. Constructive feedback is shared amongst the team members and accepted as trust grows. The progress toward deliverables and goals should be steady (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023).

Performing. This is the ideal stage for team production. Team leaders should be participating in continuous coaching, which assists in the generation of social capital, which in turn improves the key antecedents of highly effective project teams: collaboration and information sharing, empowerment of team through development, psychological safety, trust, and shared outcomes (Ahiaga-Dagbui et al, 2020). In this stage the members will have gained trust with their fellows and begin to speak more openly. Communication is a key component of any team, so this will assist in identifying bottlenecks and issues more rapidly as well as creative solutions to help adjust for success (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023).

Adjourning. As projects conclude or tasking is reassigned it must be acknowledged that there will be a stage dedicated to the natural mourning process (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023). As psychological safety and trust increases from coaching, social capital will rise. When this team environment changes with inevitable re-assignment to a different team, there will be those that once again are placed in a less psychologically safe environment where there is less trust evident. This is an uncomfortable process that can be mitigated through appropriate leadership that reinforces organizational mission, vision, and values.

Notable Differences

The agile approach to project management prioritizes different elements than the traditional waterfall approach to project management. Therefore, it is important to respect the differences in needs when forming a high-performance team.

The waterfall approach requires a great deal more documentation, change management control, and focuses decision making at the top of hierarchies (Financieras, 2020). This means that teams on traditional projects will include less necessity for the broad scope understanding. While it is still beneficial, those with deep experience and knowledge on more specific roles are required.

Agile methodologies utilize quick sprints or decision-making cycles that allow for the participation of and input of all those involved. It was developed as technology and software project needs proved that the traditional method was not flexible enough to keep up with the extremely dynamic environment (Darnall, 2018). It is a flatter control system that benefits greatly from team members having better communication and broad scope knowledge (Financieras, 2020). This is where it is important to consider team members who have the I-to-T mentality, as they will have the characteristics that allow for better performing agile production (Karaj, 2013).

Developing high-performance teams that allow for greater productivity is essential to being successful. It is especially important to engender the key antecedents of highly effective project teams through coaching so that you may build trust and psychological safety with team members. The Tuckman Model will help ensure coaching is applied appropriately so teams may be the best they possibly can. These are essential in the application of the agile methodology of project management that dominates the IT industry.

Author’s Notes

The literature for this module confuses the context of the question further. “Building high-performing and integrated project teams” (Ahiaga-Dagbui et al, 2020) is an in-depth look at bolstering key antecedents of highly effective project teams: collaboration and information sharing, empowerment of team through development, psychological safety, trust, and shared outcomes, by deriving greater social capital via coaching.

“Seven advantages of working nimbly in the world of work” (Financieras, 2020) gives seven benefits of working in an agile culture. These are factors not of building an agile team, but benefits of adopting an agile approach.

The first video in the module is labeled “How to build a high performing agile team,” but actually links to a video titled “From I to T: Collaboration of Roles in Agile Teams” (Karaj, 2013). This video gives a less than two-minute visual overview of agile benefits and related roles. It also explains the concept of I to T. The “I” represents functional roles with deep knowledge. The skills learned through collaboration to instantiate broad knowledge are represented by the horizontal topping line. Together they form the T of broad knowledge and deep knowledge coming together. This seems to be a symbolism of traditional waterfall approach requiring deep knowledge only while others in the control roles of the hierarchy manage the broader picture. The transformation is that in agile all members are also included in control roles and are responsible for broad understanding as well as having deep knowledge to enact functional change in the delivery of the project.

The last video relates again to the image posted and is titled “Tuckman’s Model for Nurturing a Team to High Performance,” which actually links to an article with a video within it. This article relates to Tuckman’s Model and its application.
