About this paper

A long-form analysis of a real B2B procurement engagement from the buyer's side: a SOC 2 auditing firm being evaluated for selection. The frameworks of the course (Trust-Based Selling, ADAPT, SPIN, BATCH, SIER, LAARC) are applied to the conversation as it actually happened, not as a textbook exercise.

Why it matters to the practice

Most of the procurement decisions I make in the studio and in Ed-Tech work look like this engagement: a small organization assessing a specialized vendor, where the relationship matters as much as the product. The frameworks gave me language for what I was already doing instinctively, and clarified the spots where the salesperson's craft was carrying me or failing me. The takeaways generalize: the rep who builds trust slowly outperforms the one who rushes the close, sales aids are not vanity but information retention, and a bad handoff between sales and account management can undo a successful close. These show up in every services and software deal I have been on either side of.

Connections

Sits inside the broader RMT track on customer relationships and influence. Also feeds the ADMG side of the program: vendor selection is a project, and the procurement workflow described here is the operational expression of the same management concepts applied elsewhere in coursework. Names have been generalized to protect the salesperson and vendor; the analysis is unchanged.

TO: Robert Lupton FROM: Joshua Keyes DATE: June 2, 2023 SUBJECT: The Buyer’s Experience, Final

Product Information

One of my responsibilities as project manager for my employer is assisting in the vetting of potential vendors that are involved in our SOC 2 certification project. I was responsible for leading the search for the needed auditing firm that would be a good fit for our organization’s specialization and processes. We have already been utilizing a compliance monitoring platform as a consulting and monitoring vendor for certification readiness. When it came time for audit selection, that platform recommended several firms to us, and the vendor described below was one of them. I scheduled an appointment and met with the vendor as part of our procurement process on Friday, November 4, at ten in the morning. The sales representative was the point of contact. The meeting was conducted virtually through Zoom (Keyes, 2022).

Trust-Based Selling

The sales representative focused on Trust-Based Selling. Trust-Based Selling involves the salesperson presenting themselves as a consultative party to the prospective buyer (Rockley, 2021). The salesperson must earn the trust of the buyer and meet customer needs (Module 1 Slides, 2023). This differs from transaction-focused traditional selling in that the focus is on solutionizing, revealing opportunity, and bringing continued value to the customer relationship over time (Module 1 Slides, 2023), in lieu of pursuing a hard sale which utilizes high-pressure methods, a short-term relationship, and immediate transaction for goods or services (Kenton, 2023).

The process of Trust-Based Selling includes Selling Foundations, Selling Strategy based on customer needs and value, Initiating Customer Relationships, Developing Customer Relationships, and Enhancing Customer Relationships (Module 5 Slides, 2023). The representative did an excellent job of demonstrating trustworthiness (Barret, 2008) and adhering to the Trust-Based Selling process.

The rep did well in explaining the features, function, and benefits (Module 3 Slides, 2023) of the service and product. They demonstrated that what truly made the product remarkable (Howard, 2021) was the connection with clients that allowed for coaching opportunities and bespoke solutions for our organization. This meant that our inexperienced team would rest assured we would have ample assistance and guidance throughout the process while helping our team to learn through the experience. This would result in a quicker, higher-quality, and enriching outcome. Another great feature of the service was the ability to directly interface with our existing compliance monitoring platform. This meant a considerable amount of time savings with higher resulting organization that respected employee time investment. The rep understood that customers buy benefits (KO Sales Coach, 2015). Through these highlights, they adequately promoted the benefits over persuasion through emotional connection (Smith, 2017).

The representative gained my trust through the utilization of Trust Builders: Expertise, Dependability, Candor, Customer Orientation, and Compatibility (Module 2 Slides, 2023). They were on time to meetings and quick to respond to scheduling. They answered all of my questions with confidence, concern, and care. Throughout the discussion they demonstrated expertise in the eight knowledge bases for sales professionals: Industry, Company, Product, Service, Price, Promotion, Market/Customer Knowledge, Competitors, and Technology (Avila et al., 2017). Specifically, the rep relied most on expertise in Product and Industry, with strong knowledge of team capabilities, the underlying platform, cybersecurity, and the needs of smaller tech organizations, which was of great value and concern to my organization.

Communications Skills

The communication skills of the sales representative were not quite as refined as their trust-building skills. However, successful communication was achieved as a team effort. The rep gave a brief overview of the firm and their auditing process but lacked queries that would better associate my organization in application.

my employer’s side of the conversation was keener on addressing a logic-based funneling sequence of questions that identified and addressed our organizational needs by presenting details and expectations (Change Minds, 2023). Ideally this is initiated through the salesperson utilizing ADAPT: Assess, Discover, Activate, Project, and Transition (Module 3 Slides, 2023). The rep’s lack of initiation forced our team to probe for discovery.

As prospectors for vendors, it is in our interest to also find a fit for our needs. Therefore, I initiated questioning that related closely to the SPIN framework: Situation, Problem, Implication, and Need & Payoff (Module 3 Slides, 2023). I presented the situation and problems and petitioned information regarding how the vendor might satisfy our need as well as what the payoff would be for my organization. It was of great interest and import that I delved into specifics in application (Change Grow Achieve, 2020) to ensure the best fit for my team. This relates well to BATCH: Bottlenecks, Affect to Others, Time, Cost, and Hassles (Change Grow Achieve, 2020). The framework is utilized to discover greater understanding of possible solutions during and assisting in the Implication discussion (Change Grow Achieve, 2020).

Discovering, highlighting, and annotating our process in comparison with the vendor’s, while also taking into account organizational values and how they aligned with ours, was essential. Through the questions on implementation, we discovered more on the vendor’s use of the shared compliance documentation and framework. We determined its suitability to our current organizational structure and approach. We also identified that the vendor used ClickUp as their task resolution management system of choice. Together we found that it would fit easily in our capabilities to adopt during the auditing process. Their methods being satisfactorily unobtrusive, along with their willingness to commit to a high frequency of communication and coaching, assuaged my concerns regarding confusing expectations or miscommunication (Keyes, 2022).

The lack of SPIN and BATCH method initiation by the representative was a bit disappointing, but by following my lead they intuited that some trust had been lost and responded by applying excellent active-listening skills. The SIER Hierarchy of Active Listening details four stages designed to assist in building effective relationships through effective communication (World of Work Project, 2023). The stages are Sensing, Interpreting, Evaluating, and Responding (Module 3 Slides, 2023). By reflecting on these we can determine why the rep effectively maintained my interest and engagement.

  • Sensing. Response to verbal and non-verbal cues was outstanding. The rep matched tone and energy, maintaining engagement. They maintained attention during the video call and, very importantly, had their camera turned on so that I could see that they were reacting to me. This was not the case in all of my meetings with potential vendors for this project, much to my surprise and disappointment.
  • Interpreting. Maintaining context and matched rhythms of the conversation on remote meetings can be difficult. Professional calls on Zoom are even less personal and have fewer situational cues than traditional meetings. Therefore it can be easier to either be too disconnected and robotic, or too unprofessional at the wrong times. The rep was dressed and presented appropriately for the meeting and met professionalism standards while being flexible enough to be slightly more casual during appropriate times, as is culturally appropriate given the industry, locations, and forum.
  • Evaluating. The rep allowed plenty of time and space for assessment. My words were carefully assessed and responded to. If there were any issues as to clarity of my statement or concerns, they inquired to gain better understanding.
  • Responding. Ultimately, the rep responded excellently to my inquiries with complete and thoughtful answers, insights, and demonstration of knowledge. It was clear that they processed accurately and satisfied my informational needs.

The reinvestment and energizing engagement revealed to me that the rep was committed and skilled in communication. Together we were dedicated to discovering if the vendor would be a good fit for my employer’s auditing needs.

Sales Dialogue: Creating & Communicating Value

During the conversation, I found the vendor’s commitment to communication to be ambiguous. I was uncertain about how much assistance they would render if concerns were found during the auditing process. This point was very important to our organization, since we had not gone through an audit before and this would be the first time for many working on our team. The rep utilized checkback questions during the discussions to generate feedback from me and gauge levels of interest (Module 5 Slides, 2023). They questioned how many people were in my organization, the complexity of our product and services in relation to stakeholders, and our technology requirements. My responses allowed them to leverage my situation and reveal that the solutions the vendor could execute would be ideal. This included responsive communication in weekly meetings with auditors for clarity in expectations, coaching in framework and testing purposes, and the use of ClickUp as an asynchronous, flexible task communication and tracking management system to allow for greater flexibility for both parties.

Sales aids are excellent at communicating value, and in this area the vendor unfortunately performed terribly. There are many types of sales aids, including visual materials, electronic materials, and product demonstrations (Module 6 Slides, 2023). With effective sales aids, a seller would implement the SPES Sequence: State the selling point and introduce the sales aid, Present the sales aid, Explain the sales aid, then Summarize (Module 6 Slides, 2023). There are many reasons for using sales aids, including increased attention, interest, persuasion, involvement, collaboration, understanding, validity, information retention, professional projection, and solutions focus (Module 6 Slides, 2023).

While multiple sales aids can create a great positive impact (Module 6 Slides, 2023), the rep had none for me, even when requested. I had wanted to see an example of what a final SOC 2 report from the vendor might look like, and they stated that they did not have one on hand but would follow up with me to deliver one through email. Even though I reminded them later in emails, I still never received the example document. I also would have liked to see a visualization of the vendor’s process. I was able to annotate what their process looked like through discussion, but it would have been to their benefit if they had a slide deck ready to illustrate and communicate the stages involved more clearly. Additionally, having a one-pager to leave behind of features and benefits would have been enormously helpful in information retention.

The rep did, however, have powerful Proof Providers on their side. Proof Providers can be statistics that validate value and claims, testimonials of satisfied clients and partners, and case histories in story or anecdotal form (Module 6 Slides, 2023). Because the vendor was a sister company to our existing compliance monitoring platform, they were able to cite many statistics, testimonials, and stories of previous clients. They had an excellent team with very experienced auditors and experts who had been working to assist organizations in pursuit of greater security compliance for many years. They had worked with several parties with much less knowledge and experience than our team to successful certification. Throughout the course of our trust-building cycle these were cited and referenced.

Addressing Concerns & Earning Commitment

Some of the objections and concerns I raised through the earning-commitment process included how much one-on-one communication would be available; what would happen if there were deficiencies found through the audit process; and what level of accountability, investment, and expectation the vendor would assert in the outcome of successful SOC 2 certification acquisition.

The rep performed excellently in their approach to sales resistance. The aspects of the LAARC acronym (Module 6 Slides, 2023) were respected. They Listened to my objections, Acknowledged my concern, Assessed the issue at hand, Responded thoughtfully, and Confirmed understanding for each. In addressing communications concerns, the rep iterated timely and clear communication with a commitment to one-on-one meetings with coaching and clear expectation setting. The rep explained that deficiencies in audit would be worked through as long as necessary to the satisfaction of my employer, with remediation opportunities and amendments if necessary. Finally, they assured me that the level of commitment to a successful audit was mutual, as it is also in the best interest as well as the primary purpose of their firm.

The rep’s techniques were effective because they were measured, logical, and earnest. Ultimately they lent to the gaining of my recommendation and my employer’s commitment to purchase. The rep knew that NEPQ — Neural Emotional Persuasion Questions — were important (Jeremy Miner, 2022). They made an excellent first impression by being professional and not appearing needy (Jeremy Miner, 2022). They also seemed ready and authentically concerned with whether their firm would be the best fit for our organization, and I believe they would have said no to us if there were any issues that would have revealed the relationship as un-ideal for either party. They were comfortable to talk with and were not looking for a hard sale which would have raised my guard (Jeremy Miner, 2022).

In the conversation and resulting email interactions, the rep respected our organization’s time and needs (Iannarino, 2012). They did not move too fast or too slow, which helped them earn our organization’s commitment. They used the Sales Commitment Technique of Future Order (Module 6 Slides, 2023) by prompting us on our timeline for when they could schedule the scoping call to initiate the auditing process. Throughout the process the greatest Principle of Persuasion leveraged was the principle of authority: “people will follow credible, knowledgeable experts” (Influence At Work, 2012). I learned much about the vendor’s highly experienced and credible team, and later validated the information through our compliance monitoring platform contact and additional research references.

Notably, after committing and beginning the contracting processes, our team was passed to a different representative to finish contract negotiations. This person had not personally put in the work to deserve commitment and violated our trust (Iannarino, 2012). They moved too fast and did not value our organizational structure and established communication channels. They did not respect my continuous communications that I was working with my team for finalization of the contract, and chose to email our CEO directly. I raised my concerns immediately to both the platform and the vendor. The response from both in support and understanding of my dismay, as well as the resulting immediate action from the vendor, saved our commitment, and we finalized the contract for products and services.

Overall Reaction to Experience

This experience was very helpful to me moving forward, as I felt comfortable and confident in my role. Acting on behalf of my team is something I enjoy but do not take the responsibility lightly. I utilized multiple resources and consulted more experienced team members and mentors throughout the process. Largely through soft skills and networking I was able to validate the vendor’s Proof Providers through evidence of my own procured from my customer success manager (CSM) contact at our compliance monitoring platform.

Additionally, due to the vendor and platform’s close relationship, it was evident that both vendors would have to hold each other accountable in support of the other. This resulted in higher incentives as a buyer, since the performance of either entity could positively or negatively affect the other. This was demonstrated by the reaction of the parties when the contract representative moved too fast.

Overall the vendor demonstrated great proficiency and competence. The sales representative earned commitment through Trust-Based Selling. It is apparent that the vendor is dedicated to the Need-Satisfaction Consultative Model (Module 5 Slides, 2023). I am appreciative and confident in our choice, as I have persisted in working closely with them in our long-term relationship that continues to be of value. I have no doubt that we found our best fit.
