ENG 104 — Academic Writing II: Sustainability & Civic Engagement

May 18, 2026

Course Snapshot

  • Institution. Central Washington University.
  • Full title. Academic Writing II: Reasoning and Research on Sustainability and Civic Engagement.
  • Catalog description. “Develops skills in research-based academic argument through assignments involving evaluation, analysis, and synthesis of multiple sources.” Offered every year (Fall, Winter, Spring).
  • Credits. 5
  • Prerequisites. ENG 101 with a grade of C- or higher.
  • General-education designation. K1, Academic Writing II: Critical Reading and Responding. One of the required Knowledge Areas in the Explore and Connect section of the CWU General Education program.
  • Thematic frame. Sustainability and civic engagement. The course is one of three thematic ENG 104 variants offered alongside ENG 102 (Social Justice) and ENG 103 (Health and Current Issues); all three teach the same core competencies through a different topical lens.

Official Learning Outcomes

Quoted from the CWU course catalog. Upon completion, a student should be able to:

  1. Analyze and critique an argument, evaluating its rhetorical effectiveness and identifying underlying assumptions.
  2. Identify and synthesize high-quality sources and use them effectively in support of an argument.
  3. Take a position on an issue by developing a focused assertion based on a shared assumption, presenting evidence in support of a line of reasoning, addressing divergent stances on the issue, and using a variety of rhetorical appeals.
  4. Cite and document sources precisely and effectively according to the guidelines of a specific style manual.
  5. Describe the interrelationship between style and meaning and make adjustments to style to enhance meaning.
  6. Craft prose that conforms to academic conventions and to expectations regarding clarity, coherence, and unity.

How the Course Is Usually Organized

Academic Writing II is the second course in CWU’s two-course composition sequence. ENG 101 builds the foundational essay; ENG 104 turns it into research-based argument. The standard arc runs in three movements:

  • Reading rhetorically. Encounter sustainability and civic-engagement texts (Wendell Berry’s “Local Economy” is a recurring anchor). Learn to identify thesis, evidence, assumptions, and rhetorical moves before responding to them.
  • Building the researched argument. Develop a focused position on an issue. Locate, evaluate, and synthesize credible sources. Construct a multi-source argument that engages opposing views.
  • Capstone project. A culminating research argument or presentation that integrates the term’s reading, source work, and rhetorical practice. Often paired with a reflective component on the writing process itself.

Workflow inside each movement tends to follow: prewriting → drafting → peer review → revision → final submission, with conferences and process documentation along the way.

Major Content Blocks

Thesis Development

  • Moving from topic to question to position. A thesis is a defensible claim, not a topic announcement.
  • The “focused assertion based on a shared assumption” framing of Outcome 3: the writer locates common ground with the audience, then argues outward from it.
  • Workshopping the thesis as a living statement that tightens through drafts.

Evidence and Source Use

  • Source typology: primary vs. secondary, scholarly vs. popular, advocacy vs. analysis.
  • Evaluation criteria: authority, currency, purpose, accuracy, relevance (often via the CRAAP test or a similar rubric).
  • Synthesis as the central skill: not stacking quotes but putting sources into conversation with each other and with the writer’s own claim.
  • Quote, paraphrase, summarize. When to use which, and how to integrate each smoothly.

Argument Structure

  • Classical structure (introduction, narration, confirmation, refutation, conclusion) as one available scaffold.
  • Toulmin model (claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal) as a diagnostic tool for unpacking and constructing arguments.
  • Rogerian moves for high-stakes or polarized topics: lead with what the opposing view gets right.
  • Counterargument and concession as features of strong writing rather than threats to it.

Peer Review and Workshop

  • Peer review as a structured reading practice, not a politeness exercise. The reviewer’s job is to describe what the draft is doing before suggesting what it could do differently.
  • Workshop protocols: cold reads, focused-question reads, full-letter feedback.
  • The reviewer learns as much as the writer. Reading others’ drafts trains the same muscles as revising one’s own.

Rhetorical Analysis

  • Aristotle’s appeals (ethos, pathos, logos) plus kairos (timing / occasion).
  • Audience analysis: who is being addressed, what they already believe, what would move them.
  • Genre awareness: an op-ed, a policy brief, and a scholarly article all argue, but argue differently. Reading genre conventions is part of reading the argument.
  • Stylistic analysis (Outcome 5): how sentence shape, diction, and rhythm carry meaning, not just decorate it.

Sustainability and Civic Engagement Themes

The course’s topical frame, drawn from texts and prompts across the term:

  • Local economy and place. Wendell Berry’s argument that economic, ecological, and moral health are inseparable, and that scale matters. The local as a unit of moral attention.
  • The commons. Shared resources (water, air, public land, civic institutions) and the dilemmas of stewardship.
  • Sustainability frameworks. Environmental, economic, and social pillars; intergenerational justice; the difference between sustaining and merely persisting.
  • Civic participation. What citizenship asks of writing, and what writing asks of citizenship. Public discourse as a practice, not a performance.
  • Climate, food, energy, community. The recurring substantive topics that anchor research assignments.

Cross-Cutting Concepts

The threads that run through every assignment.

  • Rhetorical appeals (ethos, pathos, logos, kairos). The toolkit for both analysis and construction.
  • Audience awareness. Every choice (word, structure, evidence, tone) is a choice in front of a specific imagined reader.
  • MLA / APA citation. Outcome 4’s “specific style manual” requirement. ENG 104 sections typically pick one (often MLA for the humanities thematic framing, APA when the topical work leans social-scientific) and teach it as a system, not a checklist.
  • Revision as practice. Drafting is not the work; revising is. The course treats revision as a separate skill with its own techniques (re-outlining, reverse-outlining, cutting before adding, reading aloud).
  • Style and clarity. Concision, parallel structure, sentence variety, active voice as a default. Style as the carrier of argument, not its decoration.
  • Process documentation. Many ENG 104 sections require a process memo, writer’s letter, or reflection alongside the final draft. The course teaches writing about writing.

Commonly Used Reference Texts

These are the standard companions for a research-based composition course. Any one of them is a usable reference if returning to the material.

  • Graff, Gerald and Cathy Birkenstein, They Say / I Say: The Moves That Matter in Academic Writing. The dominant rhetorical-moves handbook for college composition; teaches argument as conversation.
  • Berry, Wendell, Sex, Economy, Freedom, and Community (containing “Local Economy”), The Unsettling of America, What Are People For? The anchor voice for the sustainability frame.
  • Lunsford, Andrea, The Everyday Writer or Easy Writer. Common style and citation handbook.
  • Hacker, Diana and Nancy Sommers, A Writer’s Reference. The alternative standard handbook.
  • Williams, Joseph M. and Joseph Bizup, Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace. The deeper-style text many instructors draw exercises from.
  • Booth, Wayne, Gregory Colomb, and Joseph Williams, The Craft of Research. The standard text on research-question framing and source argument.
  • Pollan, Michael, The Omnivore’s Dilemma or In Defense of Food; Bill McKibben, The End of Nature or Eaarth; Rebecca Solnit, essays from Hope in the Dark. Common sustainability-and-civics readers.
  • Norton Reader or The Bedford Reader. General-purpose essay anthologies frequently used to source short readings.

Reading the Field as a Whole

A few patterns worth holding onto about academic writing as a practice.

  • Writing is thinking made portable. The act of drafting is itself how the writer figures out what they believe. The page is where the argument forms, not just where it gets recorded.
  • Sources are interlocutors, not authorities. A research paper is a structured conversation. The writer hosts the conversation, decides who speaks when, and adds their own voice at the right moments. Strong writers disagree with their sources as readily as they cite them.
  • Audience is the hidden variable. Every craft decision (vocabulary, evidence, tone, length, format) only makes sense relative to a specific reader. Beginning writers tend to imagine an “anyone” audience; the course’s quiet work is replacing that with a specific one.
  • Revision is where the writing actually happens. First drafts are raw material. The real composition (cutting, reordering, sharpening, finding what the piece was actually about) happens in revision. Treating drafts as precious slows growth.
  • Civic writing has stakes. When the topic is sustainability, local economy, or public life, the writing is participating in a real conversation about how to live. The course frames argumentation not as an exercise but as the form citizenship takes on the page.
