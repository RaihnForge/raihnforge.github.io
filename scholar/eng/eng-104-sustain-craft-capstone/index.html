About this paper

This is a pitch deck (not a prose essay) for the ENG 104 capstone, a mobile game called Sustain Craft that teaches kids sustainability literacy through Bright Green Cities and NetZero mechanics. The Wendell Berry 'Idea of a Local Economy' reading response and the Beddington Zero Energy writeup the course also produced are not yet recovered to this archive; this deck is the surviving capstone artifact. What is rendered below is a faithful description of each slide of the deck, the game concept, the mechanics, the tools used, and the reflection notes Joshua left as speaker prompts.

Why it matters to the practice

Sustain Craft is the first time I put the phrase 'games as a values-transmission medium' on paper in a school context. The mechanic, take in trash, reduce it to smog and slush, recycle the trash into building materials, refine the area with plants and water, win by restoring the animal population, is the same instinct that runs through Mecromage and now Archkey Studio: design the loop so the lesson is the action. The capstone also recorded a working-knowledge takeaway I still believe, that teaching is a form of learning, and that simplification is harder than expansion. The deck is a small artifact, but it is the cleanest early statement of an idea that the studio is still trying to live up to.

Connections

Threads forward into archkey-studio's mission as an AI-assisted game development studio whose first projects all carry an intentional values-transmission frame. The Bright Green Cities and NetZero references also pair with ADMG 285's sustainability work and with the broader Sustainability and Civic Engagement frame in eng-104-academic-writing-2-reference.md. A note on form. This artifact is a pitch deck, not a prose essay. What follows describes each section of the deck in the order it was presented, preserving the speaker notes and the verbatim character bio for the protagonist.

1. Pitch

Title. Sustain Craft, a mobile game pitch.

Capstone framing. Application of skills for positive change.

Why this medium. The deck opens by tracing the skills the game draws on: Marine Corps leader, graphic designer and illustrator, animator, game designer, project leader. The argument is that games are the natural medium for me to make a civic argument with, because I already have most of the disciplinary skills the medium demands.

Games as art. The deck makes a brief case that games are an art form, and that this matters for whether games can carry serious content.

Opportunity for understanding through gaming.

  • Target a younger audience.
  • Gaming as a positive alternative.
  • Games are powerful teaching tools.
  • Interaction is the lesson, not the wrapper.
  • Simplify larger concepts into smaller ones.
  • Use the game to introduce ideas, not exhaust them.
  • Games are fun, and fun is a delivery mechanism for values.

Target audience. Games as a learning tool for young people, used to teach values.

2. Concept

Sustain Craft. A mobile game that inspires and teaches. The core values stack is:

  • The value of sustainability.
  • Responsible use of resources.
  • Balancing an ecosystem.
  • Empowerment.

The pitch frames the medium choice plainly: games as a way to teach values, not as a delivery vehicle for an unrelated lesson.

Complex concepts the game gestures at.

  • Bright Green Cities (the optimistic strain of urban environmentalism that treats technology and density as part of the climate answer).
  • City hubs as the gameplay arenas.
  • Resource management as the core verb.
  • A NetZero future as the long-term win state.

These are listed in the deck as a citation of inspirations rather than a literature review. The capstone’s prose components (Wendell Berry’s “Idea of a Local Economy” response and the Beddington Zero Energy Development writeup) carry the academic argument; the deck carries the design argument.

3. Game Mechanics

Story and context

The deck includes a verbatim character bio for the protagonist:

“Inventor and eco gladiator, Parker travels from community to community healing the painful blights on the environment while teaching others to recycle, reuse, and develop technology for the better.”

The speaker notes describe Parker as a hopeful inventive kid, intentionally relatable to the target audience, and confirm that a shipping version of the game would include story, not just systems.

Resource model

The game is built around two paired stat tables.

Waste stats (the problem layer).

  • Trash. Trash produces smog and slush and is also the player’s primary resource. The same substance that breaks the world is what the player rebuilds it with.
  • Smog. Reduces air quality. Animals will not come out to play in smoggy zones.
  • Slush. Makes water unusable. Slush must be removed before water can be used to feed plants.
  • Hunger. Animals in the zone need to be fed.

Resource and building stats (the rebuild layer).

  • Recycled material. For building.
  • Plants. For sustaining the area.
  • Water. To feed the plants.
  • Energy. To maintain the buildings’ power needs.

Gameplay loop

The deck states the player loop in five verbs.

  1. Zone. The player is introduced to a new zone.
  2. Reduce. Remove and reduce waste in the area.
  3. Recycle. The waste is placed in recyclers.
  4. Reuse. The recycled material is used to build.
  5. Refine. Plants filter the air; water filters are built from recycled materials.

Win condition. Return the animal population and create balance in the zone. The deck frames this as the design’s argument: actions can have a positive effect, and the player can see and feel that.

4. Tools Learned

The deck closes the production side by naming the tools used to develop the prototype and assets, with a short note that the learning was as much about the program as about the medium.

  • GameMaker Studio. Game engine and runtime.
  • Tiled. Tilemap editor for the world layout.
  • Aseprite. Pixel art and animation for sprites and tile sets.
  • Photoshop. Compositing, mockups, and supporting illustration.

5. Notes

The speaker notes captured two reflections that the deck repeats verbatim, treating them as the working-knowledge takeaways of the capstone.

  • Solidify ideas through simplification. The instinct to add detail, complexity, and explanation is usually the wrong instinct. The capstone’s design work proved that the cleaner version of an idea is also the harder one to reach. Expounding and adding complication is easier than simplifying.
  • Interacting and working with material in a different way. Moving from prose to a deck, and from a deck to a playable prototype, taught the same content three different ways. Each translation surfaced something the previous form had hidden.
  • Teaching as a form of learning. Designing a game whose explicit purpose is to teach kids sustainability literacy turned out to be the most rigorous way to learn it myself.

The final slide is the standard close: thank you, and questions.
