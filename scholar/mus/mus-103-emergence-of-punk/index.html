About this paper

A short comparative on how punk manifested in the U.S. and U.K. in the second half of the 1970s, written for the W08 discussion in MUS 103. The frame is punk as a reaction against the highly produced, investor-shaped mainstream rock of the AOR era, rather than as a clean next step out of a parent genre.

Why it matters to the practice

The piece is really about what happens when a creative field gets optimized for revenue at the expense of authenticity, and what the reaction to that optimization looks like. That pattern is one I keep returning to in product, brand, and studio work. Punk is the case study; the lesson is about how movements protect themselves when an industry tries to flatten them.

Connections

Sits next to the W10 Final paper on Jackson and Dylan as the other major MUS 103 thread on this site. Bridges into the broader question of how culture polices its own authenticity, which is the same conversation I have inside the Forge Framework and in any branding work where 'authentic' becomes a marketing word instead of a real one.

Mainstream Excess as the Catalyst

Punk was a reaction to the mainstream excess and perceived inauthenticity of the hippie aesthetic, which had become highly produced and esoteric. It rebelled against the intricate, polished tracks that dominated Album-Oriented Rock (AOR). These investor-backed, DJ-minimized FM stations prioritized shorter, safer tracks to maximize advertising revenue. As investors focused more on securing profits, mainstream rock music became increasingly commercial and conservative.

The punk scene revived a do-it-yourself (DIY), authentic, edgy, garage-band style, rejecting the notion that highly produced recording studios were necessary. It also reintroduced raw, rebellious lyrics, pushing back against the commercial conservatism that had become artistically antithetical to rock’s identity.

Where It Started, Where It Caught On

While punk gained greater initial success in the UK, it originated in the U.S. A significant influence on punk came from The Velvet Underground, MC5, and Iggy Pop’s Stooges, all of whom shaped the genre’s raw, aggressive sound. Foundational UK punk rock bands included The Clash and The Buzzcocks. However, it was the Sex Pistols who are widely credited with bringing punk into mainstream attention in both countries, particularly with their infamous track “God Save the Queen.”

From Punk to New Wave

As punk evolved into New Wave in the U.S., it retained its high-energy, raw, tempo-driven rock expression while incorporating pop-friendly elements such as shorter track lengths and less controversial lyrics. The New Wave movement rebranded punk in the U.S. to distance itself from the genre’s associations with violence and rebellion, making it more commercially viable. The New York punk band Blondie initially gained recognition within the CBGB punk scene but later transitioned to New Wave, achieving massive mainstream appeal.
