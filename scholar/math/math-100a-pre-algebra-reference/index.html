MATH 100A — Pre-Algebra

May 18, 2026

Course Snapshot

  • Institution. Central Washington University.
  • Catalog description. “This course is designed to prepare students for college mathematics. Symbolic, graphical, and numeric representations will be studied to understand and apply the concepts underlying algebra.”
  • Credits. 5.
  • General-education designation. None. Per the catalog: “Grade will be XC. Credits will not be allowed toward meeting bachelor’s degree requirements.” Developmental / preparatory standing. The course appears on the transcript but does not count toward the bachelor’s degree.
  • Prerequisites. “Prerequisite: appropriate placement test score.”
  • Offered. Ellensburg campus, Fall, Winter, Spring.
  • Approach. A bridge course. The job is to repair, consolidate, and re-frame arithmetic so the student can step into MATH 100B (Beginning Algebra) and onward without the foundational gaps that derail most college-algebra attempts.

Official Learning Outcomes

Quoted from the CWU course catalog. Upon successful completion, students will be able to:

  1. Simplify and solve mathematical expressions of whole, integer, rational, and real numbers.
  2. Recognize problem situations that can be modeled by linear functions.
  3. Translate among numeric, symbolic, graphical, and real-world function representations.
  4. Understand and use the order of operations for calculations and algebraic manipulations.
  5. Apply appropriate technology to solve problems.
  6. Recognize, apply, and model the four basic operations of real numbers.
  7. Use and apply operations on decimal, percent, ratios, and rates to solve problems.
  8. Make and test hypotheses.
  9. Work on extended problems in cooperative groups.

How the Course Is Usually Organized

Pre-algebra at the developmental level tends to run in one of two passes, and CWU’s MATH 100A reads as a blend:

  • Arithmetic-first sequence. Whole numbers, then integers, then fractions, then decimals, then percents, then ratios and rates, then a soft landing into variables, expressions, and one-step equations near the end of the term.
  • Representation-first sequence. Start by introducing the four representations (numeric, symbolic, graphical, verbal / real-world) and revisit each as new number types are introduced. CWU’s outcomes (translating among representations, recognizing linear-function situations) point in this direction.

In practice the term is typically split into roughly: number sense and operations (4–5 weeks), proportional reasoning and percent (3 weeks), measurement and basic geometry (1–2 weeks), introduction to variables and linear thinking (2–3 weeks), and review.

Major Content Blocks in Outline

Integers and Operations

  • Whole numbers. Place value, rounding, estimation, the four basic operations, powers, square roots, divisibility, primes, factor trees.
  • Integers. The number line extended through zero. Sign rules for addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Why subtracting a negative is adding a positive (and the geometric intuition for it on the line).
  • Absolute value. Distance from zero. The first time the student meets a function-like operation that produces a non-negative output regardless of input sign.
  • Order of operations. PEMDAS / GEMDAS. The convention itself is the lesson: math notation is a contract among readers, not a discovery about the universe.
  • Properties. Commutative, associative, distributive, identity, inverse. The vocabulary used to justify every later algebraic move.

Fractions and Decimals

  • Fractions. Parts of a whole, parts of a set, points on a number line. Equivalent fractions, simplification via GCF.
  • Operations. Addition and subtraction (common denominators via LCM). Multiplication (numerator times numerator, denominator times denominator; cancellation as a shortcut). Division (multiply by the reciprocal; why that works).
  • Mixed numbers and improper fractions. Conversion both ways. When each form is more useful.
  • Decimals. Place value to the right of the decimal point. Terminating vs. repeating decimals as fractions in disguise.
  • Decimal operations. Alignment for addition and subtraction. Power-of-ten reasoning for multiplication and division. Rounding and estimation as quality-control checks.
  • Conversions. Fraction to decimal (long division), decimal to fraction (place value), fraction or decimal to percent (multiply by 100).

Ratios, Rates, and Proportions

  • Ratios. Comparison of two quantities. Part-to-part vs. part-to-whole.
  • Rates. Ratios with units, especially per-unit rates (miles per hour, dollars per pound). Unit rate as the canonical form for comparison.
  • Proportions. Two equal ratios. Solving by cross-multiplication; solving by scaling.
  • Applications. Recipe scaling, map scales, currency conversion, mixture problems, similar-figure reasoning.

Percent

  • Definition. Percent as “per hundred,” a special-case ratio.
  • Three core questions. What is X% of Y. X is what percent of Y. X is Y% of what. The student should be able to recognize which is which from a sentence.
  • Percent change. Increase and decrease. Why a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover.
  • Applications. Sales tax, tip, discount, markup, simple interest, commission, sales-data interpretation.

Basic Geometry and Measurement

  • Perimeter. Distance around a polygon. Special cases for rectangle, square, triangle, regular polygon. Circumference of a circle.
  • Area. Rectangle and square. Parallelogram and triangle (and why they relate). Trapezoid. Circle.
  • Volume. Rectangular prism, cylinder, sphere, cone (formulas given, used).
  • The Pythagorean theorem. First appearance for most students of an “if this then that” relationship between three quantities. Right-triangle context only at this level.
  • Measurement systems. U.S. customary (inch, foot, yard, mile; ounce, pound; cup, pint, quart, gallon) and metric (the SI prefixes and why kilo-, centi-, milli- are decimal-aligned).
  • Unit conversion. Dimensional analysis as a discipline: write the units, cancel the units, trust the result.

Introduction to Variables and Expressions

  • Variable. A letter standing for a number we do not yet know or a number that can vary.
  • Algebraic expression. A combination of numbers, variables, and operations with no equals sign.
  • Evaluating. Substituting a value and computing.
  • Simplifying. Combining like terms. Distributing.
  • Translating words to expressions. “Three more than twice a number.” “The sum of.” “The product of.” The translation step is the actual difficulty for most students at this stage.

Solving Simple Equations

  • Equation as balance. The equals sign as a statement of equivalence between two expressions, not an instruction to compute.
  • The balance metaphor. Whatever you do to one side, you do to the other.
  • One-step equations. Inverse operations (add to undo subtract, multiply to undo divide).
  • Two-step equations. Order of undoing: undo addition / subtraction first, then multiplication / division (the reverse of the order of operations for evaluation).
  • Equations with variables on both sides. Light exposure at this level. Heavy treatment lives in MATH 100B.
  • Word problems. Setting up the equation from the situation. The translation step, again.

Basic Graphing and Linear Thinking

  • The coordinate plane. Origin, axes, quadrants, ordered pairs (x, y).
  • Plotting points. Reading and writing coordinates.
  • Tables of values. Generating ordered pairs from a rule.
  • Lines. Plotting a line from a table. Recognizing linear vs. non-linear from a graph or a table.
  • Slope, informally. Rise over run. Steepness. Positive, negative, zero, undefined.
  • Linear functions in context. Cost as a function of quantity, distance as a function of time. The “starting value plus rate times input” pattern, named or unnamed depending on instructor.

Data Interpretation

  • Measures of center. Mean (arithmetic average), median (middle value of an ordered list), mode (most frequent value). When each is the better summary.
  • Range. Maximum minus minimum.
  • Reading data. Bar charts, line graphs, pie charts, frequency tables. Pulling a specific value, comparing across categories, recognizing trends.
  • Reasonableness checks. Does the answer fit the question. Estimation as the first defense against arithmetic error.

Exponents and Scientific Notation

  • Positive integer exponents. Repeated multiplication. Base, exponent, power.
  • Zero exponent. Any nonzero base to the zero is one (with the consistency argument).
  • Negative exponents. A negative exponent as the reciprocal of the positive exponent. The notation, not yet the full algebra of exponents.
  • Scientific notation. A number written as a coefficient (between 1 and 10) times a power of ten. Converting back and forth. Why scientists and engineers prefer it (orders of magnitude become visible at a glance).
  • Light arithmetic with scientific notation. Multiplication and division by combining coefficients and adding / subtracting exponents.

Cross-Cutting Concepts

The same handful of ideas keep returning across blocks. They are the actual point of the course.

  • Order of operations. A convention that lets a written expression have one and only one value. Without it, every line of algebra would be ambiguous. The student who internalizes this stops “getting different answers from the calculator” forever.
  • Equivalence. Two expressions are equivalent if they give the same value for every legal input. Most of algebra is the art of replacing an expression with a simpler equivalent one. Simplifying fractions, combining like terms, factoring, and solving are all moves on this single idea.
  • The equals sign as a balance, not an arrow. The single most consequential mental shift in the course. In arithmetic, “=” often reads as “produces” (3 + 4 = 7). In algebra, “=” reads as “is the same value as” (2x + 3 = 11). The balance reading lets the student do the same thing to both sides; the arrow reading does not. Most early algebra confusion traces back to this one.
  • Translating word problems. The bottleneck is almost never the arithmetic; it is the translation from English into symbols. Naming the unknown, identifying the relationships, and choosing the operation are the actual skill. Practice is irreplaceable.
  • Estimation and reasonableness. Before computing, predict roughly what the answer should be. After computing, check that the answer is in that neighborhood. Catches the slipped decimal, the dropped sign, the wrong key on the calculator. The habit, more than any single technique, is what separates the careful from the chronically wrong.
  • Multiple representations. A relationship can live as a table, a graph, an equation, or a verbal description. The fluent student moves among them without losing the thread. CWU’s third outcome calls this out explicitly.
  • Inverse operations. Every operation has an undo. Addition undoes subtraction. Multiplication undoes division. Squaring undoes square root (with sign caveats). Solving equations is structured undoing.

Commonly Used Reference Texts

Standard developmental-math textbooks. Any one of them is a usable companion if returning to the material later.

  • Bittinger, Marvin L., and Judith A. Beecher, Prealgebra (and Prealgebra and Introductory Algebra). The Bittinger series is the long-time workhorse of developmental math; clean exposition, abundant exercise sets.
  • Lial, Margaret L., Diana L. Hestwood, Stanley A. Salzman, and Diana Hestwood, Prealgebra. The Lial series leans heavily on stepwise worked examples and a chatty, encouraging voice.
  • McKeague, Charles P., Prealgebra: A Text/Workbook. Compact, drill-heavy, classic.
  • Martin-Gay, Elayn, Prealgebra. Strong visual support, integrated review features.
  • Tussy, Alan S., and R. David Gustafson, Prealgebra. Application-rich; the word-problem treatment is unusually thorough.
  • Blitzer, Robert F., Thinking Mathematically / Introductory and Intermediate Algebra. Less canonically a “pre-algebra” book, but widely used at the developmental boundary; strong real-world contextualization.
  • Open-resource options: OpenStax Prealgebra, Lumen Learning Developmental Math. Free, peer-reviewed, instructor-customizable.

Reading the Field as a Whole

A few patterns worth holding onto.

  • Pre-algebra is a bridge, not a destination. The course exists because the gap between arithmetic (concrete, computational, single-step) and algebra (abstract, structural, multi-step) is the place where most adult learners and re-entry students fall through. Closing that gap is the whole job. The course will feel slow at the top and accelerate hard at the bottom; that is by design.
  • The hard part is rarely the arithmetic. The hard part is the abstraction. Letters instead of numbers. Equations instead of computations. Relationships instead of values. Students who already know how to add fractions still struggle here, and they are not wrong to find it hard. They are meeting a different kind of object.
  • The equals sign is the rubicon. Once “=” stops meaning “and the answer is” and starts meaning “is the same as,” the rest of algebra becomes mechanical. Before that shift, no amount of practice fixes the confusion. After that shift, very little practice is needed. The course is, in a hidden way, an education about this one symbol.
  • Estimation is the unsung skill. A student who can estimate well catches their own errors, sniffs out unreasonable answers, and develops number sense. A student who cannot is at the mercy of every calculator typo. This is the part of the course that pays dividends for the rest of life, including for people who never take another math class.
  • Pre-algebra is also useful for the artist, the designer, and the PM. Proportional reasoning underwrites every layout grid, every color-mix ratio, every roadmap estimate. Percent change is the native language of analytics. Unit conversion is the difference between a build that ships and a build that crashes on a unit mismatch. The course is not separate from creative or product work; it is the literacy underneath it.
