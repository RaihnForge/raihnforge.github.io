A theory of organizational management for individuals and organizations — structured around the FORGE cycle, five personal forges, and a state-machine view of projects vs. processes.

The Forge Framework is a theory of organizational management I’ve been developing — an attempt to describe, in plain language, how individuals and organizations actually get work done, and what breaks when they don’t.

It’s methodology-agnostic and built on universal design principles: scalability, maintainability, reliability. Think of it less as a productivity system and more as the infrastructure underneath one.

Core Concepts

  • The FORGE Cycle — Feed → Organize → React → Generate → Express. Five stages, each with a wisdom principle.
  • Personal Forges — five Maslow-mapped domains: HomeForge, HealthForge, UnchosenForge, RaihnForge, PaladinForge.
  • Functional Folders — a state machine for work: Action Log, Queue, Projects, Binder, Archive. Folders represent operational state, not storage.
  • Projects end. Processes are continuous. — the distinction most organizations blur, and pay for later.
  • The Four C’s Gate — Comfort → Confidence → Competence → Compliance. Over-ambition before clearing these stages is a primary failure mode.
  • Miller’s Law (7 ± 2) — a design constraint for departments, categories, and groupings.

Where It Came From

The framework grew out of years of watching small teams and solo operators — including myself — fail in recognizable patterns. Over-ambition without competence. Projects that should have been processes. Folders that were graveyards instead of state machines. The Forge Framework is my attempt to name those patterns and offer a coherent alternative.

It’s still a working draft. That’s the point of sharing it now.
